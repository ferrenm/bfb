Jacket Bio

B. F. Becula’s expectations of society were permanently warped by formative years (0~30) in the 2 most liberal/progressive/civilized communities in the US: Berkeley and Cambridge. A high-school economic dropout, he worked at what was available (ditch digger, truck driver, welder, machinist, aircraft loftsman, machine designer, …). Driven by an abiding curiosity, he looked for answers in seminary, MIT (PhD, physical chemistry), and an academic career in thalassochemistry. The working years (30~60) taught him that science answered How Come? but not Why?, and that theology answered Why? from a wholly inadequate base.

He devoted a third of his thinking years (60~90 …) to Why? before (like many others) reluctantly accepting projection: gods are very like us. His professorial interpretation has them seeking experimental answers to a difficult problem, with us as colleagues/lab-rats – and vanishing when no longer needed.

Descended from hereditary bards of the Lord of the Isles, Becula weaves Gravesian mythopoesis and contemporary science into an optimistic and explanatory theory of religion. Among his sources are the

  • evolutionary biology of Lynn Margulis,
  • primatology of Allison Jolly,
  • human ecology of Garrett Hardin,
  • psychology of Wilhelm Reich,
  • sociology of Herbert Gintis,
  • geopsychology of James DeMeo,
  • bicamerality of Julian Jaynes,
  • Gaian vision of James Lovelock,
  • Enlightenment aspirations of the Founding Fathers,
  • mathematical intuitions from Plato to Tegmark,
  • and a nod to quantum field theory

— most of which were unknown to 200-odd earlier theories he summarizes. If some of his sources seem unlikely, he replies: ‘They had their problems, but also deep insights. The point of higher education is to learn to distinguish wheat from chaff.’
