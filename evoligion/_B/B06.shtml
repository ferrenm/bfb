THEORIES OF RELIGION

  ▽   ▽

Sociologists, psychologists, and philosophers have all sought to analyze and understand ‘Homo religiosus’ and have put forward various hypotheses to explain the strong and often contagious hold that religion has over human individuals and societies. Yet none of these attempts, although providing plausible reasons why religion can help in our daily lives, has so far led to a consistent naturalistic explanation for how religious behavior first developed and became established. — W Schiefenhövel, E Voland (2009)° W Schiefenhövel, E Voland (2009) The Biological Evolution of Religious Mind and Behavior (Springer, Berlin).

△  

Something Missing

  ▽

The revival of independent thought that accompanied the end of the Middle Ages led to critical thinking about religion. As always when there is little understanding, there are many ideas about what religion is. The problem is that of the blind men describing the elephant: one can only describe what he comes in contact with. One or two of the theories anticipate the direction we are going. Most of them ignore the central paradox, the nearly universal pothodoxy that powers religion. Yet belief — while it thrives on ignorance — has lost its hold on many intellectuals, Scandinavians, and the lapsed Anglicans of England, so that religion for many in the West is only a social gesture or voluntary discipline. Why was belief once useful? Why is it now superfluous and often detrimental? Theories which fail to answer these questions are missing something essential.

△  

Three Strands to Plait

  ▽

To begin a search for the missing bit, we put previous work in perspective with a brief review. There are 3 strands to follow. The first is descriptive and examines what the many have done about religion, including the varieties of gods we worship and how we represent them. The second is analytical and examines what philosophers have thought about religion. The third strand (Ch. 7) looks at anomalous features of religion which may guide us toward its real purpose. The first 2 strands suffer from the limitations of history, for writing came to us some 30 millennia later than religion.

△   △  

STRAND 1. VARIETIES OF RELIGION

  ▽   ▽

The traditional classification of religion counts gods. Cultural evolutionist Sir E.B. Tylor thought religion improved by reducing this number: animism progressed to primitive polytheism; polytheism was refined and gave way to monotheism. In contrast, Folklorist Andrew Lang held that foragers tend to be monotheist; farmers and herders, polytheist. Such debates form an important part of theology — yet it is not always clear what is meant by mono- and poly-. We are dealing with metaclassifications, comparing structures which are defined differently in different contexts. When religions do not recognize eachother's supernatural structure, how does one make meaningful comparisons? How do we classify incompatible supernatural beings like Catholic saints and the minor gods of Hinduism? How many gods are there when one is an avatar of another? Is a monotheism with angels and djinns distinguishable from polytheism? How, exactly, does a god with 99 names differ from 99 gods?

△  

Afterlives On Offer

  ▽

If I saw no signs of a divinity, I would fix myself in denial. If I saw everywhere the marks of a Creator, I would repose peacefully in faith. But seeing too much to deny Him, and too little to assure me, I am in a pitiful state, and I would wish a hundred times that if a god sustains nature it would reveal Him without ambiguity. — Pascal.

The classification that counts gods was invented by Christian theologians arguing against the Classical polytheisms. In doing so, they overlooked a possibly more meaningful classification. Its counting system goes ‘Zero, One, Many’, and it counts the number of chances we have to prepare for eternity (or whatever comes next, if anything).

Zero. Maimonides waffled on the question of resurrection, apparently preferring to believe in spiritual immortality as a reward for obtaining correct knowledge of god. Other rabbis disagreed. Perhaps recognizing the undecidability of the question, and accepting a form of Pascal's wager, Judaism is largely unconcerned with the ‘afterlife’. What matters is how well we do with the life we have. A capsule description is: ‘Live as though you were going to die tomorrow. Study as though you were going to live forever.’ Many Nobel Prizes result. Confucianism shares this real-world focus.

One. The other Abrahamic religions are one-shot structures with an other-world focus requiring massive overbelief. Philosophically, they lead to the logical impasses of inquisitions and predestination. Their greatest weaknesses are a direct result of this other worldly orientation: a lack of concern for the future, the physical world, and other species.

Many. Most ‘Eastern’ religions hold that souls evolve by trial and error, and that this is the purpose of the world. If we don't get it right this time, we repeat the lesson in our next life. Logically, this should lead to the same real-world results as the 0-shot approach, but the system is commonly subverted by the idea that one's lifetime status is fixed at birth and cannot be improved until the next cycle. Few Nobel Prizes result.

Purgatory is the Catholic approach to correcting Christianity's one-shot approach. With enough prayers and masses, sinners can reap vicarious atonement and move on to Heaven. The only detectable philosophical justification for this is that it makes the church a profit.

Like many aspects of religion, the reality of the afterlife is an undecidable question. No known physics supports the idea, and no evidence save cultural memories and personal experience of bicameral conversations with the dead. Anyone who has experienced such a ‘contact’ will not be lightly persuaded that it is internal. All that is needed to resolve this question is reception of repeatable, publicly verifiable data. Alas, this has not been forthcoming in 150 years of patient investigation under laboratory conditions. Believers attribute this failing to difficulties experienced by the dead in reaching us.

My personal response to this logical impasse is that if I had designed the universe, I would have tried to include reincarnation. Given the challenge presented in the front end papers, the idea of implacable karmic retribution is an appealing implementation of social justice, if a bit too slow for my taste.

‘Pascal's Wager’ holds that while we cannot decide the truth of the matter, if the 1-shot Abrahamic view is correct, the payoff — the difference between an eternity of heaven or hell — is infinite. Because we cannot know the truth, we cannot evade the payoff by denial. Therefore it behooves the rational person to live well, while trying very hard to believe. What we might call the Buddhist version of Pascal's Wager is that the prudent person will act as though reincarnation were in effect°. Suicide is the 3rd leading cause of death among adolescents, 4 times higher for boys than girls. Two of my male teen-age friends (well behaved, upright Christians, before the days of drugs) committed suicide in the 1940s, when the offical view — promulgated by pundits who had repressed their own adolescence? — was ‘true depression [is] not possible at this development point’. Hmph! My view at the time was that one's outlook was warped by depression, so I should wait until I felt optimistic before killing myself, because the problems would be more severe next time around. This worked.

Animism

  ▽

The line between nature worship and animism is blurred: ‘We know that people have separable parts that can leave the body for adventures at night. Animals also dream, so probably everything, including rocks and rivers, has a spirit body. The world is full of spirits who can both help and harm; the rules for dealing with them are complex and don't always work. It is worth keeping a shaman who understands these things, even if he is an inept hunter and we have to feed him.

We must never kill needlessly, or let food go to waste.’

There is a premonition here of sound environmental ethics, but it was neither universal nor articulated. Totemism

  ▽

While totems are known primarily from non-Western and generally less developed cultures, the Lowenmensch from the Swabian Hohlenstein-Stadel cave (~40ka), is a totemic object from the early days of Europe. In any case, totemism flourished wherever the world divided into relatives and enemies, for it increased the one and decreased the other. A tribe is generally a few kinship groups, and people in your own tribe seldom try to kill you on sight. On the other hand, it is a small gene pool and may lose alleles until a passing virus wipes it out. In the New Guinea Highlands, omnihostile tribes have genetic abnormalities and co-evolutionary diseases not known in their neighbors. Exogamy is genetically desirable, but in the Paleolithic, any meeting with strangers with the chimpanzee mind-set of killing the stranger was risky.

Totemism is a logical solution. It divides a wontok into moieties in such a way that each tribe has members of each totem. Thus one has synthetic kin who can be safely approached in every tribe, even if the tribe as a whole would happily kill you. This facilitates trading, and swapping of women. The laws of magic then mean that you cannot mate with women of your own totem. JG Frazier (1910) Totemism and Exogamy, 4 vols (Macmillan, London). (although Frazier makes it clear that the association between totemism and exogamy was not universal among the studied tribes). I will go out on a limb and suggest that in the day — some tens of thousands of years earlier — the association was stronger, and that its importance declined as population and variety increased. The clever invention was the earliest form of synthetic kinship: ‘The other tribe is not entirely foreign. Some of them share a common ancestor with me, so I can negotiate with them and don't need to kill them.’

The mythic ancestor is often an animal, but may be anything. This, however, is an afterthought:

[T]he belief in this descent is a mere explanatory myth to account for totemism; and, like all other such myths, could only arise after men were not only totemic, but wondered why they were totemic. Consequently such myths are not of the essence of totemism, and their varieties are of no importance. — Andrew Lang° (1911) Method in the Study of Totemism (Robert Maclehose, Glasgow): Ch. 3.

The real function is the prevention of gene loss by inbreeding: the existence of relatives among The Others is sufficient to allow exogamic wife exchange and minimize inbreeding. This increases viability, so any mutation which favors totemism will be reinforced. The often puzzling association between totemism and incest is a logical vextension of tribal incest rules to the totem. The optimum biological rule is that you don't marry within your tribe; marriage within your totem from another tribe confers no genetic disadvantage, but is prohibited for consistency. The topic seems understudied. Martin Wobst° HM Wobst (1974) ‘Boundary conditions for Paleolithic social systems: a simulation approach’ American Antiquity 39(#2): 147-178. made an early computer model of Paleolithic band size under various mating scenarios (monogamy/polygamy, patrilocal/matrilocal, incest/not, various fertilities, sex ratios, &c) but, unfortunately, did not include totemism.

△   Ancestor Worship

  ▽

This nearly universal practice also furnishes clues to the function of religion. Now that we know about bicameral voices, ‘consultation’ is a more accurate description than ‘worship’, for ancestors are asked for advice — and provide it. This preserves the wisdom of previous generations for the guidance of the living. As noted earlier, the practice survived into the mid-20th century in Madagascar.

Polytheism

  ▽

The division of labor in the first urban cultures generated a god for each occupation. These were humans writ large, with specific duties and peaceful coexistence. The variety of gods increases the explanatory power of polytheism, and provides an obvious answer to the problem of evil, since polytheisms mimic the human family. The pantheon is the precursor of the soap opera, displaying human dramas in mythic form that pervades the culture as entertainment and example.

Olympus was an uneasy company of 6 male and 6 female gods. Robert Graves saw this as a compromise between a native matriarchal chthonic religion and the male sky gods of Dorian invaders. If so, score another victory for Greek rationalism. The division of labor within the pantheon — the Romans had over 200 gods, although some, like Cardea, goddess of thresholds and door pivots, had minimal responsibility — makes polytheisms naturally tolerant, often extending this courtesy to foreign gods. In this respect they are a considerable advance over monotheisms.

When we first meet Olympus, Zeus and the boys clearly outrank Hera and the girls, but this was not always so. Zeus (written Δεος, ie, ‘god’) is Dyaus pater is Ju-piter, god-the-father, and like monotheistic gods, often addressed as father of the human race. Yet knowledge of paternity came late to humans, and before Zeus's time the Great Mother was the ancestor.

Thera

  ▽

The early children of Gaia, the earth, are shadowy but kindly. Her later children tend to be destructive monsters. Hesiod tells of a fight between Zeus and Typhœus, described as a towering black giant with blood running down his heel and Zeus's lightning flashing around his head. To an earth scientist, Hesiod reads like an eyewitness account (after 800 years of oral transmission°) California Indians transmitted valid geological information about the eruption of Mt Mazama (Crater Lake) for 7500 years, so there is nothing remarkable about the Mediterranean transmission. Thoreau's Walden Pond was once an ice hill — an insulated remnant of the Laurentide Ice Sheet — surrounded by glacial outwash, until it melted and turned into a ‘bottomless’ pond. Local Indian tradition recalled the event, which might have happened 10,000 years earlier.

△   Henotheism/Kathenotheism/Monolatry   ▽

Max Müller coined the first 2 terms to describe nuances between polytheism and monotheism. Henotheism is a courteous system which worships a single god, while acknowledging the gods worshipped by others. The Olympic pantheon was henotheistic from Zeus's point of view. The other gods existed and could be worshipped, but he could override them.

Monolatry is one step from henotheism, in that the other gods, while still present, are not worthy of worship.

Kathenotheism is serial henotheism. Many Vedic hymns are addressed to Indra, but ‘Indra’ is a title rather than a name (-endra means ‘chief’). Thus Prahlâda was Indra at one time, and Nahusha at another. Just as all US Presidents are announced with ‘Hail to the Chief’, so too could hymns to Indra be used for different gods.

YHVH disregarded Müller's distinctions. He wanted henotheism in Ex 20:3, ‘Thou shalt have no other gods before me’; then monotheism, ‘There is none else besides him’ {Deut 4:35}; monolatry, ‘Ye shall destroy their altars, and break down their images, and cut down their groves, and burn their graven images with fire’ {Deut 7:5}; monotheism, ‘to whom belong the heavens and the earth’ {Deut 10:14}; and again henotheism, ‘He is God of gods, and Lord of lords’ {Deut 32:39}. The Jews eventually settled on monotheism, making the Shema the preferred version: ‘Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD’ {Deut 6:5}.

In the Brihad-aranyaka Upanishad III.9.1 the number of Hindu gods is sequentially reduced from 3003 to 303 to 33 to 6 to 3 to 2 to 1.5 to 1°. The 6 gods are fire, the earth, the air, the sky, the sun and heaven. The 3 gods are ‘the 3 worlds’. The 2 gods are water and vital breath. The 1.5 gods are ‘This air that blows’, with explanation, ‘Concerning this some say: “Since the air blows as one substance, how can it be one and a half (adhyardha)?” The answer is: It is one and a half because by its presence everything attains surpassing glory (adhyardhnot).’ [Superficially, this is a pun. Or perhaps it is a key to a deeper truth.] I trust this clears up the question of fractional gods.

As in Greece, what the populace sees as polytheism is understood by the thoughtful as monotheism. ‘The Gayatri is Brahma is Vishnu is Shiva is the Vedas’ says one text. Gayatri is variously described as an ancient solar mantra, a poetic meter of the Veda, and a 5-headed goddess in her own right. Such complexities tend to reduce academic classifications to mincemeat.

△   Dualism   ▽

The tension between a polarized dyad can explain nearly anything. Such dyads are the raw material of drama and art, and include good/evil, light/dark, idea/embodiment, heaven/earth, atman/brahman, life/death, yin/yang, chaos/order, male/female, love/discord, body/spirit, god/devil, truth/error, sacred/profane, &c. How could religion escape such a convenient concept? Often, the personifications of the dyad are family members: siblings, or parent and offspring. In others they are opposing divinities; or aspects of a single god like Vishnu and Shiva, or a god and sidekick like the Great Spirit and Coyotl. Even Parsiism, originally the most emphatic monotheism, could not long do without God's Enemy, and in later forms Ahura Mazda and Ahriman are almost equal. Satan is absent from the Torah except for the snake in the (interpolated?) Potter's Tale. He is introduced in Job to resolve the problem of evil, but receives only slight mention thereafter, not reappearing until 1 Chronicles 21, where he instigates burocracy. He becomes important in the Epistles, and is a great favorite with evangelical Christians and, of course, Satanists. He is invoked by Muslims to explain why Islam has not spread across the world.

The effective dyads as seen by the Frankfurt ‘critical theory’ include 15 antagonisms: gender, generations, economic class, race, nationality, individual/collective, political right/left, order/progress, revolutionary/authoritarian, state/society, language/work°, Glossed as ‘linguistically mediated interaction of the bourgeoisie vs tool-mediated labor of the proletariat’. In the US vernacular, this would be ‘white-collar/blue-collar’. conscious individuality vs collective unconscious, efficiency/humanity, religious/secular, and man/nature. Even if these conflicts once played a positive and productive role in society, the amount of suffering they produce is so enormous that their continuation is undesirable. Optimistically, the conflicts are expected to end when ‘monotheism is reconciled with radical enlightenment° RJ Siebert (2001) The Critical Theory of Religion: The Frankfurt School (revised 2nd edition) (Scarecrow Press, Lanham MD) (ISBN 0810841401). ’ and new religions resolve these into an interconnected network of diverse positions, a question we return to in the final chapters.

△   Monotheism   ▽

Freud° S Freud (1939) ‘Moses and Monotheism’ in The Origins of Religion, The Sigmund Freud Library 13: 295-386. Ed. by A Richards & A Dickson. (Penguin, London, 1990]. wrote a story in which Akhnaton invented monotheism and taught it to Moses, who took the Jews and ran with it. Akhnaton was the first to see that polytheism was the answer to priestly unemployment; the priests retaliated by chipping his name off every wall in Egypt after he died. But the idea spread, and we now have 3 Abrahamic monotheisms that agree that there is but a single god — adding, ‘But my god is better than your god’ — or, as Asma° ST Asma (2005) The gods drink whiskey (HarperCollins, NYC): 84. put it in missionary format, ‘my god can beat up your god’, which is a sure sign that religion has lost its purpose.

Some think monotheism answered the natural question of polytheism, ‘Who created the gods?’ — but it does this only by postulating a ‘necessary’, and thus uncreated, god. This is a word game. A creator is, from both experience and logic, more complicated than his creations. Monotheism thus explains a complex entity (the universe) as having been created by a more complex uncreated entity (a god) — but this only makes the problem more difficult. Why not accept the simpler entity as uncreated and ‘necessary’? In any case, monotheism, or monarchianism (belief in a single divine ruler), became an official Christian heresy after the formulation of the doctrine of the Trinity at Nicea in 325 (although ¾ of the attending bishops walked out over the decision°). Islam appears to be a direct descendant of one or more of these suddenly heretical monotheist churches. The details are in Chapter 15. Unitarianism (strict monotheism) was outlawed in Britain from ~1650 until ~1925.

Xenophanes observed that the gods of horses would look like horses. On the other hand, we ourselves have been called the lesser gods of animals. These are not necessarily incompatible views. Lumpkin, a grey tabby rescue cat, appreciated that I could answer prayers, perform miracles, dispense boons, open the fridge, and otherwise fill the role of a lesser god. She also saw me as a poorly trained kitten. Turning up her nose when I offered a bit of my (vegetarian) dinner, she left, to return in 5 minutes with a crippled mouse, which she deposited at my feet. Her body language was unmistakable: ‘Get down here and learn to catch food. Now!’ If a cat brain can hold wildly conflicting views on the nature of godhood, is it any wonder that ours do also°? I later sold Lumpkin for $10,000. I figured the house would go for $105,000, but the buyer offered $115,000 ‘if I'd throw in the cat’. Since the buyer was a woman, and Lumpkin had long since made clear her preference (to me, and evidently to the buyer) for a carnivorous woman as her person, this made everyone happy.

△   Theism   ▽

Theism describes a personal god who oversees his handiwork and tinkers with it, sometimes on demand. Theism depends upon revelation, for many theologians conclude that all we can know of god is what he reveals. This mind-set generates 2 of the most troubling questions of theology: the problem of evil, and that of the ‘intimate other’:

  • How can an omnibenevolent monotheist god permit natural evil (earthquakes, illness)?
  • How can Rudolf Otto's ‘wholly other’ be known at all; and how can it be encountered as a person?

The latter dilemma results from not understanding bicamerality, in which the ‘sensed entity’ of the right hemisphere is perceived as external, yet it is intimate, personal, and — because it knows the innermost secrets of the left-hemisphere persona — simultaneously projects immanence, omnipotence and omniscience. Once this perception is established by bicameral experience it is perpetuated by dogma, faith, and pothodoxy as bicamerality fades.

△   Pantheism, Panentheism   ▽

Pantheism, meaning ‘Everything is god’ was coined in 1705 to describe religions which see the universe as identical to, or the only manifestation of, an immanent god. In the West, the concept was popularized by Zeno and the Stoics. ‘No part can be sentient where the whole is not sentient; parts of the universe are sentient, therefore the universe is sentient’ — hence the universe must be animate, rational, wise, and divine. (Did Zeno never cut his hair?) Pantheism comes in many flavors, which we need not elaborate.

Pantheists hold that the Universe itself possesses the attributes of divinity, such as power, magnificence, and grandeur, and so regard it as sacred. It is all that is, ever was, or ever will be. Contact with the sacred is sought in the natural world, through the arts and sciences, ethical action, and planetary stewardship (rather than revealed doctrine, saviors, or holy writ), and thus is better suited to today's needs than any of the monotheisms.

Panentheism, meaning ‘Everything is in god’ was coined in 1828 to describe a variant of pantheism in which the finite world is only a portion of the infinite god.

△   Deism   ▽

Conscious, rational, cortical thinkers unimpressed by dogma often hold that reason rather than revelation is the proper approach to religion. This is Deism, which preserves the essence of the Torah (while discarding its antiquated legalisms), and the essence of Jesus's teachings (while discarding bicameral Pauline and Johanine mythopoesis). Our first 4 presidents — among them, the writers of the Constitution — were Deists. To this fact we owe the 200-year success of Constitutional government and the early image of America as the land of freedom and opportunity.

Today Deism is better known for the reaction it inspired, epitomized by the Chicago Statements°, Two formal position papers from the International Council on Biblical Inerrancy, which met in Chicago (1978-10-26–28 and 1982-11-10–13). The organizing Coalition on Revival has issued a comprehensive series of papers on ‘biblical principles for all spheres of human life including theology, evangelism, discipleship, law, civil governments, economics, education, family, medicine, psychology and counseling, arts and media, business and professions, and science and technology’: In short, a theocracy as comprehensively stifling as that of the Dark Ages or Islam. The fundamentalist movement has completely failed to understand the Enlightenment roots of the Deist founders of the US government. which want to revert to a US Golden Age of Pauline theocracy that never was. ‘We believe America can be turned around and once again function as a Christian nation as it did in its earlier years’°. The Coalition on Revival (1978) The Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy (Dallas Theological Seminary Archives, TX): 4. The Statements are part of a series of attempts to undo the Enlightenment and return, as Nobel Economics Laureate Robert Fogel° RW Fogel (2000) The Fourth Great Awakening & the Future of Egalitarianism (U. Chicago) put it, ‘to sensuous [limbic] religion’. Known collectively as the ‘Great Awakenings’ by fundamentalists, they are largely responsible for the proliferation of the 2000 Christian sects in the country.

△   Agnosticism   ▽

The idea that the god conjecture is undecidable goes back to the scepticism of the Axial Age of Classical Greece. Yet ‘agnostic’ was not coined until 1869, by T.H. Huxley, to describe his ignorance of the very things that Gnostics were so sure of. Rejecting revelation as a source of information means that we cannot know very much about the god of the Deists. At the bottom of every deep scientific probe, the real world dissolves into abstractions which are difficult to distinguish from the descriptions offered by an Eastern mystic. The known, mathematically describable hither shore of the gulf separating physical from spiritual is already incomprehensible to common sense. Since whatever gods may be necessarily reside on the far side of this gulf, there is little that can be said about them.

Agnosticism is not for the faint of heart, and most people seem to prefer familiar tales to the admission of total ignorance. There is, however, one thing that we have leaned about god since Huxley's day, and that is that whatever a theist tinkering god might be able to accomplish psychologically, there is no evidence of an ability to do anything physically. Our instruments are sufficiently precise, and our records good enough, so that there is no room for anomaly or departure from the deduced laws of nature during the last century. No miracles, unless they occur by manipulation of the laws of chance in a zero-sum situation — perhaps a partisan god depriving one person of something prayed for by another. Do we want that sort of god?

△   Ethnological Definitions of Religion   ▽

As long as there is poverty, there will be gods. — Durant, Mansions of Philosophy, 568.

The Human Reference Area Files at Yale are our most accessible source of nearly 200 years of anthropological and ethnographic data, organized, encoded, and prepared for statistical use (by removal of dependencies) by the communities associated with the Journal of Ethnography and World Cultures.

This material is essentially all we will ever know about the cultures involved, for as Heraclitus might have said, ‘You can't study the same culture twice’. One way to use the Files is to select 2 characteristics and asks the computer to report the number of cultures that share the characteristics, and the statistical significance of this result. Textor° RB Textor (1967) A Cross-Cultural Sample (HRAF Press, New Haven CT). did this pairwise for 400 cultures chosen to be representative of the world, and 536 characteristics (hence the 3000 pages of his Standard Cross-Cultural Survey, mainly computer print out). He discarded all pairs that were not statistically significant, and gave numerical indications of the strength of the correlation. One might expect that this would simplify the verification of ethnological hypotheses, but this is not always the case. One often needs to examine groups of characteristics, selected to represent some higher-level construct that one is interested in.

An example may help. Table 6.2 includes everything coded about ‘religion’. Consider line FC ‘Finished Characteristic’ 426: ‘High god is present’. The codes are:

  • A ‘A high god present but otiose or not concerned with human affairs’, 69 cultures;
  • B ‘A high god present and active in human affairs but not offering positive support to human morality’, 26 cultures;
  • C ‘A high god present, active, and specifically supportive of human morality’, 61 cultures. A, B, and C are combined into the 156 of Column Yes;
  • O ‘A high god absent or not reported in substantial descriptions of religious beliefs’, 104 cultures in Column No.
  • Column ? is ‘No Data’, with 140 cultures.
  • Column X is ‘Irrelevant’.

This leaves 140 cultures unrepresented for FC 428, either because informants never mentioned their gods, or because the right questions were not asked. The implicit statistical assumption is that these cultures would divide in the same ratio as the ones that provided the Yes and No answers. Among ‘hard’ scientists, such data would be considered suspect. The meaning of the ‘Yes’ and ‘No’ columns is not under control of the analyst. He cannot refine the questions, nor change the coding. (He does have the option of coding new characteristics by reading the literature, but this is a daunting challenge.) Nevertheless, this is what we have to work with. The years of intense intellectual effort involved in reading, evaluating, and encoding the original reports means that those involved may tend to overestimate the statistical reliability of conclusions drawn from the data. The natural response is to take conclusions that are contrary to one's predilections with a grain of salt — while enthusiastically accepting the ones you like. Caveat lector!

Perhaps more important is FC 428, gods with an interest in morality, of which there are only 61/400, or 15%. This casts considerable doubt on the commonly voiced idea that ‘all cultures have [useful] religions’.

△   The Paleofunctional Theory   ▽

We can avoid many of Guthrie's difficulties in formulating a theory of religion if we limit ourselves to considering what religion was originally, rather than what it has become. Just as Paul prided himself on being all things to all men, he — and a thousand others — tailored their sales pitch to their congregations. The results today are bound to be a bit various and difficult to classify. Consequently, as mentioned in the Preface, I prefer a ‘paleofunctional’ hypothesis that gets to the heart of the matter:

Religion is the synthetic kinship, invented bicamerally, that got us out of the Middle Paleolithic and out of the animal kingdom, and turned us into time-binding humans.

’Bicameral’ reminds us that religion stems from preconscious days. When our gods spoke to us directly from the right hemisphere, there was no doubt of their reality and authority, and we obeyed them willy-nilly. ‘Synthetic kinship’ reminds us that the purpose of religion was to keep us from killing eachother, using the only argument that we were capable of understanding at the time. ‘Middle Paleolithic’ recalls the 160,000 years of rather pointless existence before we thought of this idea, when our only way of dealing with strangers was to kill them. The definition in the Preface added a clause about events in the Neolithic, which we will take up in its proper place, when we found that there was a price to pay for leaving the Middle Paleolithic.

Appendix 6a epitomizes ca 200 of the more significant theories of religion. Many are opinions maintained before anyone thought religion needed a theoretical basis, but sometimes a dogma says a great deal about the unvoiced theory behind it. Increasing awareness that religion needs explaining has resulted in a steady stream of recent books.

19th-century theorists gave sweeping answers to large questions, going well beyond what their data would support. To these authors, ‘evolutionary theology’ meant the upward sequence from animism to totemism to classical religions to the perfection of Christianity.

Critics attacked the method of reaching conclusions by comparing unlike cultures. Later workers, following Evans-Pritchard, studied a single culture in intimate detail to describe how its religion worked. We were left with an unsatisfactory polarization: enthusiastic generalizations drawn with a broad brush on the one hand; detailed sociology that may not apply to other cultures on the other.

  7. What viability advantage did religion bring?

We will try to answer these over the next 6 chapters.

No prior approach asks all of these questions. Believers accept gods as explicantia rather than as explicanda; anthropologists and sociologists discuss the functions that religion serves in particular societies; theologians and psychologists do not mention viability. Few address the sheer necessity of belief.

A number of theories of religion — Marx's class opiate, Weber's theodicy, Parson's social order — assume far more infrastructure than was available early on. Religion as we know it, created by farmers and herders, is a development of the last 0.25% of the human experience; religion studied in Neolithic tribes represents at most 2% of this period. These samples bear little relation to the religion that originated in the Eurasian Paleolithic, which has to be something that made sense to — and changed the behavior of — talking chimpanzees.

A structured view of religious theory suggests a layered approach. The bottom layer is the more or less universal animism that populates the earth with invisible beings, probably continuous with the limbic response of a horse spooked by a blowing newspaper. Bicamerality gave voices to this layer, and some measure of unconscious control and direction, of which Hamurabi took full advantage. The third layer was the poetically constructed theisms, poly- and mono-, that took us up to the Axial Age. Then Gautama and others made it clear that the earlier stages had been crutches, helpful before we developed our own ethical sense, but obstructive and distracting when our society had healthy bones of rational understanding.

Reviewer Daniel Yee° D Yee (1997) Review of Stewart Guthrie's Faces In The Clouds http://dannyreviews.com/. voiced a different sort of complaint:

There has been relatively little new work on theory of religion in recent decades: there seems to have been a general acceptance that the subject is too hard, that the existing theoretical morass is undrainable.

Perhaps this defeatist conclusion is the result of not looking far enough into the past, and so omitting the essential discipline of evolutionary biology from theories of religion.

There is one more aspect that prior theories of religion have omitted. In productive disciplines, theory and practice interact with constant feedback. Experiment suggests revisions to theory; theory suggests novel experiments. The concept of experimental religion is anathema to religious leaders (with the notable exception of the Dalai Lama), apparently so threatening to their world view that it is dismissed as impious, impossible to do and irrelevant when performed. Consequently feedback is still absent from religious studies, which in turn denies its results to theorists of religion. While religion has continued on its traditional path, one of its existential questions has been answered by other disciplines entirely. This brings us to the theory intimated in the first end paper, and described more fully below.

In the whole historical evolution of mankind there are only 2 general world-views to be distinguished: the ancient Babylonian and the modern empiro-scientific. — Hugo Winckler: historian, linguist, Orientalist

A common shared aspect of religions is the ‘supernatural’, describing the something additional to, and beyond the limitations of, the natural, quotidian world described by reductionism. It has been the common belief of humanity, in the 35,000 years since we invented religion, that there is something ‘out there’ that differs in essence from commonplace things like caves, cattle, flints and fire.

A more prosaic approach finds its challenge in the commonplace world itself. Beginning with the Aegean attempts to find principles rather than principals controlling the world, and continuing with the astronomers, Newton, and the scientific method, it eventually led to the Standard Model of Fig 6.2. This has, indeed, uncovered phenomena different in essence from the commonplace things we understand. That the phenomena are not quite what supernaturalists were expecting is a typical result of exploration. This approach — with equivalent input from many other disciplines — has satisfactorily answered the primary existential question: ‘Where did we come from?’. It is relevant to our theme that the discovery of the Standard Model was informed by symmetry theorems of mathematical physics.

The answer shows that we had been looking in the wrong place, misled by experiences that made particular areas of research easy of access (but unproductive of results). This is the logical fallacy of lux facilis, searching for lost car keys beneath the streetlight. Everyday logic took us down to the concept of atoms, one level above the standard model. Moving beyond that had to wait for the Renaissance, electricity, vacuums, and high technology.

The simplicity of the grid of Fig. 6.2 is deceptive. The quarks, u up and d down, c charmed and s strange, t top and b bottom), are held together in pairs (mesons) or triples (baryons: neutron udd and proton uud) by gluons g, of which there are 12 varieties. Only the light generation form stable objects: the others fleetingly inhabit high-energy regimes. The ancient alchemists anticipated the day when we will manipulate gluons as easily as we now manipulate photons and electrons.

Note that electrical charge (upper right corner of the boxes) is orderly, while the mass (bottom) seem arbitrary. The completed model is expected to explain the masses in an elegant manner. Under the tiles labelled ‘e’ and ‘γ’ lies everything we know about electric motors and generators, magnets, optics, computers, radar, gamma rays, X-rays, spectroscopy, radiant heating, antenna theory, neon lights, LEDs, Josephson junctions, &c, &c. Admittedly, these are the most familiar and accessible tiles. Their wealth of practical applications are the result of 2 centuries of hard work.

We know less about the other tiles, although they represent the life work of a covey of little-known geniuses. The bailiwick of the other bosons is the atomic nucleus, which we do not yet know how to manipulate (except in the crudest manner), so we don't know what can be done with them, but one wonders about the possibilities of elemental transmutation, nuclear fusion, and similar alchemical dreams.

The Standard Model's neatness is deceptive in another way. The Higgs boson (missing from Fig. 6.2) has been found, and is expected to explain the peculiar masses of the fermions. Still undetected is the graviton, which might explain how gravity works. The Model says nothing about ‘dark energy’, a 5th force that drives the accelerating expansion of the universe, nor about ‘dark matter’, thought to be needed to keep the universe ‘flat’ and explain rotational aspects of galaxies (although MOND, the MOdified Newtonian Dynamics° MOND adds small corrections to Newton's f=ma on the scale of galaxies. For 25 years, the incomplete Standard Model has not failed (nor has any indication of the anticipated supersymmetric version yet appeared). What we currently understand takes us back to the origin of the universe, and step by step organizes it into its current form, without requiring anything more. Establish the rules and initial conditions, and all else follows, much as the analysts of the Enlightenment imagined would happen. We do not get the sterile clockwork universe in which everything is preordained that made early Deism unrealistic, but one filled with randomness, turbulence, friction, chaos, and — surprisingly, rules which use these very qualities to generate startling emergences. The rules turn out to be imbued with subtlety, so that simple beginnings lead to complex results — but always in a natural way, understandable after the event, although not predictable before.

△   Feedback   ▽

The incomplete answers we have found should be fed back to improve the practice of religion. (Gautama, Zoroaster, Augustine, and the Dalai Lama all advocated this approach, so it is not exactly a new idea.) If it is not clear how to go about this, it is because lesser religious leaders have spent 2500 years denying the need, sure that an omnipotent god is benevolent and needs no help. It seems to me that this is a place where ‘religious studies’ have an opportunity to become relevant. Religion itself may be committed to maintaining tradition, but the philosophical study of religion is able to discuss the failings of this approach. One place to begin (for instance) is by disabusing the current ‘elites’ of the notions which support their fantasies. They are not the most important part of the universe, nor is some god rewarding them for their virtue. Some hold their position by tyranny, some by virtue of unethical laws which support their illusions. We do not matter at all to the universe until we produce and maintain an ethical civilization. We begin by changing the laws of our society.

Is that too abstract a description? Here is one simple, practical, and eminently fair example: Let us examine the distribution of income in our society, and carve it into quintiles. The mean of the lowest quintile — the average income of the poorest-paid 20% of the population — we denote Q ($11,240 in 2011). Now, if we are serious about the Constitution's purpose of ‘promoting the general Welfare’, we link the pay of our lawmakers to their performance, by making it some small multiple of Q. I might suggest 6Q for Congressional representatives, with similar grades throughout government (and a Utilitarian death penalty Or 10 years in prison, to appease the liberals. for taking or offering bribes in the form of campaign contributions from corporations) — and suddenly every burocrat and politician in the country is no longer jockeying for the support of corporations, but focussed upon the Constitution's goal of promoting the general welfare in the most personal and concrete way°. A necessary corollary is a legal recognition of the fact that excessive managerial pay is stealing from both stockholders and employees.

△   Interesting Questions   ▲

The Standard Model scarcely begins to answer the things we wonder about. Looking toward the past, we raise the intellectual questions: Why these ‘laws and conditions’? What is in the textbook used by the student goddesses? What results are produced by other sets of ‘laws and conditions’? Why does even the relatively straightforward Fig. 6.2 conceal immense and subtle mathematical sophistication?

Looking toward the future, we find even more important questions: Why is it so difficult for self-conscious intelligences to produce an ethical society? (Many small-scale societies do better than the New Deal, which seems to have been one of our better large-scale approaches.) Why, when we show signs of approaching one, do we so willingly cooperate with those who want to destroy it?

One answer seems to be a nearly universal ‘Expletive! I'm alright, Jack!’ response. This is characteristic of those recently elevated (by social action) out of the often desperate straits of the lower class, when they discover that they are now expected to pay taxes to help lift others. This is exacerbated in the US by the Horatio Alger/Ayn Rand mythos that attributes all progress — whether social or personal — to the hard-working individual, while overlooking the social infrastructure that has made any progress possible.
