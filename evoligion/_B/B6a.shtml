  ▽

“The beginning of the history of religion was [with bicamerality as the prehistoric religion of listening,] with the Chinese religion of measure, with Hinduism as the religion of imagination, with Buddhism as the religion of inwardness, with Zoroastrianism as the religion of light and darkness, or good and evil, with the Syrian religion of pain°, This phrase apparently comes from Hegel's Lectures on the Philosophy of Religion, which is an omnium gatherum of student notes from the lectures, and it is not clear to me that anyone understands what he was referring to here. with the Egyptian religion of riddle, with the Jewish […] religion of sublimity, with the Greek religion of beauty and fate, with the Roman religion of utility, with the Christian religion of freedom,” and ending with the Arab religion of absolute force. — where the direct quote is taken from Rudolf J. Siebert (2001)°‘The Critical Theory of Religion’, a web essay, many of whose paragraphs appear in later work by Siebert, but which can no longer be found on the web. and the closing example is a paraphrase of Zwemer (1905)°. SM Zwemer (1905) The Moslem Doctrine of God (American Tract Society, Boston).

It is a formidable matter to make oneself master of single systems, how much more so is it to make oneself master of all, as must be done by those who look forward to speaking, with a view to the discovery of truth, both for all philosophers, and also against all philosophers. — Cicero De Natura Deorum i.V

Anyone proposing a new theory of religion, when there is already a surfeit, must first make sure that he is not merely repeating what has gone before. As Cicero noted — at a time when there were rather fewer systems — it is no easy task to make oneself master of them. I cannot claim to have done this, and you should not take what I have said as an indication of what others might find in the same material. I have avoided most Christian infighting as mysterious and irrelevant: perhaps Wikipedia can help here. If I have totally misunderstood someone, let me know, and I will try to fix things.

Many of the earlier theories are not deeply concerned with the aspects that most excersize me. A couple of near misses, August Comte's insight about the family and the utility of religion in creating larger associations, and James Cobb's Is it too late?: a theology of ecology show that I am not wholly eccentric.

Nevertheless, the attempt to epitomize a philosopher's work in 3 lines is doomed from the outset. Sometimes I have quoted, or paraphrased, what the author actually wrote; sometimes I have conjoined sentences written 40 years apart (as for Chardin); often I have grasped wildly at whatever straws of thought I imagined that I understood. The dates may give some sense of the changing views of religion, but the aspect that shines most clearly through a comparison such as this is that the analysis tell us as much about the analyst as about the subject.

This table is meant for quick reference, as a reminder of who said what, and when. None of the theories is wrong, for religion has been many things to many people. The question is, Which is most useful today? Might I recommend Akhnaton's?

The Pre-Socratics

  ▽

The Ionian philosophers were part of the Axial Age, reacting against the animistic anthropomorphs of Olympus described by bicamerals Homer and Hesiod. More details can be found in Jaeger°W Jaeger (1947) The Theology of the Early Greek Philosophers (Oxford). . It is unfortunate that so little survives of their work, because this was the first conscious, non-bicameral culture of the West, exploring the possibilities of a new operating system. Francis Galton°, F Galton (1869) Hereditary Genius (Macmillan, London). one of the founders of statistics, estimated (on the basis of geniuses/population) that they were the world's most intelligent race — with lowland Scots coming second.

Judging from their statues, the Ionians were tall, blond, and Morton-toed (2nd-toe longest). If they survived the intra-hellene wars and the Roman Conquest, they seem to have been eradicated by the 7th-century Slavic invasion. The Miracles of St Demetrios mentions otherwise little-known South Slavic tribes: ‘Drogobites, Sagudates, Belegezites, Baiounetes, and Berzetes’ — perhaps collectively, Sclavonians, and evidently short, dark-haired, 1st-toe longest, (with no interest whatsoever in philosophy or any of the other accomplishments of the Classical Greeks°). This population replacement has political repercussions to this day, in, e.g., the demand for the return of the Parthenon Marbles, rescued by Thomas Bruce, 7th Earl of Elgin, ca. 1798. When neither the British nor Ottoman governments, nor the inhabitants of Athens, were interested in keeping the statues out of the lime kiln, Lord Elgin salvaged them and shipped them to England at his own expense. In any case, the present occupants of Greece have little but mitochondria in common with the people of the Parthenon. They apparently killed the men and kept the women, since the mother-tongue survived as demotic Greek. See Historum for further confusion.

Andrew Lang° (1913) Myth, Ritual, & Religion 2 vols. (Senate, NYC). considers the problem of debased myths about exalted gods — always an embarrassment to believers — which was first questioned by the thinkers in this section. He reviews the early explanations (including a score I have missed), and then — from the point of view of the comparative anthropologist — settles for ‘the wild [irrational] element of myth is a survival from the savage state’. This is just the backward projection of the gradual purification of religion described John Dewey. Still, I find it difficult to see much progress since the Axial Age.

Axial Age

The number of remembered Western Axial thinkers is larger than for other regions. Part is because we remember our own. Part is taphonomy and the ravages of time. Part is deliberate destruction; too many have actively destroyed what they could not understand: the milieus of Zoroaster and Buddha have suffered much from this. Part is authorial ignorance: I am far out of my depth here.
