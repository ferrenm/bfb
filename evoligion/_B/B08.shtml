△   Why the Debate?   ▽

The question of the existence of god is undecidable, or it would have been convincingly decided by now. However, some things can be said with certainty. Among them: if there be gods, they and their behavior are unlike the descriptions by prophets. As I may have mentioned, my suggestion is that gods are more like equations than anything else in human experience, for equations have the godlike qualities of omnipotence°, Predictably, a Muslim reader objected: ‘Only Allah is omnipotent. Equations have limited applications.’. Until we discover the Theory of Everything, our equations are more like a pantheon. Maxwell's Equations, describing the behavior of the electromagnetic (EM) field, work throughout the universe, and cannot be circumvented. If the EM field is zero, the equations still apply. The solution to a quadratic (by all accounts a lesser equation than Maxwell's) may have less to do, but it works everywhere, it knows all there is to know about its job, and it always provides the correct answers. omniscience, and omnipresence, and in the manner of the deist gods, they are simultaneously immensely helpful and unaware of our existence.

Let us frame a simple proposition, supported equally by psychology and observation:

When society needs gods, they are present and obvious;
when society no longer needs gods, they vanish.

When we were bicameral, gods were conspicuous. With the development of consciousness c. 1200 BCE, gods began to depart, having served their purpose. The problem was, society might no longer need gods, but thanks to Neolithic pothodoxy, most people still did, and like kittens being weaned, we did not take this loss of support gracefully. Theist gods claim consistency: those of Sumer had served all Mesopotamia with only a change of name; ‘For I am YHVH, I change not’ {Malachi 3:6}; and ‘the same yesterday, and today, and forever’ {Heb 13:8}. The disappearance of the gods then meant either theism's consistency claim was wrong, or gods lie to us, or the god conjecture itself was wrong. Pothodoxy made us keep the god conjecture and explain away inconsistency. We are still doing this 3 millennia later. This seems excessive, so it is fair to ask, why is it so difficult to take advantage of an adult operating system, and move on?

Despite Jaynes's and Paul's pragmatic explanations, arguments about religion have historically made much of a priori and a posteriori knowledge. A posteriori (human, rational) knowledge arises from experience, and is never absolute, because it can always be increased by future experience. A priori (divine, revealed) knowledge is an axiomatic basis that we assume, and thus ‘logically prior to experience’, without which no progress can be made. (Customary examples are the rules of logic or Euclid's postulates.) Priesthoods insist that knowledge of god can only be had from their particular a priori revelations.

The rebuttal is the jewel of the Enlightenment: Revealed religions are arbitrary. This idea immediately puts one at odds with 90% of the world (who nevertheless can see this clearly of religions not their own). It is seldom worth arguing with them, for until they are ready, debate merely reinforces their position. When they are ready, they will think of it themselves. As an example of arbitrary ‘revelation’, the Excluded Middle (x cannot be both A and not-A) is axiomatic in Aristotelian logic, where it is understood as a posteriori, firmly based in experience. Nevertheless, it was pragmatically rejected in India by Samkara (c. 800 CE) in aid of brahmanic creation of a priori mythology to control newly assimilated tribal cultures, with the unfortunate result of leaving an ‘ability to swallow logical contradictions wholesale [… in] the Indian national character’°. DD Kosambi (1965) The Culture and Civilisation of Ancient India in Historical Outline (Routledge & Kegan Paul, London): 7.1.

Theism claims that the only thing about god that can be established by human reason is that god exists. My problem in presenting the medieval arguments for the existence of god is that I find the axioms and postulates of theistic reasoning incompatible with logic. I will do the best I can, but I give fair warning that I cannot read, with any comprehension, Leibniz's° GW Leibniz (1710) Essais de Théodicée sur la bonté de Dieu, la liberté de l‘homme et l‘origine du mal (Isaac Troye, Amsterdam). Theodicy, which in clear and luminous language defends the theistic arguments of the early 18th century. I cannot evaluate the consistency of his response to Pierre Bayle (whose propositions he sets out fully and fairly). This an intellectual failing, because as Cicero explained, a philosopher should be able to defend both sides of an argument persuasively before he tries to explain it to anyone. It is a level of abstraction too far to say ‘If I believed A, and L (who does) asserts that B and C imply D, would I agree with L?’ The implications of believing A on B require separate and detailed consideration, as do the implication of believing A on C. Next we must consider the interactions of the revised view of B and C — and where was I? We don't have room for the details, and it is just a whole lot easier to say ‘Not-A, therefore not-L’. With that cautionary introduction, we look at some of the more interesting approaches.

△   Gaps   ▽

In The Guide for the Perplexed (c. 1150 CE), Maimonides set forth 25 ‘propositions which are employed in the proof for the existence of God’. Some are purely metaphysical (undecidable), suitable only for word games. Some are influenced by the curious motion of planets, resolved by Copernican heliocentricity and Newtonian physics. Some are confused approaches to mechanics, General Relativity, infinite set theory, the invisible fields of physics, and other problems which have been answered by science. In other words, nearly everything that was not understood was attributed to god. This is still the case: god lives in the ever narrowing gaps of Fig. 8.1. Leibniz pointed out that the ‘god of the gaps’ was only a gap in our understanding.

As our understanding of the cosmos expands, its population of sentient life expands accordingly. Cosmos 4 (Fig. 8.3) expands the number of ‘souls’ by a factor of 200,000 if 1 star in a million in our galaxy has an inhabited planet. Cosmos 5 increases ‘souls’ by another factor of 100 billion.

Kant° I Kant (1755) A General Theory of the Heavens; or, Essay on the Mechanical Origin of the Structure of the Universe, on the Principles of Newton (University of Michigan Press, Ann Arbor, 1969). described the mechanics of Cosmos 4 in detail in 1755 (anticipating Laplace's Nebular Theory by 40 years). He took the scale of this cosmos, and the regular working of the laws of nature, as proof of the existence of god° ‘… there is a God precisely because Nature itself, even in chaos, cannot proceed except in an orderly and regular manner.’ , but this is over-interpretation: What Kant showed was that the equations worked.

When it comes to the ultimate size of the universe, Lucretius got it exactly right in 60 BCE:

Since you must admit that there is nothing outside the universe, it can have no limit and is accordingly without end or measure. It makes no odds in which part of it you may take your stand; whatever spot anyone may occupy, the universe stretches away from him just the same in all directions without limit.

We don't see this from earth, but instead see the inside of the ever expanding sphere of the Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB), ‘visible’ only to radio telescopes. Others outside our range of vision see a similar sphere of CMB, as in Fig. 8.4. Cosmos 6 — our current understanding — is infinite in size.

Three of the important parameters of our universe are mass density, curvature, and expansion rate, represented by the 3 Ωs of Fig. 8.4. Recent cosmological observations have put constraints on all 3, as indicated by the colored areas of the triangular graph. ‘CMB’ is a constraint from the Cosmic Microwave Background; ‘SN’ from the distance to supernovas, and ‘clusters’ from the dynamics of galactic clusters. It appears that our universe has ordinary flat (Euclidean) geometry; that it does not contain enough mass to stop expanding and recollapse; and that its rate of expansion is increasing, driven by ‘dark energy’, a sort of negative gravity, not yet understood.

Cosmos 6 is the ‘ΛCDM concordance’ universe, where Λ (lambda) is Einstein's cosmological constant and CDM is Cold Dark Matter. This has become the Standard Cosmological Model, with 6 parameters: the cosmological constant, the cold dark matter density, the baryon density, and 3 other more obscure values.

Every person sees a different rainbow, because rainbows lie on a cone whose axis passes through the sun and the viewer's head. Every observer sees a different spherical universe with himself at the center. The white circle in Fig. 8.4, with the Earth at its center, is the farthest distance we can see, limited by the time since the Big Bang and the expansion of the universe. At the outer edge all we see is the CMB of photons which first escaped from the Big Bang 300,000 years after it banged. We continue to see them because older, cooler photons keep arriving as the visible edge expands.

Point A in Fig. 8.4 is the same age as we are, containing 13.7-billion-year-old galaxies and 4.6-billion-year-old planets with astronomers on them — but we don't see it that way. What we see at A might be small galaxies forming gravitationally bound clusters and colliding to create large galaxies like our own Milky Way. Similarly, astronomers at A see what was happening here 7 billion years ago. ‘Now’ becomes a very slippery concept at extra-galactic distances. A viewer at A sees a spherical Cosmos 5 just as we do, but in the blue crescent, he sees galaxies whose formation we will watch much later, while the white crescent is hidden behind his CMB. Observers at B and C see a CMB surface statistically like the one we see, but they cannot see us, just as we cannot see them.

There is only a finite number of ways of arranging a finite number of particles — but Cosmos 6 in infinite in extent. We have seen above something of the complexity inside any one of the white circles of Fig. 8.4, and the Wikipedia page Observable Universe will provide an entry to filling out the details inside the Hubble volume. Now we want to think about what is outside that volume.

The number of nucleons (protons plus neutrons) in our Hubble volume is about NH = 1080. The number of different ways of arranging NH particles in a Hubble volume VH is large, even very large, but still finite, but only a small fraction will be stable, functioning systems. Among these will be arrangements that include exact copies of you (the difference lying elsewhere in the universe), so that even in our own patch you have many duplicates. In fact, there is a continuum of arrangements that slowly morph you into every other person on the planet. Some of these twins and near twins will have made the choices that you did not.

Max Tegmark° M Tegmark (2003) Parallel Universes. Scientific American 288(#5): 31-41. points out that there are other patches outside of ours — quite far away, perhaps — that duplicate the particle arrangements in ours. In short, there are an infinite number of ‘yous’, many of whom have life histories that parallel yours up to a particular branch point, where they made an alternate choice. The ensemble ‘you’ has tried, and lived, all possible lives available to it.

This has odd philosophical consequences. Whatever difficult character-building ethical choices you may have made, whatever selfless sacrifices for the benefit of others, whatever altruistic services you may have performed, were not made by one or another of your doppelgangers. Do your spiritual rewards for good behavior outweigh their materialistic rewards for selfishness? Is social justice at work for the ensemble, if not for individuals?
