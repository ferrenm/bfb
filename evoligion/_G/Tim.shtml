When Things May Have Happened

Perhaps there was indeed a great invasion, with battle after battle between tens of thousands of opposing soldiers over the course of several years (629 to 636 CE). But if there were, it would seem that at the time, nobody noticed. — Y Nevo, J Koren (2003),Crossroads to Islam (Prometheus Books, Amherst NY): 135 commenting on the Traditional Account of the Arab Conquest of Syria.

I have included my own perhaps controversial hypotheses, (and a few from others). They are listed without comment, just as though they were established facts, in order to show how they fit into the established schema. If you mention these in public, be prepared to be challenged!

Items in red are from Nevo & Koren's highly non-traditional interpretation of the foundation of the Arab state, based on non-Arab documents, numismatics, and inscriptions from the Negev, the only place they can be studied today. Their thesis is that Byzantium deliberately downsized the Empire as Saharasian desiccation made areas unproductive and untaxable, gradually withdrawing to Antioch, subsidizing Arab tribes to replace border garrisons, fostering heretical churches to replace Chalcedonian (Byzantine) supervision, and encouraging self-rule in the provinces, all of which left a power vacuum into which Arab tribes moved and squabbled, eventually being subdued by Mu’awiyah, the first ‘Arab king’ mentioned in non-Arab sources {Ch 4}. Although this thesis infuriates Muslims, it is consistent with external history, as the Traditional Account is not.
