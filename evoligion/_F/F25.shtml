△   △   RELIGION: WHAT IT WAS FOR   ▽   ▽

We and our prophets have imagined 100,000 ways to explain the universe. Periodically, pedestrian thinkers announce that one of them is The Right Way, and that we have final answers: Our favorite description of the universe is complete, with nothing left to learn. Prophets — Mani, 240 CE, and Muhamad, 615 — said it. Nobel laureates — Michelson, 1894, said it, and Dirac, 1929, almost said it. The reptilian brain (and conservatives) believe it absolutely. The limbic brain (and teenagers) believe it emotionally. John Horgan J Horgan (1996) The End of Science (Addison Wesley, C/O Pearson, Upper Saddle River NJ). wrote a book about it (but only by misunderstanding his sources). The truth is just the opposite: the number of interesting questions awaiting answers increases steadily as we learn more about the complexity of the universe.

Nonetheless, the cortical brain often agrees that ‘we know enough for all practical purposes’. The minimum ‘enough’ for social primates was Instinct-1 of the alpha male. This worked for 15 million years of ancestors, getting us out of the trees and into the caves, with fire, furs, and weapons. Still, as Hobbes put it, life was ‘nasty, brutish, and short’. And so it remained, for 160,000 nearly pointless years. Our alpha males look back on this time with nostalgia and homesickness, because although we didn't have much in the way of amenities, what we had was all theirs. They know this in their genes, and they want it to be so once again.

△   Racism was a Good Thing, Once   ▽

Then we found that instincts are not forever valid. After 16 myriennia in the Middle Paleolithic rut, one group deliberately discarded the chimpanzee-rule that we must kill strangers on sight. Neandertals were fair game, but not people who look like us. Judging by an otherwise unexplained 3.7-fold increase in the rate of population growth, this idea occurred in Western Eurasia 35,000 years ago (35 ka), possibly among the Aurignacians. We had been our own worst predator; racism let us multiply, and permitted supra-tribal cooperation so that inventions made sporadically for the last 100,000 years could now be shared instead of dying with their re-inventors. Neandertals, H. erectus, Denisovans (and perhaps others) paid the price for this advance, and disappeared within a few millennia.

Thus began time-binding and exponential technological growth, enabling the Great Leap into the Upper Paleolithic, during which we refined language and invented art, altruism, and synthetic kinship. This last we know today as ‘the brotherhood of man, under the fatherhood of god’ (and its awkward gender-free variants). It had begun as totems, tribal moieties notionally descended from non-human ancestors. Together with the incest taboo, totems enabled enough non-lethal contact with other tribes for genetically beneficial exogamous mating, but manifestly not enough for friendly converse and exchange of ideas.

Given the burgeoning social importance of language, our brains — challenged, then as now by climate change (but then at a rate that biological evolution could cope with) — responded by creating and exploring new hardware, and so developed a better language-based operating system (OS) than the instinctive ‘chimpanzoid’ system of the Middle Paleolithic. Finding that each temporal lobe could develop a sense of personality, our brain used the left lobe for the minutiae of existence, and the right for critical decisions according to the rules of society: this system Julian Jaynes called bicamerality. Interhemisphere communication — right to left over the anterior commissure (?) — was verbal. In times of confusion we heard the voice of ‘god’ instructing us with our culture's tried and true response. One-third of children today have invisible playmates. If these do not wander off by age 9, they may mature to become ‘invisible gurus’ and ‘guardian angels’. Hypnosis, mass movements, demagogs, and the ‘instant-seduction’ You Tube videos show how vulnerable we remain to control by voices. (Only if the ‘voices’ verbalize the dark urges of the unconscious, do we call them schizophrenia — ‘split mind’.)

△   Religion was a Good Thing, Once   ▽

Boys will be boys (as in Lord of the Flies), so when we ran out of Neandertals (~32 ka), we might have returned to killing Other totem members. Apparently we did not, and the Paleolithic figurines suggest that the reason was what we now call ‘religion’, but at the time was the bicameral voice of one tribe's wise woman. We might think that her subconscious was steeped in 60 years of observation of the practical wisdom of her folk. She thought of it as a new voice telling her that the Mother of All Totems forbad violence between her grandchildren.

The Grandmother Totem united the principal concerns of the day: food (the fertility of nature), and children (the fertility of women). She apparently spoke to the Gravettians, with sufficient authority to allow 10,000 years of intertribal peace remembered in folklore as the Golden Age, when religion worked, people were healthy, resources were abundant, and life was good. If the climate was chilly, people were nevertheless finding and sharing new ways of dealing with life's problems. We had begun the exponential increase in both population and mastery of technology, but they were so slow that they posed no threat.

Racism, bicamerality, and the Grandmother Totem increased viability (the fraction of children who survive to breed) until we spread from the Atlantic to Lake Baikal, with long-distance trade and intelligbility. Evolution (numerically) is an increase in viability, and any behavior that increases viability will end up in our genes. However, evolution in practice is the art of jury rigging, so we got more than we needed. We learned to use implausible stories as in-group proof of synthetic-family membership to define those who didn't need killing). But — as Gibbon noted — we also reinforced the need to believe such stories. This need we call pothodoxy — and however useful it was in its youth, in its senility it drives ‘religious’ terrorism.

△   △   RELIGION: WHAT WENT WRONG   ▽   ▽

The Golden Age lasted until ~22 ka, and then the Last Glacial Maximum forced us into half a dozen separate refugia in south Eurasia. For 5000 years we told stories as our languages (and stories) diverged. At ~17 ka, when the ice began to melt, we came out of the refugia with each group telling a story about itself alone. When we met later in the Neolithic, we had new languages and believed incompatible tales, each one ‘true’. Cooperation was no longer possible, cultures and stories competed, from the Aryans going forth to Aryanize the world to the Assyrian coming down ‘like a wolf on the fold’. Around 1200 BCE, faced with multiple version of ‘truth’, and the voices of the gods giving conflicting instructions when strangers met, the consensus-dependent bicameral OS faltered.

Jaynes offered several scenarios for the failure of bicamerality — and assumed that his own replacement — conscious introspection — was universal. The possibility of such replacement arises from 2 phenomena:

  • For 200,000 years, our brain has been the fastest evolving organ in all of biology, and
  • The growing brain writes its own operating system as it develops, with different childhood experiences leading to different results, so that

gradually, other OSes developed. One, taking advantage of new wiring of the prefrontal cortex, produced Jaynes's introspective consciousness, most notably in the Aegean littoral. However, it may be that a more common replacement is not the complexity of introspection (accompanied by the angst of critical analysis), but a simpler approach in which bicamerality (with its automatic and peremptory verbal delivery of experientially absorbed social rules) is replaced by intuitive guidance from parentally indoctrinated rules (defined from scripture now that we had writing), giving us pothodoxy and conservative religion — in short, fundamentalism.

In any event, some people moved on to introspection. Using this new tool, philosophers in Greece, Israel, Iran, India, and China radically rethought their cultural myths during the ‘Axial Age’ (800 – 200 BCE), generating advances as important as the Great Leap of the Upper Paleolithic and the later Enlightenment. Socrates, Isaiah, Zoroaster, Buddha, and Confucius reformed our understanding of the world.

Alpha males (now 1, if not 2, operating systems behind, but hanging on as conservative priest-kings) recognized the threat of Axial ideas and did their best to eradicate them. Thus began the great philosophical divide of the last 2 millennia:

△   Planetary tenure  ▽

If we remain planet-bound, the human experiment — and as far as we know, the universe's closest approach to social justice (deemed to be the goal of the experiment) — dies as the sun ages and warms. Religions consistently explain that ‘heaven’ is a desirable dwelling place in the sky. This should be taken literally: the first planetary civilization to master interstellar travel will own the galaxy. If not us, who?

△   △   EIRENE, GAIA, APOLLO  ▽   ▽

Three ancient Greek divinities suggest the directions religion can most helpfully take. (Remember: no matter how much it bleats about gods and prophets and scripture, if it wants to kill ‘the other’, it remains a tribal marker (like filed teeth) and does not qualify as a religion!

Eirene's purview is peace, and she presided over the Great Leap, Axial Age, Enlightenment, and perhaps the first 50-year period of peace that the Warring States of Europe have experienced. A dedication to peaceful co-existence is the essential defining characteristic of ‘religion’. Eirene inspired the one mandatory ‘religious’ behavior that made civilization possible, and the only guide to behavior that we needed until recently: That which you hate, do not do to sentient beings.

Gaia's concern is planetary homeostasis. Now that our numbers and behavior threaten stability and diversity, Gaia's concerns are the most vital to our short-term survival. The 100-year supply of cheap oil that supported industrial civilization has run out, and much to our annoyance, it turns out that we cannot burn the remaining expensive fossil carbon without raising planetary temperature beyond acceptable limits. No replacement for oil's ability to support more than 3 billion humans is yet in sight. Our politicians remain resolutely ignorant of Human Ecology and Geophysiology — the subjects Gaia would teach were she a professor — so our laws run counter to our needs. The existence of habitable exoplanets, combined with the galactic absence of anybody within reach of radio telescopes, is consistent with 100-year lifetimes of technological civilizations. Lester Brown's Plan B, and Lord Stern's report on climate change lay out the minimal emergency response to conditions created by dismissal of Gaian thinking since the Industrial Revolution. Any religion concerned with human welfare will take these as scripture. Gaia's disciple James Lovelock recognized the evolutionary ethical constraint demanded by survival: Leave the world in better shape for your children.

Apollo is the god of the conscious mind and the uniquely human prefrontal cortex. Primitive religions were ritual (to soothe the reptilian brain), contemporary creeds are limbic (to deal with the destructive aspects of hunger, fear, rage, and lust). Cortical religions such as Zoroaster's and Spinoza's have not been popular. Nevertheless, we now know that the existential questions — Where did we come from? Why are we here? Where are we going? — are sharply constrained by cosmology, evolution, and mathematics. None of these Apollonian disciplines is a threat to the idea of a Guiding Intelligence; they merely show that pontificating about what else that Intelligence might want of us is premature egoism. Revelation, purporting to transmit such information, is an arbitrary expression of the prophet's unconscious. Questions of the spirit will be more easily answered after we have mastered the simpler questions of M-theory, sorted out the evolution of the epigenomic control of biological development, and understand why only those things expressible by computational mathematics exist physically. In the meantime, Apollo inspired the rules that come with the conscious mind: ‘Know Thyself’, the commandment of introspection in the absence of bicamerality, and ‘Nothing in Excess’, the limiting behavior imposed by a spherical planet.

Religion that does not incorporate discoveries of the prefrontal cortex may be wonderfully comforting, but it threatens our very survival.
