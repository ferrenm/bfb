The ocean moves heat poleward in concentrated surface currents (e.g., the Gulf Stream); the return flow is by cold deep water, but melting ice makes the surface of polar seas less dense and inhibits the return flow. The atmosphere moves heat from the tropics to the upper atmosphere, where it can radiate to space. The concomitant to moving hot moist air poleward is moving cold dry air equatorward. Planetary rotation complicates the process, and is largely responsible for weather. Storms are a consequence of this energy transport; increase the energy to be moved, and established weather patterns change and winds increase. The temperate zone does not see a steady rise in mean temperature, but higher-energy storms. △   NEPA, 1970   ▽

Congress authorizes and directs that, to the fullest extent possible … the policies, regulations and public laws of the United States shall be interpreted and administered in accordance with the policies set forth in the National Environmental Policy Act. — NEPA itself.

In 1970, responding to the rising ecological awareness among the electorate inspired by Earth Day, the US Congress passed (NEPA), which introduced the unwelcome concepts of ‘ecology’ and ‘environmental-impact statements’ to Washington's regulatory agencies. NEPA mandated them to consider the ‘relationship between short-term use of [the] environment and the maintenance and enhancement of long-term productivity’, the ‘irreversible and irretrievable commitment of resources’, and the ‘effects on natural resources and on the components, structures, and functioning of … ecosystems’. The IC and ID Conjectures

  ▽

A complex system that works is invariably found to have evolved from a simpler system that works. — A commonplace of engineering.

Throughout history observant people have seen complex systems working well in the natural world. Many interpret this as evidence of Intelligent Design (ID), and conjecture an Intelligent Designer. According to Cicero, ‘Anaxagoras was the first to hold that the order and measure of all things was planned and accomplished by the power and intelligence of an infinite mind.’ This hypothesis was extensively explored by Cicero in 45 BCE, and has been revived many times since. In 2 millennia no one has found a way to advance the hypothesis beyond the bare statement, ‘Parts of the universe look as though they had been designed’. Lucretius added, ‘And many of them are very poorly designed’. Helmholtz came to the same conclusion.

There is no way to falsify ID, so by Popper's definition, it lies outside the reach of science. It is also an undecidable question. This is not unusual, because Gödel showed (in an easily understandable proof) that such questions can be asked but not answered within any formal system. The reason that scientists dismiss ID is that it has not been a fertile paradigm. ID was the only approach we had during the Dark Ages (400–1300 CE), and it led nowhere. Anything useful that it produced is already in the textbooks. No verified prediction has been based on ID, nor has it inspired significant research. When William Paley's Natural Theology (1802) described the Great Watchmaker, he was using the same argument disposed of by Cicero. 1600 years without a new idea suggests strongly that there is little to be gained from ID once the concept is acknowledged and filed away in case someone thinks of a way to falsify it.

The mathematics of species evolution was done in 1930 by R.A. Fisher, combining Mendel's genetics and Darwin's natural selection. When the ID community can produce a book like Fisher's The Genetical Theory of Natural Selection (that is, explaining in predictive mathematical detail the process of ID in the biological world), they will be taken seriously, and any consequential results that they then produce will be included in the text books as a matter of course. Until they can do this, they will be ignored.

Some biological structures are so complex that removal of a single component causes system failure. The doctrine of ‘Irreducible Complexity’ (IC) then conjectures that these ‘cannot have been derived from simpler precursors’ and must have been created in completed form. This reveals a failure of analytic thinking and lack of imagination. Darwin mentioned that people would think of the eye like this. Then he suggested a series of simpler eyes that he thought could be found among animals. These have been found in 4 different phyla. Every IC candidate has been shown by biologists to have a series of earlier, simpler forms with fewer components, in the manner of any household device.

Proponents of ID admit ‘micro-evolution’ to account for the variability of, say, the domestic dog. Otherwise, the catchall term ‘kind’ in Genesis has to accommodate 10 million species in 7 groups. The process that admits ‘micro-evolution’ but prevents the evolution of new species is the so-called ‘species barrier’. This has never been described in molecular detail. While many processes that contribute to biological evolution are understood only at the handwaving level, the simplest — point mutation — is well known. Before ID/IC can compete with evolutionary theory, it must explain the ‘species barrier’ wiinth the same mechanistic detail. Here is a clear, simple test — and one that is resolutely ignored. Margulis's horizontal gene transfer by endosymbiosis is an important addition to Fisher's synthesis, and directly contradicts a species barrier.

Before ID can be taken seriously, we have to understand the consequences of Jolly's hypothesis of Machiavellian intelligence that enables us to deduce what other people might be thinking, and why ‘We cannot help but see design and purpose wherever there is complexity or beauty.’

ID is such a simple idea that it is a central feature of most creation myths. Anthropologists estimate that there have been 100,000 of these. Most were lost before the invention of writing. Still, we have several hundred such stories. (Genesis contains 2, or at least one and a large fragment.) Many of them are entertaining and not without literary merit.

The IC and ID conjectures take us far from the central interest of [whatever specific topic is being covered]. Today they belong to the history of philosophy, for they have not contributed directly to [our subject]. Anyone emotionally wedded to them is encouraged to pursue one of the bold-face topics above as a term paper/class project.

△   Teaching Notes   ▲

In a few short paragraphs, the concept of ‘intelligent design’ has provided the basis for over a dozen major classroom projects. Anaxagoras, Cicero, Lucretius, Helmholtz, Popper, Gödel, Paley, Fisher, Mendel, Darwin, and Jolly can all be understood at many levels, and it is never too early to begin. Helmholtz was both physicist and optician. Why did he think the eye so poorly designed? What other examples of inept design are there? What Darwin actually said is little known, but needs to be, and Origin of Species is easy reading. (Fisher is not for the faint hearted or mathematically unprepared. He practically invented statistics, and was the equal of Gauss and Laplace as a mathematician. But his summary of his work is straightforward: ‘Natural selection is a mechanism for generating an exceedingly high degree of improbability’.) The idea of preventing evolution is a fertile one. Why is it needed by ID? How might it be implemented? Why is it so difficult to do? Machiavellian intelligence is an important part of being human. With whom do we share it? What does it do that is useful? What does it do that might not be useful? Comparative mythology offers numerous suggestions of intelligent (and not so intelligent) design, embedded in stories of varying plausibility. How can these stories be classified? Why do we choose to believe one and not another?

(Critics who might respond ‘That's not what we mean by …!’ should be invited to offer relevant examples and ways to test them. Perhaps compelling the suggester to propose a test which would falsify his example will have salutary results? It could happen.)

A competent teacher will have no trouble devoting one class period to ID/IC at any level from 5th grade to graduate-school seminars, based on suitable modification of the above paragraphs.
