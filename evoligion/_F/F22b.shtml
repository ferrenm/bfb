△   The Greenhouse   ▽

We examine a toy thermodynamic model in which the earth is a blackbody. The model is rigorous, simple, and depends upon 3 numbers only, 2 of which are uncomplicated and one that is not. It ignores minor variables, packs all of the incredible difficulties into that single recalcitrant quantity (τ = infrared optical depth), and still covers everything important (to first order). It depends on the First Law of Thermodynamics (Energy is conserved), the Perfect Gas Law (PV=RT), and the standard well mixed, constant-composition, troposphere. It starts from a steady state in which all the energy that the earth receives from the sun is reradiated to space at the same rate (otherwise the earth warms or cools). These are all valid assumptions, and they are where the ~40 supercomputer models also started. The model For a while the Heartland Institute — a mouthpiece for the 1% — had a paper on the web purporting to show why the ESMs ran hot, but it has been withdrawn without explanation. is shown in Fig. 22.11.

What intercepts the sun's rays is the Earth's disc, with area πR2. What radiates to space is the Earth's spherical surface, with area 4πR2. This factor of 4 accounts for the major difference between the incoming and outgoing energy densities; the other 408 W/m2 is reflected immediately and does not have to be radiated. (Incidentally, 99% of the visible (short-wave) solar and infrared (long-wave) earth radiation — the orange and red outgoing arrows in Fig. 22.11 — lie on opposite sides of the 4-micron line, and so are easily separable by satellites.) The relation beween radiated energy density and temperature is the Stefan-Boltzmann law, W = σT4, where Stefan’s constant, σ = 5.67·10-8 W/m2T4. There is little to argue about in these number, and not much that anyone can do about them except live with them. The CERES (Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System) satellite recorded a mean long-wave energy emission of 239 W/m2 for 2009 (compared to 240 for Fig. 22.11), showing that this simple model is right on target. (It's only variable is the albedo (fraction of sunlight reflected), which depends on aerosols, clouds, snow, ice, and vegetation.

Many sunburnt people realize that the sun is the major actor in global heating (true) — and therefore assume that it is the obvious cause of the minor change we call anthropogenic global heating (not true). To avoid having to deal with the lack of a top to the atmosphere, we ignore gravity and compress the entire atmosphere to its scale depth of 11 km, at which size it is all at a pressure of 1 atmosphere. This compression makes very little difference to anything in the model, and it lets us give a precise meaning to the optical depth τ, which is the lowest depth from which infrared photons can escape without interacting with any more gas molecules. Obviously, like everything else in the climate system (such as the radius of the Earth or its distance from the sun), this is a mean value allowing one to make understandable first-order models. For more detail, see the IPCC reports. Having found the optical depth, we unscale the atmosphere and let gravity restore the pressure gradient.

We now need the concept of ‘potential temperature’. Think of air as existing in ‘parcels’ about the size of weather balloons surrounded by infinitely elastic membranes which prohibit mixing or energy exchange. Motion is then adiabatic (no heat enters or leaves the parcel). The parcel changes pressure, volume, and temperature in accordance with the perfect-gas law as it rises or descends, but it is always at the same temperature at the same altitude. For climatologists, it makes sense to define the potential temperature at the optical-depth/radiation-level (near 500 mb): its actual temperature after being compressed to 1 bar is then the surface temperature of the earth. Notice once again: no heat is required to raise its temperature during this process, and below the radiation surface, back-radiation from greenhouse gasses can be included as part of the turbulent mixing that maintains a constant composition troposphere. PV work is done on a parcel as it descends and is compressed and warmed; it does equal work on the surroundings if it rises, expands, and cools. The rate at which the temperature increases below the radiation level is the lapse rate, and ranges from 5.5°C/km for wet air to 10°C/km for dry air, with the meteorologists' standard value 6.5°C/km.
Energy is conserved in these processes; entropy, which measures the usability of that energy, is produced. Entropy is always confusing because we have no sensor that notices it, and no word for its unit, which is joule/°K,* although the name ‘clausius’ has been used informally. The most accessible definition is dS=dQ/T, where dS is the change in entropy, dQ the change in heat, and T the temperature. The Earth exports 5800°/255° = 22.7 times as much entropy as it absorbs from the sun, and it is this entropy production that makes energy available and so drives weather and ocean currents and supports life.

1. Water vapor

This is perfectly true — in the lower troposphere where radiation is trumped by turbulent mixing. 2. Saturation

Noticing that all the available energy in the infrared CO2 band is absorbed in about 10 m, a voluble subset of deniers has fastened on this fact as meaning — erroneously — that adding CO2 to the atmosphere cannot warm us further, because, obviously, all the IR is already being absorbed. This is exactly equivalent to saying that a cloud (which blocks all direct sunlight) does not get darker as it thickens, but simply casts a pure black shadow.

Another way to see that this argument fails is to look at Venus, whose radiation temperature is 220°K. This is lower than the Earth's 255°K even though Venus is closer to the sun, because dense sulfuric-acid clouds reflect 80% of the incoming energy. Nevertheless, 965,000 ppm of CO₂ raise Venus's surface temperature by 510°K, compared to the Earth's paltry 33°K increase.

Obtaining the 288°K annual mean surface temperature from the observed daily 100°C range of ground temperatures across the Earth required a long and painstaking international effort. The 255°K radiation temperature is a function of the albedo (fraction of energy reflected), which required worldwide data about land-surface reflectivity, aerosol composition and cloud droplet size, concentration, and distribution, and gas-photon interactions. Explaining and predicting the future of the optical depth is an immensely complex chemical, physical, and mathematical problem, requiring 7-dimensional integration (3D spatial + composition + concentration + wavelength + time) over the spectrum of interactions (with light, and with eachother) of some 200 time-varying chemical species.

What I (and others) failed to realize — 50 years ago — is that most of the ocean does not come in contact with enough solid carbonates to matter on a human timescale. There are none on the deep sea floor (because sinking foraminifera tests dissolve by a pressure effect before reaching the bottom), and it takes 10,000 years for water to make the thermohaline circuit (sinking under polar ice shelves to form bottom water, flowing slowly around the globe, rising imperceptibly to the surface — most distantly in the North Pacific — and flowing by surface currents back to the poles.
