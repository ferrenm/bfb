[S]ocial problems have to be answered with social solutions, and … the only effective response to our ongoing national catastrophe must be a completely new set of social ideals.  But to achieve that, we will need a new myth, one that will both value the accomplishments of the past and look to the potentials of the future. — Cory Panshin

This is not a time to think of national security. It is a time to think of planetary security — indeed, of planetary survival. — Michael Ruppert.

Evolution is ad hoc, so racism and religion share with the immune system and the eye the challenge of adapting to a changing environment. Few of us are reluctant to seek medical help against bacteria The exceptions are, to some extent, self-limiting. A cousin, boon companion of my childhood, died without issue of a treatable disease because his immune system needed help that his Christian Scientist upbringing refused to consider. The rest of his family became creative members of society; he died of infection by a deadly meme, propagated by a pseudobenevolent (and highly profitable) institution.

Natural selection eliminated whatever genetic combination allowed him to feel that belief was preferable to antibiotics. Can I weep for his loss? Yes. Can I approve of his parents' steadfast faith? No. Pothodoxy meant that his contribution to society was limited to the genetic death of a dysfunctional allele selection. He had had more to offer. , or assist the short-sighted eye with glasses. Racism — however beneficial it may have been in the Paleolithic — is no longer useful in a multiracial society, so we have done our best to fix it by discouraging rudeness, punishing transgression, and celebrating diversity. A religion claiming ultimate truth by revelation is no longer useful in a multicredal world, nor is aggressive proselytizing. These need fixing as much as infection, myopia, and racism.

‘Fixing’ religion offers employment for nearly anyone, depending upon which aspect one wants to work on. Table 21.0 attempts a 2-dimensional outline of the challenges, but be aware that ‘medium-term’ in the header is equivalent to ‘planetary lifetime’. ‘Ultimate concern’ runs far into the future!

We ignore the soteriological aspects of religion because they are irrelevant and distracting. This is not a time to think about personal salvation. It is a time to think about planetary salvation. Naturally it is easier to dismiss Chapter 20's future headlines, and imagine that something will turn up to solve all problems. It is easier to believe that ritual acceptance of a savior or prophet absolves you of all other responsibilities. Comforting as such evasions may be, they guarantee disaster. No ritual or belief is going to save us.

Rituals were invented to minimize thinking in the Jurassic, but they didn't save the dinosaurs from the Chicxulub impactor. Ritual may help organize your life, but don't expect more than that from it. Belief was invented to minimize thinking after the bicameral days, but hasn't saved us from exploitation by schemers. Belief may make you a better person, but don't ask more of it.

Chapter 21, Pogo's Predicament, reminds us that most of our problems are of our own making. Current religion is not helping us solve them. Some suggestions about short-, medium-, and long-term repairs are offered.

Chapter 22, Gaian Repairs, notes that a practical substitute for faith in divine intervention is to recognize that instantiating Moral Justice is hard for us and will take time, so our first priority is a sustainable culture. The Gaian imperatives thus take precedence over the Eirenic. Lester Brown's Plan B and the Stern Report lay out most of what needs to be done, with the added suggestion that the only truly sustainable energy source is space-based solar power. Theologically, this step implies extending synthetic kinship to our planet.

Chapter 23, Eirenic Repairs, suggests, as a useful replacement for ritual, concentration on the Eirenic aspects of religion and actuallly getting down to brass tacks and building Moral Justice into the culture. Inequality, bad law, poor education, corporate greed, mismanagement of science, and some aspects of religous philosophy all need attention. Theologically, this step implies taking seriously the common denominator of our better religions, the Golden Rule.

Chapter 24, Myth and Vision, examines the need for ‘nonscientific’ ideas, suggests some myths that need destroying, offers some suggestions about replacements, and introduces a useful idea that is crying for its own mythopoet.

Chapter 25, Recapitulation, reviews the argument of Books 1-6.◼
