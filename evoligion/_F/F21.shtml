Pogo's Predicament

⩔    ▽  

[I]f your party's central mission is to comfort the comfortable and afflict the afflicted, you need to claim that all kinds of wonderful side effects will take place from what might otherwise look like a combination of greed and cruelty. — Paul Krugman

The one-line extract from Table 21.0 in the heading table suggests that an important short-term project for fixing religions is the replacement of the objects of current pothodoxy. The religious person appears to be able to justify nearly anything as ‘important to his religion’, so the myths protected by traditional belief range from the harmlessly diverting (Easter bunny) through tribal markers (beards, dress codes) to erroneous and socially disruptive (homophobia, male chauvinism) and the economically fallacious (tax exemptions). Why we haven't refined our myths in the 3 centuries since the Enlightenment is a deep question about human behavior: explanations include inertia, conservatism, neophobia, privilege, and self-righteousness, none of them exactly a worthy cause. Equally likely, perhaps, is our reluctance to arrive at Pogo's conclusion, with nowhere else to lay the blame.

The primary myth of the United States is ‘American Exceptionalism’, which combined the free gifts of a virgin continent (in terms of industrial resources), the heritage of the Northern European Enlightenment (in the form of the Founding Fathers), and immigrants (a subset of the population self-selected for initiative) into an initially realistic evaluation: ‘We are blessed among nations!’. Unfortunately, wishful thinking and Instinct-1 have turned this into pervasive, if mythical, conclusions:

  • We can do whatever we want to the environment.
  • We can do whatever we want to other nations and lesser peoples.
  • This will always be true.

American Exceptionalism still pervades American thinking. A century as the world's major superpower left us believing that this was the natural and permanent order of things.

Babies go through a similar stage. The classical outcome is to grow up; if this fails, we end up with narcissists and psychopaths instead of adults.

DIY RELIGION

⩔   ▽  

Europe had 4 powerful mythological traditions in full career before the introduction of Christianity: [… Classical Greek, Roman, Celtic, and Germanic. They agreed in] represent[ing] something that is typical in Europe and nowhere else — that is, respect for the individual and the individual path. — Mythologist Joseph Campbell (1990) Transformations of Myth through Time (Harper Perennial, NY, 1999): 210; emphasis added.

There was little outcry from America's churches against the Patriot Act's carefully planned destruction of civil rights, or against Presidential sullying of America's international image by a return to torture and the mentality of the Inquisition. Civil rights being our principal reification of the abstract goal of living together peacefully — which was the original purpose of religion — one might hope that churches would take a more active role in their defence. Early readers reminded me that churches lose their tax exemption for indulging in politics. The definition of ‘politics’ is itself a political question, and no doubt those in power would interpret anything that threatened them as illegal. Two points:
1) The Old Testament prophets constantly railed against defective government, and this can be done from a position of high principle (as opposed to really illegal partisan campaigning).
2) Church income not used for Gaian and Eirenic purposes should be taxed — and civil rights are as Eirenic as it gets. We still understood this a few decades ago, when anybody who remembered the Axials or the Enlightenment put himself in harm's way during the Civil Rights Movement.

One hopes that any Christian — and his church — will be sensitive to a recrudescence of inquisitorial practice, lest we remind ourselves the hard way of the centuries (~12th–20th) when the church was the chief enemy of civilization.

I was in Australia for the Nixon years (as usual, without TV), and followed the daily degradation of the Presidency only through the political cartoons stuck on campus doors. I was in northern Europe for the Bush years ( " ), and could not observe the public consequences of electoral corruption, disregard of the Constitution, and corrosion of democracy. Perhaps I missed something that would help explain why the official myth of 9/11 so easily overwhelmed what I had understood to be a national commitment to the Enlightenment ideals of the Founding Fathers. I was continually embarrassed by the critical, if insightful, comments of my foreign professorial colleagues, but did what little one expat could to apologize for the behavior of my government. Nobody I knew had voted for them.

In any case, from my distant perspective, I see no sign of a grass-roots resurgence of ecclesiastical leadership to regain the rights that we fought the Revolutionary War to establish. Why is this? Don't sectarian schools teach history? The threat to any of our protections from government is a threat to all, including freedom from an imposed religion.

Religious people rarely adhere to every nuance of their religion — if for no other reason, because there are too many contradictions in scripture, and some practices once commanded or performed by the priests Human and animal sacrifice, lapidation, temple prostitution, killing infidels, inquisitorial torture, burning witches, seducing altar boys, comforting widows, … are now frowned upon in civilized nations. We are forced to pick and choose, and we follow interpretations which appeal to us. Our minds — just like Thomas Paine's — are our own churches, no matter how slavishly we follow selected portions of a revealed religion. If we are not going to smite our neighbors for wearing cotton-polyester shirts (as we were ordered to do), or take bullwhips to bankers (as our pre-eminent behavioral model did — although here we probably should follow Jesus's example), we might as well accept full responsibility for defining our own personal religion. Then, if it has rough edges, we have nowhere to lay the blame, and must smooth them ourselves. If it turns out to be incapable of doing its duty, it is ours to correct.

As Campbell's epigraph makes clear, this is the Western tradition. With unique insight, he rescued a key instance that is precisely relevant to our situation. Malory, translating and condensing La Queste del Saint Graal as Le Morte d'Arthur, omitted the passage that for Campbell ‘epitomize[d] the whole sense of the Grail symbology’. When Gawain proposed the Grail quest as a fitting occupation for the Round Table, the knights ‘thought it would be a disgrace to go forth in a group, so each entered the forest at a point that he, himself, had chosen, where it was darkest and there was no path’ {211}. The path — your path — ‘is the fulfillment of something that never was on the earth before, namely, your own potentiality’ {212}. This idea is foreign to earlier cultures, and to contemporary ‘honor’ cultures of the East where the individual is submerged in her tribe or family.

Personal Options

⩔  

I was compelled to participate in worldly affairs, which excited the envy of fools, but which made the wise pity me. — Al-Biruni, Persian polymath, 1000 CE.

Table 21.1 suggests 4 approaches to ‘fixing’ personal religion. Because religion is primarily a social activity, none of these is entirely satisfactory. I have Buddhist friends who have sold houses and changed cities to be near a preferred guru, and been happy with the result. But schools and jobs often militate against uprooting. If one were serious about Akhnaton or another minority religion which might be totally acceptable, one might be hard put to find compatible minds within reach.

If the opportunity to join a pressure group for intelligent change is important, joining and influencing an existing congregation may be the best option. Kirsopp's Law — ‘The church shrinks from left to right’ — suggests that this becomes increasingly difficult with time, and I can vouch for the improbability of changing anything a Baptist minister has once gotten firmly into his head.

The best example we have of ‘Consensus DIY’ is Islam-3 in its many versions. Many Moslems (apparently unaware of the scriptural instructions of Islams-1 and -2) subscribe to versions of Islam-3 — always interpreted as ‘the Real Islam’ — that they are personally comfortable with. These are often partially Eirenic (as Mohamet's Islam-1 and Tradition's Islam-2 are not), but they retain an inherited belief structure with many dubious remnants, and they have no influence on Islam-1. As soon as they recognizably differ from Islam-1 they become apostate and subject to Islam-1's corrective lethality.

There is a rising discipline of ‘thealogy’, a long overdue response to the boost given to male egos by the discovery of the function of semen which still dominates the patriarchal religions. One aspect is a (necessarily self-conscious) attempt to rediscover the several pre-Paulist religions of Europe, going by the names of Goddess worship and Wicca, with many variants. With its matrist outlook, it is anathema to Instinct-1, so it is worth investigating. According to a New Zealand anthropologist who studied the local Wiccan development, Wicca allows people

to locate authority within themselves, to give themselves licence to construct and conduct their own spirituality (unmediated by minister, priest or priestess), to recognize their beauty and value, to use their power, to trust their own judgements and intuition, to take responsibility for their lives, to love themselves and others. — K Rountree (2005) ‘The Lion, the Witch and the Celebration of Discursive Diversity’ Feminist Theology 13.2: 161–173.

Rountree does not explain why New Zealanders don't take these abilities for granted: perhaps it has something to do with growing up Anglican? Still, if it takes Wicca to alert them to their autonomy, then Wicca pretty well sums up what one expects of a DIY religion.

In the absence of projects like Wicca, the DIY solution is for rugged individualists who feel no need for the flock. It has the advantage of providing a perfect fit to one's philosophical predilections, but this may not be much help in resolving real-world problems. Still, we know enough about the genesis of religion for people to construct versions that lift the spirits, inspire care for our life-support system, and give meaning to life, without the urge to destroy others.

If you feel up to advancing the state of the art, the dyads of Frankfurt critical theory and the project of reconciling radical enlightenment with any aspect of supernaturalism provide serious challenges. The summary of the Gathas in Table 14.1 and the end paper strikes me as the closest thing we have to an all-around guide to behavior, and it has the advantage of already having influenced Hinduism, Judaism, Christianity, and — at least in the trivial direction of monotheism — Islam. It should not be impossible to rediscover some of these ideas in a Christian environment, since Zoroaster introduced many of the better ideas picked up by the Abrahamic religions.

Constraints

⩔  

If there is any universal characterization that applies to known religions, it might be that they recognize no common constraint. This is sufficient to exclude many of them from the category ‘useful’. The two constraints demanded by that category should by now be familiar.

⩓  

Eirenic constraint ⩔  

An imbalance between the rich and the poor is the oldest and most fatal ailment of all republics. — Plutarch

When the Congressional Research Service produces a report that shows zero correlation between low income-tax rates for the wealthy, on the one hand, and improved performance of the national economy, on the other — thus undermining one of the central GOP arguments two months before the election — the reaction of Senate Republicans is to force the withdrawal of the report. — Tod S. Purdun

A personal religion should be Eirenic — not ‘peaceful’ in Mohammed's sense of allowing people to live only after they agree to believe your story and pay your taxes — but in the wider sense of the Enlightenment values. This does not mean that we have to like everyone, or that we are all of equivalent value. The things that make evolution appear teleological, that have led to better survival of our children — cooperation, The silicon-chip industry is about as cutthroat as it can get, but its success in implementing Moore's Law has for 25 years depended upon the underlying cooperative effort of Sematech, a consortium of 14 rival companies dedicated to bringing technical possibilities to fruition. altruism, and intelligence — are demonstrably more valuable than competition, self-centeredness, and stupidity. A cooperative open-source Linus Torvald is more valuable to the world than a monopolist Bill Gates; an altruistic microbanker Mohammed Yunus than financier David Rockefeller, an exiled Dalai Lama than a vote-fraud president like George W. Bush — because of their ideas and behavior.

On the other hand, whether we are valuable or useless to society as individuals, we are equal in the sight of whatever gods may be, and — theoretically — we are equal in the eyes of the law. (‘Theoretically’, because unequal arms mean unequal combat and biased outcomes.)

Proselytizing religions fail the Eirenic constraint because they recognize equality only after conversion (Islam maintains that infidels have no souls). What is required is that everyone have an equal opportunity to work for, and share in, the religious and evolutionary task of ‘establishing Justice, insuring domestic Tranquility, providing for the common defence, promoting the general Welfare, and securing the Blessings of Liberty to ourselves and our Posterity’.

We wrote those goals into our founding political document because our religions had failed to achieve these preconditions to a civilized life. One thing we seem able to agree upon is that we can't do these things when we are miserable, which makes the alleviation of human misery a fundamental duty of society. Ignorant people make life difficult for us (especially if they get elected), so it is in our society's interest to educate them to the limit of their ability. Economic inequality, with its resulting envy and inefficiency, causes many problems, so it is in our group interest to find an equitable economic policy. Unhealthy people are both miserable and unproductive, so it is in our social interest to keep them healthy. (Caveat: Public medical interest ceases in the absence of personal responsibility for avoiding the 4 great life-shorteners: lack of excersize, unhealthy diet, obesity, and smoking. Indulge here, and pay the price yourself.)

It is not necessarily the job of Eirenic religion to deal with these issues directly, but to provide the psychological grounding that makes them palpable matters of immediate and public concern.

Gaian constraint

⩔  

A personal religion should be Gaian. The greatest Gaian danger today is ecosystem collapse as the result of unsustainable practices. 3 years after the tsunami, radioisotopes from the 250 tons of nuclear fuel that melted out of 3 Fukushima reactors are moving down the US coast, and will spread throughout the Pacific Ocean in another 3 years. This may help dolphins and tuna (by making the latter unsafe for human consumption), but the Daiichi reactor site is still a potpouri of disasters waiting to happen. The worst-case scenario spreads plutonium aerosol across the northern hemisphere, where its 80-million year half life will make it a permanent carcinogen. This might trump ecosystem collapse. This is something we have much experience with. but little awareness of, primariy because record-keeping is lost early in the collapse. It is, for example, moot whether desiccation, salinization, or warfare was the principal destroyer of Mesopotamia, or whether these can even be separated. The Dust Bowl is our clearest modern example. Caused by poor farming practices and a deep El Niño, its social consequences were unnecessrily exacerbated by bankerly (ie, deliberate, cruel, profitable, malicious) exploitation, it is an experience we should pay more attention to, because its friends and relations lie in wait for us as the global temperature rises and armored psyches multiply.

The carrying capacity of the world depends upon one's axioms, and in particular, upon the per-capita energy deemed necessary to maintain an adequate life style. Given this number, ‘sustainability’ enfores an exact mathematical constraint, given by:

‘sustainability’ requires that   
(solar energy) / (energy per person) ≥ world population   (1)

While the equation is defining, the parameters are adjustable. The term on the left is a complex function of technology, economics, politics and social systems, and as mentioned, we are already consuming a major fraction of currently available solar energy. Nuclear fusion — perennially ‘50 years in the future' — is the only known extension of this equation. There are also material constraints on ‘sustainable’, since the planetary inventory of crustal chemical elements is finite, those in the core inacessible, and those of the mantle limited to volcanoes and mid-ocean ridges. Entropy is not on our side, meaning that sustainable recycling uses appreciable energy.

Where has the zinc gone when galvanized iron rusts? Where is the molybdenum that went into dry lubricants? The tungsten in incandescent bulbs and toolbits? The mud in the upper reaches of Narragansett Bay may be worth mining for precious metals from the early jewelry trade, but this is not true of most of our dispersed materials. If we can't wait for geophysiological processes to reconcentrate these, we have to find alternative materials, which is not always feasible.

Considering the relative rates of population growth and state failure on the one hand, and resource depletion and geophysiological change on the other, the presently neglected discipline of ‘sustainability studies’ — encapsulated in equation (1) — should be a primary and ultimate concern of Gaian religion.

Energetic and material constraints mean that we can even now estimate the sustainable population for any chosen parameters. 3 billion people at 1/3 the US standard of living is an optimistic but perhaps reasonable value: if you want more (because whatever political or religious dogma you are caught up in insists upon ‘More is better’) show how it can be done sustainably. No advocate of population growth has yet been sufficiently numerate to do this! Wishful thinking does not work here: We need an engineering answer, with plans, numbers, worst-case scenarios, safety factors, and instructions on how to recoup from failure. (Sample: Do you have a feasible route whereby we might retreat gracefully to 3 billion if 7 billion proves to be undesirable?)

In this context, note that UNFPA's State of World Population 2011, like most official reports, is an interpretation from an innumerate 3rd-world point of view that assumes Stone Age instincts are immutable values, and that the conditions that allowed the myriennial Golden Age are accessible as soon as we recover from colonialism. The Marxist claim that every hungry mouth is accompanied by a pair of capable hands — which fails by eq(1) — is replaced by the hope that more people mean more good ideas, thus increasing the numerator of eq(1) (ie, making solar collectors more efficient. Currently, 20% is good; 45% is on the horizon.) This hope overlooks the Aegean experience, where 2500 years ago, 30,000 people had perhaps 75% of all known good ideas — and we killed the Aegeans through the population pressure of tribal peoples with no ideas, and extinguished most of the good ideas for 2 millennia. We can do this again.

Enlightenment values

⩔  

In crafting a personal religion, we can dispense with Secularism for the sake of mythic structure, but other principal Enlightenment values should figure prominently: Scepticism, Tolerance, Rationality, Individuality, Democracy, Empiricism, and Social Justice. All of these values are in short supply; our own schools make a point of not teaching some of them; and there are wannabe world systems that reject every one.

Epistemology

⩔  

One also needs to decide on criteria for truth. Table 21.2 suggests some stages in developing a working scepticism. There is no problem with believing something beyond the reach of science, provided that it does no harm and increases the Eirenic and Gaian content of life — and can be modified if later found incompatible with data. I know Buddhists and Conservative Jews who perform what I regard as pointless rituals, for the same disciplinary reason that I abjure owning an automobile: rituals do no harm (and may help), and they provide a structure to daily life that reminds one of what is important. (Such people are selective and critical; they do not kill infidels or homosexuals or neighbors who gather wood on the Sabbath, although the tribal laws that outline their rituals demand these things of them also.)

Meaning

⩔  

If neither harps nor houris lie ahead, what is there to compensate for life's miseries? If nirvana and ecstasy are on a par with the haze of alcohol and sparkles of LSD, what meaning is there in meditation and attempts to reach these states? Why not just settle for a nap?

‘The Meaning of Life’ is a very personal matter, but rather over-rated among the chattering class. For most of the world, it reduces to surviving for one more day; maybe tomorrow will be better. Cats are content to catch mice, sire or raise kittens, and purr. Dogs want to please the pack leader and go for walks. In the affluent West, we watch bank accounts grow. Survival, purrs, walks, and bank balance affirm personal worth. ‘The ancestors helped me through another day’. ‘My god loves me’, rumble rumble. ‘I hear and obey and will be rewarded’, thump thump. ‘Worth? I know exactly what I am worth!’

Others may settle for the ‘meaning’ offered by a guru and make it their own. But most gurus have paltry imaginations: Paul, Mohammed, Moon, and Smith seldom rose above daytime TV. L. Ron Hubbard at least matched Star Wars in epic scope. Anyone who has made it through this book can do better! The US Founding Fathers never had to have agonized about Meaning: Moses, Jesus, and Enlightenment philosophers offered a mix that guided 2 centuries of world leadership.

‘Leaving a better world’ looks more difficult now than in those heady days of a virgin continent and inspired statesmen. The immediate challenge is to ameliorate life on the way to the world of 2100 CE, with its population of 3 billion, no petroleum, encroaching seas, stronger storms, more deserts, camels, clipper ships, hotter weather, less air conditioning. This is the problematic process that British inventor and atmospheric scientist James Lovelock calls a ‘sustainable retreat’. Denial, which has always been our most popular defence against the unpleasant, works only until reality catches upwith it.

Considering the ethics of some of the founders of known religions, and the slow increase in ethical understanding over the millennia, the average person today should be able to construct a religion superior to the average of the 100,000. It would not take much to construct one in the top 1%. This follows from the knowledge that most of our religions were managed or designed bicamerally for power, or consciously for profit — the lowest of motives and pure Instinct-1. The average person, thinking only of the existential questions and ultimate concerns of religion in the sort of universe he would like to live in, will automatically do better than a religion crafted to extract money from people.

The major epitomizations of ethical behavior from the last 2500 years are collected in Table 21.6. While they are all useful, they differ in important ways. Diogenes resolved the question of deliberate evil, (but, because insufficient money is disastrous in a complex monetized society, most people are sure his observation applies only to those who have more money than they do). The Axial Age produced variants of the Golden Rule, making it possible for us to live in communities, but they suffer the universal failing of naturalistic ethics, having neither carrots or sticks to back them up. Without them we get failed states and auxipauses and chimpanzee politics, but the world goes on as it always has, and many people, preferring not to grow up, see no reason to give more than lip service to them. The Enlightenment extrapolated beyond the individual, looking for a more universal formulation, but adding little. One has to be very selective about Nietzsche.

However, interpersonal ethics is not the real challenge. More serious is the need to complete the work of the Axial Thinkers and Enlightenment by finding a persuasive philosophical fact-based rationale for civilized behavior, where civilized is shorthand for ‘Eirenic and Gaian’ — ie, ‘religious’. What public stance is there that can prevail over corporate greed and the only thing it understands: ‘Maximize monetary return on investment’? Any CEO, any MBA, will explain that this is the Golden Rule writ large. It is exactly what he wants for himself, so it must be what is best for the world. ‘What's good for [the CEO of] General Motors is good for the country.’ This turned out to be so far from true, that at the end, the President of the country had to fire the CEO of GM. British business schools are trying to teach MBAs ‘corporate social responsibility’, so that in 15 to 20 years ‘socially sustainable’ concepts such as ‘environmental protection, race and gender equality, workforce rights, and avoidance of destructive risk taking’ will have worked their way to the top levels of management {GW 2009-05-15 p43}. Too little, too late, but certainly a nice idea. With good parenting, they would have learned these things in childhood!

Lovelock managed to put universal evolutionary ethics as tersely as Hillel's Golden Rule. His approach has the world's biggest stick-and-carrot behind it, although it acts too slowly to be easily recognized. The carrot is survival of your genes — the very thing that you and Instinct-1 are all about. This is why we fall in love, think sex is fun, money good, and power worth conniving for. The stick is non-survival of our genes, for any species which systematically destroys the support system of the succeeding generation will quickly remove itself.

The idea of leaving a better world for our children is one that must have crossed the mind of every parent worthy of the name. But the evolutionary reason for doing so — if we don't, we go extinct — is a key realization. Any number of feudofascist philosophers have claimed that we owe nothing to future generations (‘What has posterity ever done for us?’). This is the wrong end of the stick: We are someone's posterity, and the beneficiaries of what they did to improve the world. In the English Civil Wars, and the US Revolutionary War, people died so that we might enjoy the benefits guaranteed by the US Constitution. A Guardian reader answered the Notes & Queries question, ‘Has any war left both sides better off?’ with the suggestion that the American Revolution did exactly that. We owe them a debt which we cannot repay — except by doing as much for posterity as they did. We could begin by re-instating the US Constitution.

The self-centered attitude is, perhaps, suitable for adolescents, whose obligation to their genes is to stay alive long enough to pass them on. Note that evolution has seen to it that the better adolescents are also equipped with a healthy dose of idealism and the ability to see that their parents' generation is making a dog's breakfast out of the world they are going to inherit. Recall the old saw about socialists: ‘Anyone who is not a socialist at 20 has no heart; anyone who is still a socialist at 40 has no brain’ — the causal difference being the neural rewiring of the 30s. Much the same here, if less pithy: ‘Anyone not in thrall to Instinct-1 at 20 will leave no descendants; anyone still in thrall to Instinct-1 at 40 is working toward extinction’. A testable hypothesis is that if the wave of mature neural rewiring that sweeps forward in our 30s peters out before reaching the frontal lobes, it leaves behind a feudofascist.

The idea that how we individually behave has any effect on the long-term survival of the species is a very recent discovery; not obvious to anyone except the evolutionary biologists of the last few decades; and most clearly articulated by James Lovelock. Lynn Margulis, E.O. Wilson, George Hutchinson, and others can claim equal credit for the ideas. John Muir, Aldo Leopold, and their ilk could all say, ‘Yes, that's what I meant!’ Lovelock has been the first to acknowledge them — but he said it best:

But now it just happens, quite by accident and not by any conscious thought by myself or anybody else, that [geophysiological] Gaia does offer moral guidance. It does so because its rules are simple: any species that improves its environment favours the welfare of its progeny, whereas any species that adversely affects the environment dooms it for its progeny. And this is very moral. It gives us something to which we are accountable — the Earth itself. — James Lovelock, father of Gaia theory.

Empiricism, Semantics and Ontology With apologies to Rudolf Carnap (1952) ‘Empiricism, Semantics and Ontology’ reprinted in Carnap’s Meaning and Necessity (U. Chicago Press): 202–221.

An aspect of Table 21.5 which may not be obvious is a qualitative difference between the epitomizations. Most of them involve value judgements and are impossible to quantify. PhilosophersRF Kitchener (1994) ‘Toward a critical philosophy of science’ in D Lamb New Horizons in the Philosophy of Science (Avebury, Aldershot): 4–25. regard

ethical statements as matters of personal taste and not subject to rational justification; furthermore, since they have no epistemic standing and since facts and norms are categorically distinct, facts cannot be relevant to their epistemic standing. — [with verb tense changed to present]

More compactly, ‘is’ and ‘ought’ are categorically distinct, so (as often noted) one cannot deduce ‘ought’ from ‘is’. However, ‘ought’ and ‘will be’ are not categorically distinct: ‘will be’ follows as directly from ‘ought’ as our actions follow our obligations, so any concern for the future, however slight, imposes ethics on our behavior.

One might look hopefully at Hutchesons's ‘greatest happiness’ and ‘greatest number’, but one cannot optimize 2 variables simultaneously. ‘Happiness’ is difficult to measure, and it is not clear, as we face the imminent reduction of human biomass, that the ‘greatest number’ is in any sense desirable.

However, Lovelock's epitomization is a different matter. There will be different definitions of ‘improving the environment’ and ‘welfare of progeny’, but (as shown in the next 2 chapters) facts are relevant to these concepts and they are subject to rational and economic justification, so that unlike the other ethical prescriptions, they can be analyzed, quantified, costed, and reduced to specific political choices on which a group consensus can be reached. Coyotl's prophets failed to understand the need for self-preserving behavior, or to explain it in ways that alpha males and fundamentalists can understand, so it is a case of Gaia or extinction.
