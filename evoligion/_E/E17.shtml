△   1. Anthropic Principle   ▽

The Anthropic Principle is an attempt to explain the apparent ‘fine tuning’ of the universe. The laws of physics and the constants of nature allow the development of self-aware observers (ie, us). Change the laws or the constants, and the universe will not allow life as we know it to exist. Change the accidents of evolutionary history, and humans do not evolve. This idea was immediately acclaimed by theologians, as noted in the section epigraph. We will see that this triumphalism was a bit premature.

△   –1. The Anthropocentric Principle   ▽

A better name for the Anthropic Principle described by Barrow and Tipler is the ‘Anthropocentric Principle’. It is the centerpiece of Middle Eastern theology (Genesis 1:26 ‘Let us make man in our image’) and Western philosophy (Protagoras: ‘Man is the measure of all things’). In time it extends from the Paleolithic to today (‘How many Harvard students does it take to change a light bulb?’). One. He holds the bulb and the universe revolves around him. It strokes our egos in a way that nothing else can approach. This in itself is sufficient to make the idea suspect.

Nearly 3 dozen pithy sentences have been put forward as Anthropic Principles, and given acronyms like Strong (SAP) and Weak (WAP) and Somewhat Weak (SWAP). The Final (FAP) said:

Intelligent information-processing must come into existence in the universe, and, once it comes into existence, it will never die out.

Martin Gardner sagely suggested this one should be renamed the Completely Ridiculous Anthropic Principle (CRAP). (If it were true, its most probable interpretation would be that we are not the intelligent information-processors after all.)

Cosmologist Brandon Carter B Carter (1974) ‘Large number coincidences and the anthropic principle in cosmology’ in MS Longair Confrontation of Cosmological Theories with Data (Reidel, Dordrecht) 291–298.) coined the term ‘anthropic principle’, And considering the confusion it has caused, he later wished he had given it a different name. so he gets the last word on the appropriate formulation, which turns out to be non-mysterious and common-sensical:

[W]hat we can expect to observe must be restricted by the conditions necessary for our presence as observers’.

This reduced the Anthropic Principle to a simple tautology, but it did not stop postmodern maundering. Nick Bostrom N Bostrom (2002) Anthropic Bias Observation Selection Effects in Science and Philosophy (Routledge, NY & London): Ch. 2. put it more simply: a really astute observer would not be surprised to find that the universe seemed finely tuned to support him. What other kind could he live in?

Physicist Victor Stenger VJ Stenger (1995) The Unconscious Quantum: Metaphysics in Modern Physics and Cosmology (Prometheus Books, Amherst NYC). got fed up with people marveling at the appearance of fine tuning, so he wrote a little computer program called MonkeyGod. MonkeyGod assigns random values to 4 of the critical physical parameters — the ones that cannot vary by as much as a factor of 2 and still permit life as we know it. The fine structure constant, the strong-interaction strength at low energy, the mass of the electron, and the mass of the proton. From these, Stenger made order-of-magnitude estimates of such things as the mean lifetime of stars and the size of planets. Life as we know it cannot appear in most of MonkeyGod's universes — but life as we don't know it seems to be possible over ±5 orders of magnitude for the ‘magic numbers’. The stars live long enough, and complex structures are possible. Some structures are autocatalytic — and we have the first characteristic of life, reproduction. The others will soon follow. 10 orders of magnitude is not fine tuning by any stretch of the imagination. Inhabitants who find a universe fine tuned to their needs do so precisely because they evolved in it.

Cosmologist Anthony Aguirre A Aguirre (2001) ‘The Cold Big-Bang Cosmology as a Counter-example to Several Anthropic Arguments’ Phys. Rev. Looking back at the Anthropic Principle, one hates to rain on Wheeler's parade, but there is an alternative to the Participatory Anthropic Principle. PAP starts off with ‘[One version of] quantum theory states that any physical system remains in a superposed state of all possibilities until it interacts with …’ — and at this point PAP suggests, ‘ an observer’, which is the Copenhagen consensus. A less restrictive alternative suggests interaction with ‘the environment’ — a commonplace process devoid of paradoxes and any need for ‘intelligent observers’ to bring the universe into existence. Ockham's Razor is clear on which of these to prefer!

Interacting with the environment leads to quantum decoherence, or loss of entanglement between the components of a system in a superposed quantum state. The major consequence of decoherence is the classical behavior that we normally observe, but it takes several pages of quantum algebra to show this.

Consider a macroscopic system such as Schrödinger's cat, The cat is put in a closed box, with a slow radioactive source, a Geiger counter, a hammer, and a vial of cyanide. If the counter registers a hit, the hammer drops, the vial is broken, and the cat dies. (I cannot account for the ailurophobia of the Copenhagen group, but there it is). Seeking a necessary role for humanity in the universe, it occurred to them that since they didn't know the state of the cat until they opened the box and observed it, it must be that their observation was the essential step that ‘collapsed’ the possible wave functions of the dead/alive cat. (And why wasn't the cat a sufficient observer of her own state? The many cats I have known kept track of such matters!) existing (at least in the minds of the assembled Copenhagen quantum mechanics) in a limbo of superposed ‘dead’ and ‘alive’ quantum states until they open the box and observe the result. In the real world the cat continuously interacts with a host of small systems, such as photons and air molecules. Each interaction reduces the coherence, eventually leaving an incoherent mixture at the thermodynamic ratio of probabilities (there is always a small probability that the cat will die of natural causes, rather than cyanide, while in the box). The result is that the cat is in a classical state at all times.

Decoherence by interaction with the environment is effectively instantaneous, since the number of such interactions is astronomically large. In short, the mysterious ‘wave function collapse’ that occurs in the Copenhagen interpretation when an observe records a measurement is replaced by a normal, continuous decoherence interaction with the environment.
