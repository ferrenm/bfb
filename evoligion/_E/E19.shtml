△   Counterfeit/Autofeit   ▽

You can place more faith in me, a tiger, though I feast sometimes upon men; in a serpent, whose sting makes your blood cold the very next moment; or in a rat, which does a thousand mischiefs in your house. But never trust a goldsmith. — Indian folk wisdom, from the days when goldsmiths filled the role that bankers fill today.

The local goldsmith, having an obvious supply of valuables, was often asked for loans, which frequently became a major source of income. In the manner of loan sharks everywhere, he made the rules, and made them profitable. In addition, being a natural target for thieves, he had a strongbox to protect his stock, and did a bit of business protecting his neighbor's valuables also, giving them receipts describing the deposited wealth (often gold coins). His customers then found it convenient to exchange the receipts instead of the objects they represented, just as we find paper money convenient today.

Once such receipts were accepted by the community as being ‘as good as gold’ the goldsmith found that he could issue receipts against his own wealth in exchange for market goods. The receipts might circulate for indefinitely before he was asked to redeem them. He was, in effect, making interest-free loans to himself. (If caught short, he might always claim that a particular receipt was counterfeit.) Then, finding that deposited valuables were never all reclaimed at the same time, he could with little risk, do his own counterfeiting by creating more receipts for uniform items like gold coins, than he had coins in his strongboxes.

This is exactly how banks create money today, although such counterfeiting by an entity legally empowered to create circulating currency is evil (it benefits the counterfeiter and dilutes the value of existing currency). It is also central to Modern Money Mechanics. Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago (1992?) subtitled A Workbook on Bank Reserves and Deposit Expansion. 50 pp. It deserves a name of its own, and the logical choice is autofeiting. One caution: it gets messy when done by every neighborhood bank, so we thought a central bank would be an improvement.

△   Money Creation   ▽

Banking is inherently unstable in that it involves both lending out most of what is borrowed from depositors (and keeping only a fraction on reserve, for example, 10%). —Jennifer Taub J Taub (2011) ‘What We Don’t Talk About When We Talk About Banking’ (February 10, 2011) in G Epstein, MH Wolfson (eds.) Taub's description sounds simple and reasonable. The instability arises because the deposits are inherently short term and the loans long term, and this can lead to banking runs if depositors get nervous. But this is not the real problem. Consider a $100 deposit in 1910, in the form of 10 gold ‘St Gaudens’ $10 eagles. These market price of the gold in these eagles was almost their value as legal ender. The US Mint charges a bit of seigniorage: ‘Seigniorage is earned by selling the coins above the melt value in exchange for guaranteeing the weight of the coin’, says Wikipedia. ⬆︎ The bank reserves one gold piece and loans 9. Doing this 10 times results in $100 on reserve and $900 in loans. This is an honest, if somewhat risky, business: 2 depositors might come in on the same day and ask to withdraw their money. The banker must talk them into settling for half their money today, but he explains that the bank's ‘real’ assets are the $900 (plus interest) he is expecting to be repaid by borrowers, so there is no problem if they will just be patient.
