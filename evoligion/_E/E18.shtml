Earlier we covered the large-scale failure of the Pax Gravettica rather briefly. At 17 ka, when we left the refugia, all was well, although the seeds of future conflict had been sown. Nevertheless, let it be understood that the ‘purpose’ of religion; — its evolutionary contribution — was to keep us from killing eachother. If it cannot do that, it cannot be called religion.

△   1. 1-Gene, 2-Allele   ▽

To farmers and gardeners ‘F1’ does not denote a noisy car race, but a hybrid with some desirable feature (gaudy flower or more profitable crop), whose seeds won't breed true — much to the profit of the seed company! The advantage comes from biochemical diversity, with 2 alleles, and thus 2 proteins, per gene, one from each parent. If the parents are homozygous with, say, hh and HH alleles, the F1 (first filial) generation is uniformly heterozygous, with hH, but the next, or F2 (second filial) generation is mixed, with hh, hH and Hh (which are usually identical) and HH in equal quantities. Here we are concerned primarily with the heterozygotes hH and Hh.

Plant breeders start with parental races that are homozygous for the desired alleles — a luxury not available to human geneticists looking for evidence of heterozygote advantage. Until reading a complete genome is a routine process, demonstrating heterozygote advantage in people will require a large database. Sickle-cell L Cavalli-Sforza, MW Feldman (2003) ‘The application of molecular genetic approaches to the study of human evolution’ Nature Genetics 33: 266–275. is the only confirmed example (although there are a number of other candidates).

△   Sickle-cell   ▽

Sickle-cell alleles produce different forms of hemoglobin. The form built by the wild ‘Wild’ in this usage refers to the most abundant, or normal, allele, because most observed variation from the wild type occurs in domesticated varieties that are protected from natural selection. H allele provides a welcome home for the malaria parasite in red blood cells. A new-born inherits his mother's antibodies against local pathogens. These wear out, and there are a few vulnerable months between their loss and the child's ability to create his own, during which many babies die of malaria. In 5 different regions, evolution independently altered the hemoglobin molecule — one amino-acid change is sufficient — to protect babies from malaria. If half of their hemoglobin molecules are altered (hH alleles), their red blood cells are unsuitable hosts for malarial merozoites, and the babies survive. Unfortunately, if all their hemoglobin is altered (hh alleles), red cells ‘sickle’, becoming mishapen and rigid at low oxygen content, and plug venous capillaries. Plugged areas go anoxic and die, with confusing symptoms, disability and painful childhood death.

24% of children carry one copy of the sickling allele and are protected from malaria: ‘1 allele good’. 2% carry 2 copies of the sickling allele, and die: ‘2 alleles bad’. The logical response to sickle-cell is early detection and abortion of homozygous fetuses. This has no effect other than the prevention of childhood pain and parental anguish — neither of which bothers anti-abortionists as long as it happens to someone else. (The other 74% are homozygous for ordinary, malaria-friendly hemoglobin, and take their chances.) Evolution, lacking parental emotion, cares nothing about the trauma of dying children, but thinks that 24% protected for 2% lost is a net profit. You cannot moralize about this, for it is the same bargain we make with insurance companies, paying a small amount for protection from larger loss. Whenever a deleterious allele persists in the population, it means that heterozygote advantage is at work. The advantage, in terms of differential survival of children, may be invisibly small. Over a few thousand years, a 1% advantage is large enough to spread through a population.

Reviewing Chapter 6a, we can pick out a rough history of attempts to bring reason and religion closer together. The usual suspects in this process tend to be known as philosophers, and deplored as atheists by the churches. Freethinking being just that, there is no coherent doctrine in this process, but once again, I find Thomas Paine's The Age of Reason (1776) to be the most accessible presentation.

One might take this meagre harvest as an indication that Jaynes's hypothesis of conscious introspection replacing bicamerality is too optimistic, and that some of the alternatives of Fig. 2.3 are indeed still with us. 2500 years after the Axial Age, we remain reluctant to abandon the animist overbelief in a virtual invisible world as complex as the tangible one. We seem unable to grow out of infantile dependence on supervision by a loving parent. We elect national leaders who listen to astrologers — and to Dick Cheney masquerading as the voice of god.

△   Failure to Self-Correct   ▲

Childhood's inability to introspect leads directly to epistemology's most toxic failure. As foreshadowed earlier, theologians have never discovered a way to falsify their hypotheses.

Theologians have spent a lot of effort trying to falsify other religions — but only verbally, never experimentally. This is mere polemic, because they have never discovered a way to falsify their own dogma. They have no way to distinguish justified belief from personal opinion.

This well known fact is almost never discussed, but it is very curious. Among the adherents of any religions will be intelligent, practical people, and as Pascal pointed out, what was at stake was infinitely important: the difference between an eternity of painful torment, or glorious fulfillment. One might expect a lot of interest in getting things right here, and there was seldom a time when there was not a heresy or another religion entirely on offer, with incompatible rules of behavior requiring an active choice if salvation were to be achieved.

2500 years, in which the world's smartest minds must have given some thought to the epistemology of dogma — produced neither a solution to the problem nor a record of their questioning, with the exception of a few troubled souls who generally played safe and accepted the mainstream tenets.

Meanwhile, the brightest among the sceptics not only pieced together a more logical version of the story, but — ultimately — discovered that falsification was a reliable test. It might not tell you that something was true, but it could tell you when it was wrong, and this is enough to avoid a lot of dead ends. Ultimately, it told us that we do not need the various gods conjectured by the prophets. There may be something out there more comprehensible to the human mind than inflationary quantum fluctuations, but if so, it has not yet been properly described.
