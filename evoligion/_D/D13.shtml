I conclude that while the concept resonates strongly with people today, it has attracted little academic research. The most respectable ethnological connection is to the Native American ‘Turtle Island’ myth, which is 20,000 years later than the period of interest.

In the absence of the data I was hoping to find, one still can deduce something from this negative result. 500,000 hits means that over the last decade, in an environment of many competing religions and advanced science, one person in 1000 from the North Atlantic community of 500 million people has put up a ‘new–age’ web page containing ‘mother’, ‘earth’, and ‘totem’. During a 5000–year window of opportunity in the Paleolithic, when totems filled the roles of religion and science — and always remembering that these people were as intelligent as we are, lacking only the accumulated data of our libraries — it is not unreasonable to suggest that at least one person in 1000 might have made a similar connection. This brings us to our second evolutionario. It was, quite possibly, our first triumph of unconscious rationality over instinct.

△   id="05"> Evolutionario 2: The 1st Revelation   ▽

In Ch. 10, we hypothesized that the shamans successfully deflected intratribal violence into interracial violence, so that Cro-Magnon tribes could share technological advances. Then:

When the last Neandertals had been dealt with, the tribes flourished. But the bachelors grumbled. Without the excitement of ‘warfare’ they were bored. The youngsters, initiated into the tribe at puberty, felt cheated because, while their fathers still bragged about their ‘wartime’ exploits, they themselves could kill no Neandertals to impress the girls. So they eyed eachother, not in any spirit of malice, but simply as tools in the mating game. They solved their problem as boys will; 40-odd millennia later it would be called a gang fight. The Wooly Rhinos routed the Horses in their first encounter, but suffered severe injuries when a couple of older Horses joined the next fight.

When her great–grandson was wounded in one of these escalating games, the oldest woman in the group, 66 years old, was scolded by her totem. ‘Why do you let your family members harm eachother?’ ‘Horses are no relatives of mine!’, replied Greatgrandmother indignantly. A new voice answered her, ‘But they are. All the totems are my children’ — said with such authority that although the idea was new, there was no alternative but to accept it. ‘Who are you?’, asked Greatgrandmother, at last.

‘I am the Mother of All Totems. I am the bear and the lion and the herds. I am the land. I am the rivers. I am the sky and the clouds and the rain. I am the trees and the fields of grass. I am the earth.

‘And you — you are my children's children. You have better things to do than hurt one another.’

This was the first bicameral revelation. It was not merely convincing, it was Truth itself, allowing no thought of questioning. Greatgrandmother understood that she would be in trouble if she did not explain this to the tribe and get them to do the ‘better things’. What these were she had no idea, but her task was plain. She had to make the tribe understand that you did not harm your family — and that they were all family, whichever totem they belonged to. To the detriment of gang fights, this made the other tribes family too, since they were members of the same totems.

Explaining this new idea to their neighbors (the Gravettians) turned out to be one of the better ideas, and the Gravettians prospered also. And thus was the Pax Gravettica So named because it lasted into the period of Epi-Gravettian dominance. established. For the next 10,000 years, they found that sharing ideas and techniques was more rewarding than killing eachother.

Of course there were lapses, but common sense prevailed. Those who could not keep the peace faced expulsion from the tribe — a death sentence unless another tribe could be persuaded to adopt a person rejected for cause by his own people.

E.O. Wilson EO Wilson (1978) On Human Nature (Harvard University Press, Cambridge MA). suggested that 1000 generations would be enough for a major new trait to evolve. This is much longer than required for selection of an existing trait. The domestication of Siberian foxes LN Trut (1999) ‘Early Canid Domestication: The Farm–Fox Experiment’ American Scientist 87: 160–169. — exactly analogous to our learning to live fearlessly with unrelated humans, as we saw in Table 4.6 — was accomplished in 35 generations of strong selection. Once presented with the challenge, we could have domesticated ourselves in less than 1000 years, Thirty-five 18-year generations come to a paltry 630 years, less than the uncertainty of a radiocarbon measurement for the period. instantaneously on the scale of Fig. 10.1.

The pre–existing mechanism that came into play here was probably trust in parental advice (ie, Conservatism, #5 among the Anomalies of Ch. 7). Once the group consensus accepted that we were all grandsiblings of the same totem — an idea that in later times would be called ‘the brotherhood of man under the fatherhood of god’ — this notion became one of the bicameral rules, and bicameral voices are even harder to disobey than angry parents.

We may take the Mother of All Totems and her bicameral message as the first manifestation of religion, for we now have both a definable deity and a mission of peace-keeping — the first identifiable supra-totemic . Long after Africa; long before the recorded religions — and in whatever manner Evolutionario 2 occurred — we have the essentials. From here it is but a small step to discover the qualities of benevolence, omnipresence, and omniscience that distinguish gods from mere ghosts and spirits. The unconscious bicameral mind cannot be avoided, and it is always with us. When bicameralism is working well, it is there to help us whenever we have a dilemma with an accepted social solution.

Jean Auel, who has probably read and integrated more scientific papers than any of the specialists who wrote them, describes the Pax Gravettica succinctly, without naming it. Nor does she explain how we escaped from the prior chimpanzee "See a stranger, Kill it!" mode that we brought out of Africa and left with the relict tribes along the exodus route.

Her description of ‘the better things’ is in her 6th book, The Land of Painted Caves, p 81 of the paperback editiion:

The world during the Ice Age with its glitering glaciers, transparently clear rivers, thundering waterfalls, and hordes of animals in vast grasslands was dramatically beautiful but brutally harsh, amd the few people who lived then recognized at a fundamental level the necessity of keeping strong affiliations. You helped someone today because you would lkely need help tomorrow. It was why customs, conventions, mores, and traditions had developed that sought to diminish inter-personal hostility, ease resentments, and keep emotions in check. Jeaousy was discouraged, and vengeance dealt with by society, with retribution meted out by the community that would give the injured parties satisfaction and ease their pain or anger, but that would still be fair to all concerned. Selfishness, cheating, and failing to help someone in need were considered crimes, and the society found ways to punish such criminals, but penalties were often subtle and inventive. (Emphasis added.)

The social period she describes is manifestly some centuries after Evolutionario 2, when the ‘better things’ have grown to include bold-face list mentioned by Auel. Recall that midnight assassination of the incorrigibly greedy was included among the traditions of small scale societies studied in the 19th century. Unfortunately, not enough of this was done to remove the relevant genes from all societies.

△   id="07"> From Culture to Genes   ▽

It would be several millennia before we ran out of Neandertals and H. erectus, and by the time we had, the shamans were entrenched as an intellectual power structure parallel to the physical power of the alpha male.

Given the long dependent period of human children, anything which produces a safer childhood environment increases viability. This is co-evolution, in which behavior selects alleles. In an Instinct-1 band females have small opportunity to choose mates. This does not keep them from trying. ‘Stealing copulations’ is the chimpanzee equivalent of human affairs, requiring the same deceptions, coded signals, and trysts. Sexual selection quickly gave us the ability to lie convincingly.

Suppose neighboring tribes of H. sapiens stopped attacking eachother, either by agreement or because they were too busy with Neandertals. This enlarges the gene pool and offers new opportunities for mating. The fraction of offspring sired by the alpha male drops, with selection for pacifism. Bards and shamans — the intellectuals of the day — tend to stay home while the heroes go off to fight. The Bavarian for ‘bastard’ is pastorkind (pastor-child), suggesting a long and lusty tradition of priestly comforting of warriors’ neglected wives.

Obviously we did not breed out Instinct-1 in this process. I suggest only that this period allowed Instinct-2 to become a significant player in the human genome. Earlier, individuals with a double dose of ‘pacifist’ alleles would have been weeded out in childhood. Now they survived to become the first philosophers.

Group cooperation leads to more than cross-fertilization of ideas. It actually speeds the evolution of the brain. It is hard to see this happening in humans, because cultural evolution has masked biological, but we can watch it in orang-utans. Orang-utans are the most solitary of the great apes, with little opportunity for social or copy-cat learning. But in the swamps of Sumatra there is a resource-rich area which attracts orang-utans in what is for them large numbers. As a result, the orang-utans of Suaq Balimbing are the only ones who are known to use tools. The people who • C Van Schaik (2006) ‘Why are some animals so smart?’ Scientific American 294(#4): 64–71.

Another 6000 years, and what once had been a simple ‘grandmother totem’ inspired the people of the Anatolian refugium to build the world's earliest known temple at Göbekli Tepe (‘Potbelly Hill’). This is from the pre-pottery Neolithic, when food ‘production’ was still primarily foraging, although the effort necessary to create the site implies that it drew workers from a radius of scores of kilometers, with

a large, sedentary, well organised hierarchical community, willing to invest in the materialization of its complex immaterial world over many generations and at a considerable cost in energy. — J Peters, K Schmidt (2004) ‘Animals in the symbolic world of Pre-Pottery Neolithic Göbekli Tepe, south-eastern Turkey: a preliminary assessment’ Anthropozoologica 39(#1): 179–218.

Interpretation of the unexpected aspects of this hilltop site must await further discovery and analysis This has not stopped enthusiasts from relating it to the Garden of Eden. (the excavators suggest that exploration will continue for 50 years). The range of animal species carved on the pillars — 81 individuals, at least 13 taxa, not counting a scorpion — and bones found in the filling debris — 20 mammals, 20 birds, 2 fish — is astonishing. The species mix is reminiscent of Æsop's fables; the number of individuals (>38,000) reminds us of how thoroughly we have decimated other species. The absence of humans in the carvings might be totemic; the T-shaped pillars (which presumably once supported a roof) have been described as anthropomorphic, but they could as easily be merely architectural. The dating suggests (to me) that the site was abandoned — it was carefully filled, perhaps with the thought of preserving it against an eventual return — during the Little Ice Age, as its animal species moved to lower, warmer, levels and the people perforce followed, forgetting the site before the climate improved.

△   id="09"> Golden Age   ▽

Many Eurasian cultures describe an earlier era in which people were peaceful, unstressed, healthy, and virtuous: the Graeco-Roman ‘Golden Age’, the Judaic ‘Garden of Eden’, India's ‘Krita Yuga’, and China's ‘Age of Perfect Virtue’. Reich sought the origin of armoring in sexual development, a hypothesis falsified by DeMeo's data. The correlation is real; but causality evidently runs in the opposite direction: armoring warps sexual development. Its physiological basis probably lies in the autonomic nervous system, combining hypertrophy (over-growth) of the sympathetic branch with hypotrophy of the parasympathetic branch. (None of this is under volitional control.) An overabundance of worry while the brain is young and plastic leaves the anxiety system overdeveloped and the pleasure system undernourished. One ends up undervaluing not only his own pleasure, but more importantly, that of other people. Their misery is a social null, not a matter of concern. What matters is that his anxieties be assuaged, by constant reassurance of his importance. Egoistic intangibles such as ‘honor’ loom larger than physical realities like pain and hunger.

This does not seem to be correctible by the experiences of later life, although apparently no one but Reich has wondered about corrective therapy, and his went off on a tangent. Famine kills hippocampal cells, R Sapolsky (2005) ‘Are the Desert People Winning?’ Discovery Magazine 26(#8): 38–41. which provides a mechanism for the irreversibility of the armoring, and suggests that a clinical cure might involve transplanting stem cells into the brain and retraining them — skills that lie in the future. If this is a correct interpretation of armoring, it falls — as expected — athwart the anxiety-schizophrenic region of Fig. 1.11 — but unexpectedly also in the ‘hallucination’ area. Armoring itself has been little studied; its relation to hallucination is terra incognita. For Reich, armoring was a problem of individual psyches. DeMeo finds it an ecotope-scale problem of societies, and its origin in the stress of gradual desiccation. He suggests that:

• Patrism is a novel feature arising from this stress.

• The harsher the environment, the less cultural freedom it allows and the fewer people it can support.

• A previously relaxed matrist culture becomes increasingly repressive and short-term survival-centered as climate worsens.

• Children form food-stealing gangs as the social fabric breaks down. The mother-infant bond survives longest, but eventually starving mothers also abandon their young.

‘Civilized’ communities do the same things under the same stresses: Travelers to Mafiya Russia were warned not to eat duck outside of Moscow — because ‘duck’ was the euphemism for human flesh during WW II. No lasting damage is done if famine is a matter of a single generation. Individuality, emotions, and feelings go by the board. To repeat a generation-old example, consider a lifeboat containing 50 people, a capacity of 60, and 100 more people in the water. The choices for those in the lifeboat are stark:

One. We may be tempted to try to live by the Christian ideal of being ‘our brother's keeper’ … Since the needs of all are the same, we take all the needy into our boat, making a total of 150 in a boat with a capacity of 60. The boat is swamped, and everyone drowns. Complete justice, complete catastrophe.
Two. Since the boat has an unused excess capacity of 10, we admit just 10 more to it. This has the disadvantage of getting rid of the safety factor, for which action we will sooner or later pay dearly. Moreover, which 10 do we let in? ‘First come, first served?’ The best 10? The neediest 10? [The richest 10?] How do we discriminate? And what do we say to the 90 who are excluded?
