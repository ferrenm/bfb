On the basis of observation (not revelation!), Akhnaton drew 2 logical, novel, but evidently very difficult conclusions, given their rarity today:

  • There was no need for a multiplicity of gods (or anthropomorphic gods), since the sun did everything that needed doing;
  • All species must be equally important in the grand scheme of things, since the sun nourished them all equally.

Although he was the most powerful person in his world, surrounded by his own city of people who worshipped what he worshipped, no one understood him. △   Greece   ▽

After the autumn crops were in, Athens annually went off to fight a neighbor, making ‘peaceful’ a relative description. But bear in mind that these wars occupied fewer than 1000 men, that fighting was hand-to-hand, with no surprises from new technologies, and that in general, the better man won. The javelin was the long-distance weapon, and Heracles is chided in drama for ‘cowardly’ use of archery. In Euripides's Heracles, Lycus, king of Thebes, describes Heracles as ‘a man who has won a reputation for valor in his contests with beasts, in all else a weakling; who ne'er buckled shield to arm nor faced the spear, but with a bow, that coward's weapon, was ever ready to run away. Archery is no test of manly bravery; no!’ Such warfare selected for physical prowess, and against clumsiness and stupidity. The smartest of the lot were never killed accidentally, and considering how many famous people served in these wars, they may have selected for intelligence. Short annual wars consistently killed a small percentage of the population, so perhaps (as in Polynesia) the effective function was population control.

While Socrates is the iconic figure of the Axial Age in Greece, he had rather more known help than those in other countries. Pythagoras was a contemporary of Gautama and Confucius (and a spiritual ancestor of Roger Penrose and Max Tegmark), seeing in mathematics the underlying ground of the universe.

△   China   ▽

Confucius and Lao Tse are almost polar versions of the Axial Age explosion of Instinct-2.

Confucius, or Kung Fu-Tzu (551–478 BCE), is probably the best documented and most conservative of Axial thinkers. He claimed no original ideas, presenting his new philosophy as traditional and stressing the importance of tradition, family, conduct, and social relations. His formulation of the Golden Rule was echoed by Hillel: ‘What you do not wish for yourself, do not do to others’. The central text of his teachings is the Analects, probably written by the first generation of disciples. The religious background was animist nature worship under the guidance of Shang-ti (Supreme Lord, also the constellation Ursa Major), whose theodicy included calamities, early death, and punishment of the next generation. The happiness of the souls of departed relatives depended largely on the good behavior of their descendants. There is no indication of bicameral revelation.

Despite a promising Eirenic philosophy, conservative Confucianism as the state religion may have encouraged Chinese stagnation. China invented the precursors of the Industrial Revolution 1000 years before the West — but made the transition a century and a half late. A culture which had sent 500-foot wooden sailing vessels to Madagascar and the New World turned inward and self-absorbed.

Lao Tse is not as well documented as Confucius. His name means Old Sage, and he represents the liberal version of Instinct-2. His major text is the Tao Te Ching, with an individual and mystical emphasis, a Gaian respect for the natural world and scepticism about the joys of Instinct-1.

Taoism and Confucianism are complementary, not contradictory, and classical China never had a problem with being simultaneously Confucist, Taoist, and Buddhist. Despite this auspicious heritage, Instinct-1 rides high in contemporary China. Its Marxist enthusiasm for the exploitation of nature and its rule by technocrats has given it the world's worst environmental behavior among developing countries. With too many people it has no qualms about claiming neighbors’ territory and doing its best to eradicate Tibetan Buddhism. Lao Tse and Confucius would be ashamed.
