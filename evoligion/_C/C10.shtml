Urzeit means ‘primeval time’, and in German theology, the period in which religion was invented. Figure 10.1 extends Fig. 0.4 and frames the major events which contributed to religion. Humanity as we know it from myth and monument was born religious, so we need to look into prehistory if we are to find the origins of religion.

The top of Fig. 10.1 is at 1 Ma, in the Middle Paleolithic of H. erectus. The status quo was Instinct-1, which established the principal hominid method of removing genetic defects: All female alleles are represented in succeeding generations, but not all male alleles. In the Neolithic, Instinct-1 becomes an important negative aspect of religion, but in the early days it was a mixed blessing. It gave us the planet, but it is incompatible with civilization, and it knew nothing about religion. If the people who arrived in Europe had had religion, it seems unlikely that New Guinea could have avoided it. This suggests 40 ka as the terminus post quem for the invention of religion.

The terminus ante quem is poorly constrained, but the Paleolithic figurines are abundant by 20 ka, suggesting that religion was underway earlier. This gives us ~25–40 ka for the Urzeit, which is a sufficient period for pothodoxy to work its way into our DNA. This range is bracketed as the ‘critical period’ in Fig. 10.1, at the beginning of the Ice Age when the Eurasian population was below 10,000. △   The Paleolithic Transition   ▽

This critical period was (I found) also the Middle-to-Upper Paleolithic transition. It has been described as ‘The Great Leap Forward’ by Jared Diamond, as a ‘creative explosion’ by John Pfeiffer, as the result of a mysterious black monolith by Arthur C. Clarke This entry is poetic license. Clarke actually placed the influence of the monolith some 6 million years earlier. Its major effect in the film version of 2001 was to inspire an ancestor to tool use, clubbing a member of another band to death with a heavy femur. Given that we now know that chimpanzees use spears, this seems like an invention that did not require extra-terrestrial assistance. Or perhaps teaching weapons use was the monolith's sole accomplishment. The M/U-P transition is much sharper and more impressive. — and most accessibly, as Jean Auel's heroine ‘Ayla’, in the Earth's Children books, where she singlehandedly introduces most of the features of the Paleolithic Transition. The Transition is usually seen as an improvement in technique, including new stone tool forms, sewing, long-distance exchange of raw materials, and the use of bone, antler, ivory and rare minerals E.g., naturally occurring marcasite (octahedral FeS) for fire-starting. Then it turned out that AMH were present in Africa 100,000 years earlier, without modern behavior. The most astonishing advance in the history of human culture was orphaned of a cause. If the immediate cause of the Transition was neither the speciation of AMH, nor an identifiable modification of the brain, we are left with a change in neural wiring, or, perhaps, nothing more tangible than an idea. However, despite its results, this idea could not have been esoteric, or a philosophical call to higher ethics. It had to have been something down-to-earth that would resonate strongly with the limbic urges of talking, food-cooking chimpanzees, which is about all we can claim to have been as we left Africa.

Resolving this dilemma will take us on a long journey, 4 of whose major events are indicated in the upper white area of Fig. 10.1: Toba, Out of Africa (OOA), Arrival in New Guinea, and Arrival in Europe.

△   The Eruption   ▽

Toba ejected >2800 km3 of dense rock equivalent as ash, the largest explosion in 2 million years, to be compared to 40 km3 from neighboring Tambora in 1815, the most impressive historical eruption, and 0.2 km3 from Mt St Helens. Most ash was carried northwest by the summer monsoon, with 9 m deposited in western Malaysia, 6 m in eastern and 3 m in western India, and lesser amounts in the Middle East and northeast Africa. Jwalapuram, in the middle of the Indian Deccan, has identical Middle-Paleolithic tools below and above the 2.5-m Toba ash layer, showing that India was repopulated quickly. The tools are ambiguous: M Petraglia &13a (2007) ‘Middle Paleolithic assemblages from the Indian subcontinent before and after the Toba super-eruption’ Science 317: 114–116. if we left Africa early, they might be ours; if we left late, they belong to erectus.

△   Population Genetics   ▽

The human race has less genetic diversity than a band of 50 bonobos (Fig. 11.1), but far more phenotypic (racial) diversity. For any given genetic locus, 15% of the total variation is between groups, phenotypically (racially) visible, and 85% is within group. This imbalance led evolutionary biologist Richard Lewontin RC Lewontin (1972) ‘The apportionment of human diversity’ Evolutionary Biol 6: 381–398. to conclude that race was an invalid taxonomic concept lacking biological significance. △   Haplogroups   ▽

I am really sick of these geneticists and their lunacy. They know nothing about archaeology. — ‘ren’ on the Quetzalcoatl anthropology website (posted 2006-12-21).

Despite the success of the Out of Africa hypothesis, there has been just enough gene flow to confuse the issue with the competing Multiregional hypothesis, which holds that that humans arose two million years ago and subsequently evolved as a single intercontinental interbreeding species. The underlying hypothesis is that we saw other H. sapiens groups as friends rather than as competitors, to the extent that intergroup gene flow was consensual and relatively common. 6–9% of the genome of a ~40ka Romanian Qiaomei Fu &15a (2015) ‘An early modern human from Romania with a recent Neanderthal ancestor’ Nature; doi:10.1038/nature14558 was derived from Neanderthals.

Craig Venter's team JC Venter &272a (2001) ‘The sequence of the human genome’ Science 291: 1304–1351. found that ‘all the glorious differences among individuals in our species that can be attributed to genes falls in a mere 0.1% of the sequence’. Recent selection has affected pigmentation, body build, brain size, lactose digestion, and disease resistance. Human family trees are followed by examining DNA that does not recombine in every generations, specifically, mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), inherited from mothers by everyone but not transmitted by males, and NRY, the long non-recombining portion of the Y chromosome, inherited from fathers by sons. In both cases, family groups (‘clans’, or haplogroups) are designated alphabetically. To distinguish them, we prefix the letters with the symbols ♀ for mtDNA and ♂ for NRY. 4 shows the current F Cruciani &5a (2011) ‘A Revised Root for the Human Y Chromosomal Phylogenetic Tree: The Origin of Patrilineal Diversity in Africa’ Amer J Human Genetics 88(#6): 814–818. understanding of the major branches of the haplogroup trees. (Complete trees would end in a thicket of 7 billion leaves, and working trees now require wall charts. Updated versions are available on the Internet from Wikipedia, the Y-Chromosome Consortium and the Genographic Project.)

The mtDNA and NRY of all non-Africans descended from 2 individuals separated by 120 millennia. Specific haplotypes disappear like last names in a closed patronymic community: the Y-chromosome of any man who fathered only daughters vanished, as did the mtDNA of any woman who bore only sons. Our other chromosomes are mixtures of the DNA of the other members of the OOA group.

△   Cultural Universals   ▽

The assertion that descendants of the Common Emigrant did not have religion in the 1930s — which means that we did not have religion when we left Africa — challenges received wisdom. Lowie RH Lowie (1936) Primitive Religion (George Routledge & Sons, London). claims that:

Religion is verily a universal feature of human culture … because all [societies] recognize in some form or other awe-inspiring, extraordinary manifestations of reality … connected with the sense of mystery or weirdness…

There is nothing in Lowie's description with the power to create a genetic need-to-believe. Raising an epiphenomenon to the status of a universal seems to be a common tendency among believers, yet religion is not among a more sober list of nearly 100 cultural universals proposed by Donald Brown. D Brown (1991) Human Universals (Temple University Press, Philadelphia). Furthermore, our concept of religion has moved on. Lowie's ‘awe inspiring’ is too unfocused. △   BMH ≠ BMH   ▽

The designation ‘BMH’ becomes ambiguous at this point. Traditionally, it means Behaviorally Modern Humans — that is, those that made the leap into the Upper Paleolithic. In Fig. 10.1 it means ‘Bicamerally Modern Humans’, as a differentiation from the later CMH, or ‘Conscious Modern Humans’. The problem is that not all bicameral BMHs are necessarily behavioral BMHs. More to the point, we do not know the origin of bicamerality either temporally or spatially. Because it requires a certain level of linguistic development, it is natural to associate it with the increasing linguistic skills of the Upper Paleolithic, as was implicitly done in Fig 2.3. This interpretation implies that bicamerality was carried into Africa by back migration, and that it existed but was unrecorded in many early ethnological studies, so that knowledge of its geographical distribution is spotty, dependent upon indirect recognition. (Neither of these conditions is improbable, but we can wish once again that the concept had been current a century earlier.)

We are resolutely ignoring a third problem, which is that the very hypothesis of bicamerality is accepted only by a minority of workers in the disciplines related to our topic. Many people still consider Jaynes's ideas to be mad, or, more generously, too speculative and their consequences too imponderable to be useful. Still, Ockham's Razor finds bicamerality indispensable in so many ways that it is more parsimonious to insert it long after we left Africa, than it is to explain religion without it.

If we migrated up the South Asian rivers c 55 ka, there are 20,000 years in which to make the conversion from the ‘chimpanzoid’ operating system to bicamerality, and this seems to be the myriennial period in which we have the least information about what our ancestors were up to. Perhaps, since the earliest recognized bicameral cultures are Mesopotamian, we were on the way to developing bicamerality by the time we crossed the Straits of Hormuz and reached the beaches of Persia.

The chain of thought is that today rituals are important among behavioral modern humans, therefore they can be understood as an indication of modern abstract thought. This sort of confusion has persisted throughout history, so there is no reason to doubt either its appeal or its cohesive utility. Irish women on the bus to market cross themselves when they pass a church, unaware that they are doing so. Group ritual differs from superstition by requiring concerted activity, doing something together despite all other differences between us, and thereby minimizing the effect of those differences. △   Pax Gravettica?   ▽

Long before their last stand in Gibraltar, Neandertals were locally extinct elsewhere. Instinct-1 will then have urged us to internecine fights, since nothing better was on offer. The Pax Gravettica could easily have broken down, but the genetic diversity density of Europe says that it did not. We may have exterminated Neandertals, Denisovans, erectus, etc, but we acccepted conspecifics, so it may have been here that we learned not to kill strangers on sight. This is, however, counter to our behavior since we started building walled cities in the Mesolithic. Can we account for this apparently peaceful period?

The first time 2 tribes walked away from a fight to sit around a campfire and listen to why they hadn't killed eachother may have persuaded them that this was a good idea, but it was a pragmatic gambit and not yet a genetic endowment. New Guinea tribes fight their neighbors sequentially in a decadal cycle. Yet even here, individuals try to avoid fights. Strangers search for a common ancestor, so that they won't have to try to kill eachother. ‘We don't have to kill them today’, was a one-time tactical decision, but there must have been individuals early on who were thinking, ‘Why do we have to kill them at all?’ Perhaps a good excuse was all that was needed to start the shift to genetics: the belligerent die young; the pacifists survive.

What we have so far is not yet religion. It is simple racism, devoid of overbelief, but it protects strangers who look right. It is what allows policemen carrying guns to talk to white suspects instead of shooting them on sight, as they so often do to black suspects. It was a giant step forward 35,000 years ago (if not quite adeqate for today's multiracial societies). Completing the shift from racist-protection-of-phenotype-strangers (look-like-me) to religious-protection-of-genotype-strangers (all humans) requires input from other influences at work on our ancestors, which we examine in the next 3 chapters. Protection-of-living-strangers — all life forms — requires post-religious concepts (e.g., Buddhism), but it is the next step. We must not leave the Urzeit hanging while we look at its contents, so we close this chapter with one of the more dramatic events of prehistory.

△   Note: INTERBREEDING   ▲

F  ‘… we can kill THEM’ does not imply a great increase in Neandertal kills, just a decrease in H. sapiens kills. It does not preclude interbreeding, given the human propensity for sex with anything remotely feasible. Genetic evidence supports interbreeding ‘with Neanderthals and Denisovans at least three different times in the past 100,000 years’. ‘The absence of Neanderthal mtDNA sequences in Europe is compatible with at most 120 admixture events between the two populations despite a likely cohabitation time of more than 12,000 years. This extremely low number may imply that ‘the two populations were distinct biological species’ {Curr04} or only minimally interfertile, or it may be that any hybrids growing up among Neandertals were killed as Neandertals, while hybrids among sapiens may have been rejected at birth. Green et al. find 1–4% Neandertal genes in non-African H. sapiens and suggests 4 scenarios to explain them. Their absence of support for Scenario 2, ‘gene flow between late Neandertals and early modern humans in Europe and/or western Asia’, is compatible with Evolutionario 1, as is Scenario 4, ‘old substructure in Africa that persisted from the origin of Neandertals until the ancestors of non-Africans left Africa’. There are a number of sites with possible hybrid skeletons {Rogi54}, {Yaki80}, {Duar99}, but not everyone {Tatt99} is convinced.F
