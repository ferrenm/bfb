Book 1: Its Preludes

⮐   ▼  

STORIES

⩔   ▽  

I grew up with what my professorial father maintained had been ‘the best folklore library west of the Mississippi’. (It had been spread over a hectare of hillside by the half-meter boulders of the La Crescenta check dam in the last hours of 1933, along with rest of the house. Much had been recovered and dried; I read it all, scraping mud from the pages with a table knife as necessary.) By the time I met the Potter's Tale of Genesis, I had enjoyed the creation myths of 100 older cultures, some with more interesting versions of the Old Testament stories. I cannot recommend this approach too highly — the flash flood, the mud, and the knife are incidental, but the stories are essential!) It is hard to take Biblical literalism seriously when you've read the same stories in earlier material!

⩓  

The Baseline

⩔  

If we did not bring religion out of Africa with us, as in Fig. 0.4, what did we have? Preverbal hunters — wolf packs, lion prides, orcas, dolphins, and yes, chimpanzees — manage a considerable amount of cooperative effort with instinct and signals. Still, consensus has it that building boats and crossing 90 km of ocean (at 50 ka, 15,000 years away from Africa) requires some command of language. This much would also support the disembodied instructions of an early bicameral society, 40,000 years earlier than Jaynes thought it happened. The first voices heard would be known speakers: recently deceased relatives (with homely advice and loving concern), and the tribal alpha male (demanding unquestioning obedience to his rules).

Koko (the language-competent gorilla) is as interested in gossip as any devotee of TV soaps and their cardboard characters. But when asked an existential question, ‘Where do people [ie, gorillas] go when they die?’, she replied matter-of-factly, ‘In a hole in the ground’°. This was apparently an idea she picked up from TV, since burial has not been observed in wild gorillas. This may be our best insight into the philosophical interests of our chimpanzoid-OS during the whole of the Middle Paleolithic. The God Conjecture

⩔  

The lack of demonstrable religion in 20th-century New Guinea indicates that the resources of Table 4.1 do not yet add up to religion — whose defining characteristic is the idea that it is in our own interest not to kill other people. Religion depends in detail upon the stories that we tell, which customarily involve gods and a creation myth that together:

  • reflect the curiosity and sophistication of the culture,
  • provide satisfying answers to its existential questions.

Contemporary New Guinea folktales° TH Sloan (2001) One Thousand One Papua New Guinean Nights 9 . The first 50 that I read were pointless, random, illogical, unimaginative, and just plain dull. If, for lack of contradictory evidence, we assume that this applies a fortiori to the mindset of the strandlopers of the African exodus, then something fundamental happenene Papua New Guinean Nights, 2 vols. (Masalai Press, Oakland CA). do not encourage one to believe that the language and logic of 50 ka could produce the gods or creation myths that we associate with early red somewhere else, which became so important to us that it branched into 100,000 varieties.

Every other analysis I have found quietly overlooks the ‘something fundamental’ and the ‘somewhere else’, so we too will leave them for Chapters 9 and 13. The basic concept of the 100,000 varieties is the idea of god — but even today the idea is not a:

  • theory (supported by publicly verifiable data), nor a
  • hypothesis (with a way to falsify it), but a
  • conjecture (a speculation based on incomplete evidence).

While is true that some famous conjectures have been proven true long after they were proposed, the longevity of this one is matched only by its dearth of support°. It would be fascinating to watch the world's monotheists attempting to determine which god had been detected, if we found one!

We might frame the contemporary god conjecture as:

There is a powerful being worthy of obedience and worship°: The behavior of some gods in the scriptures of their followers makes even this minimal response impossible for an ethical person. our creator, who made and fine-tuned the universe for us, and revealed instructions for our behavior through prophets.

This works for unsophisticated early societies, Islam, and Deism. Theists, finding such gods emotionally unfulfilling, added:

He possesses some characteristics of a person, cares about us, sometimes answers our prayers, and produces occasional miracles.

This is a general version independent of sectarian nuance. To see why this conjecture became so important to us, we must examine what happened in its absence.

Cosmos: Versions 1, 2, & 3
⩔  

Our earliest creation myths have oral roots in the 8000 BP Neolithic. They attempt to make sense of the world of Fig. 4.1, common to Aryan and Semitic world views. The exterior waters are ‘the waters of the abyss’, and — peculiar to Genesis — the ‘waters above the firmament’, added to explain Noah's Flood. While there is serious confusion about waters, ‘windows’ and astronomical objects, the conception integrates many facts as then known into a comprehensible if physically implausible whole.

The New Testament cosmos is already dramatically different (Fig. 4.2 Left), and by 1644, the Copernican Cosmos had been recognized (Fig. 4.2 Right). With keen insight, Pope Alexander VII saw that it was incompatible with Genesis — so he condemned it: ‘My favorite tall tale does not describe the real world: Therefore, the world is wrong.’

This pattern has continued, with the cosmos increasing in complexity, and the explanation holding fast. Comparative mythologist Joseph Campbell explains what happens when knowledge outpaces myth; when the answers to existential questions no longer match our awareness of the cosmos:

Your mythology, your imagery, has to keep up with what you know of the universe, because what it has to do is put you in accord with the universe as known, not as it was known in 2000 BCE [6000?] in the Near East. — J Campbell (1990)° J Campbell (1990) Transformations of Myth Through Time (Harper & Row, NYC): 22.

Today's observational cosmology presents a particularly difficult challenge to conservatives, because it changes after every every new satellite telescope. The simplest resolution is that astronomers are unreliable and can be ignored in favor of the unchanging word of god. Explaining this remarkable failure to keep up, Aleister Crowley°A Crowley (1953) ‘The Gospel According To Saint Bernard Shaw’ In Francis King (ed.) Crowley on Christ (C.W. Daniel, London, 1974). ungraciously observed,

It never occurred to religious writers … to try to guard themselves against any rational criticism. The thing practically did not exist; and to this day the vast majority of Christians are absolutely incapable of understanding any such arguments, which they regard as mere blasphemy.

Gender Bias in the Tall Tales
⩔  

A serious and continuous obstacle in the study of ancient societies is the indolent assumption that they must have resembled our own. — Marija Gimbutas° (1991) The Civilization of the Goddess (HarperSanFrancisco): 324.

The first creation story in Genesis 1–2:3 breaks off in the middle, perhaps because it was gender neutral: ‘in the image of God [Elohim, plural] created he humans; male and female created he them‘. The 2nd, more complete story, is male chauvinism in full cry, and with a different deity: ‘the LORD God [YHVH] formed man from the dust of the ground’ and as an afterthought, ‘the rib, which the LORD God had taken from man, made he a woman’. Different gods, different stories, different gender status. The earliest written creation story we have is the Enuma Elish of Babylon, a straightforward tale of a god, Marduk, demonizing and beating up a goddess, Tiamat, which sets the stage for the subjugation of women from that day on. Remnants of this pattern of a matrist or gender-egalitarian culture being subjugated by patriarchal invaders are known around the world. Many males pretend this pattern is a recent feminist invention, but it is not.

ONE STORY

⩔   ▲  

Given 100,000 tries, we might expect to get the story right. Unfortunately, we did not, as is obvious from the development of the narrative. Each story is sui generis, claiming to be uniquely correct and the others wrong. This is not the way truth is arrived at in other fields. Those who lack the credulity allele have therefore pieced together a different explanation of the world, whose narrative developed naturally. Independently discovered pieces hang together, forming a coherent (if incomplete) tale. A discordant bit will either be refined, or alter the existing text, until it fits. It appears that there will ultimately be a single consilient story that describes all aspects of the cosmos.

This story accepts the changing understanding of the cosmos, and subsumes all manner of details from other disciplines — but it does not tell us we are important, and it does not promise ‘happily ever after’, so many see no point to it at all — although it did give us airplanes and iPhones.

Unsurprisingly, both explanations depend upon invisible agents. Both sorts are held to be important: traditional agents bring messages and adjust events; non-traditional agents govern everything from electric toothbrushes to the Earth's orbit, and they include 96% of the mass-energy of the universe. The difference is that traditional agents are anthropomorphic, with intelligence, intention, and concern for us. The non-traditional agents obey simple rules (equations), and totally ignore us. Agents can be sometimes be visualized: Archangel Gabriel is said to appear to certain people (but not to others° Thus Aisha, Mohammed's child-wife, upon being introduced to Gabriel by Mohammed, replied, ‘You see what I don't see’ {Bukhari 4:54:440}. who are present); magnetic fields can be visualized with iron filings, but not necessarily understood. The unconcern of the non-traditional agents means that on every line in Table 4.4 the traditional explanation will satisfy more people. Having been in a popularity contest for 12,000 years, the survivors are the stories that make us feel best.

Elegance
⩔  

The traditional explanations offer 100,000 different rules. Advocates of some rules are so convinced and caring that they will kill you for your own good° Especially if you have money or property they can then get their hands on. rather than let you follow a wrong tradition. There were days when 50,000 Europeans were slaughtered ‘to save them’. The non-traditional explanation avoids this problem with a single story that promises nothing. Nor does it kill you°. Many will dispute this, pointing to nuclear weapons, Frankenfoods, biocides, water boarding, and other scientific discoveries as very efficient ways of killing. So are stones. The problem is not discovery, but the Neolithic mind-set that wants to use them intentionally, either to kill The Other, or in aid of making a profit. It doesn't talk about purpose and ultimate causes or ask ‘Why?’, but inquires about mechanism and proximate causes, asking ‘How come?’. How did the world come to be the way it is? Here we can find answers. They are composite, forming a story of many chapters, and the chapters aim at the subtle concept of elegance.

Table 4.5 attempts to dissect ‘elegance’. Like beauty, elegance is in the eye of the beholder. Given two explanations of the same puzzle, smart money bets on the more elegant as understood by the people who use it. Cosmological models, describing the first moments of creation in mathematical terms, are at the cutting edge of physics. Assumptions introduced into models for elegance — ‘simplicity, expediency and aesthetics’° F Wilczek (2003) ‘Analysis and Synthesis III: Cosmic Groundwork’ Physics Today 56(#10): 10–11. — produce good descriptions, and become components of better theories. Some call this the ‘unreasonable power of mathematics’. We don't know why it works, yet it may be our best pointer to the fundamental nature of the universe.

An important guide to elegance is Ockham's Razor, freely translated: ‘Other things being equal, the simplest explanation is best’. Yet there is a danger. The simplest explanation of anything is ‘a god did it’. Insurance companies don't pay for ‘acts of god’, so ‘god’ is economically useful. But this is an ad hoc explanation that is not part of a chain of natural cause and effect. Despite the simplicity and universality of ‘a god did it’, ‘supernatural’ does not qualify as an elegant explanation.

The last 2 entries in Table 4.5 are the acid test. It may seem contrary, but if you don't try to make an explanation fail quantitatively, you can never trust it, and if you can't change it when it is wrong, it explains nothing.

There is currently a debate about the meaning of elegance and the role of models. The conventional (often, ‘Aristotelian’) view is summed up by an engineer:

Science is all about models of the real world, whether natural (basic science) or manmade (applied science, or technology). These models are not discovered in nature, for nature has no numbers, no coordinate systems, no parameters, no equations, no logic, no predictions, neither linearity nor non-linearity, nor many of the other attributes of science. Models are man's creations, written in the languages of science: natural language, logic, and mathematics. — Jeff Glassman° (2007) ‘Conjecture, hypothesis, theory, law’ Crossfit #64; 4 pp.

The alternative (‘Platonic’) view, suggested by the front end papers, championed in detail° • E Fredkin (1990) ‘Digital Mechanics’ Physica D: 254-270.

Abstract or running on a computer moment by moment, differential or discrete, probabilistic or deterministic — there is some reason that we can describe the universe mathematically. We have an immensely challenging philosophical problem to explain: Why does mathematics work? Few have attempted this, and no one has offered a better suggestion than Plato's: the Ideal World — of ‘numbers, coordinate systems, parameters, equations, and logic’ — is in fact the basic ground of the universe.

Questions to be Answered
⩔  

The questions which are supposed to be answered by the 100,000 stories are ‘existential’. Traditionally, these include:

Where did we come from?

Why are we here?

Where are we going?

We do not like to feel accidental, useless, or directionless, and much prefer answers that flatter us. This is the role of myth, and nihilism was the response when the Enlightenment began to take our myths away. Nihilism was an unimaginative response, but it evidently takes a resolute spirit to understand that we had always made those supportive myths ourselves. The thing to do is roll up our sleeves and make more useful myths. Some might call this ‘forging Scripture’, but then, most ‘valuable antique furniture’ is modern forgery, the originals having long ago succumbed to wood worm and dry rot. Its real value lies in successful design and careful workmanship, not age.

Coyotl's answer to the 2nd existential question is that he wants to see what will happen next. Most people find such free choice unsettling, for it means that the 3rd answer is up to us: Coyotl hasn't a clue. This is more responsibility than most people want — but it has always been true. Yet we have some good data which can help us decide where we want to go. Take a hard look at the last 200 years (and the last 5) and see where people go when they have a choice, or their own culture is inadequate. Immigration being the sincerest form of flattery, historically, many headed straight for the one nation intentionally designed on Enlightenment principles. This should tell us something important. Today, refugees and the culturally superfluous° Does this sound callous? If so, find a better term to describe that fraction of the population that its culture cannot supply with useful, comfortable, and satisfying lives. head for the West, or in desperation blame it for their plight.

Physical destination is obviously not what the existential question is intended to ask. But the complete lack of a verifiable answer to its philosophical intent, and the suggestive answer to its physical reality, might be taken as a useful clue.

The traditional questions do not exhaust the stock. Perhaps the most important existential question is ‘Where should we be going?’ We will offer some suggestions in the final chapters, and it will prove impossible to disentangle the physical and philosophical answers.

Learning to Live Together
⩔  

We conclude the discussion of stories by comparing 2 processes belonging to unrelated fields, that gave us answers to dimly recognized but important problems. The common features are that the problems were unconscious and resolved long ago, the results were unanticipated, the time required was long by human standards but instantaneous paleontologically, and the mechanisms were pragmatic selection. Both processes can be characterized as groups learning to live together.

⩓  
Quotidian assistance
⩔  

The more recent need was simple: to move beyond hand-to-mouth survival, we needed help with daily living, so we recruited suitable animals — and no civilization has arisen without exploiting them. We owe a deep and irrefragable ethical debt to the animal kingdom. We needed them once, in all their variety — and we will need them again. This is a debt that civilization owes — and if our overarching goal is implementing social justice, it is a debt that we must repay to realize our true potential. Repayment is simple: this is their world too, and we must not monopolize it to their detriment. Fail in this — as we seem intent upon doing — and we fail in our entire purpose.

Leaving our 160,000-year rut

▲  

The earlier need was also simple. Our predecessor, Homo erectus, spent 2 million years as a chimpanzee who cooked his food. We remained in his Middle Paleolithic rut for another 160,000 years — with our social horizon limited to ‘See a stranger; kill it’ — and we needed a way to move on. We didn't know what we were looking for. The immediate need was only to avoid a fight one day, but the solution we found triggered the exponential growth that distinguishes us from other animals and (briefly) got us to the moon.

The last 5000 years have taught us that our universe is not one in which social justice appears automatically. The fact that we have discovered the concept is encouraging: as far as we know, this is a unique discovery. (No other planet in our galaxy has done better, or we would know about them.) The question remains of how to discharge our responsibility. Table 4.6 may offer a clue.

For all of the similarities of process in the way we answered our 2 needs, the results are perceived to lie in unrelated fields. It is rare to find a suggestion that animal husbandry and human spirituality have anything in common; this oversight may be explained by the overwhelming differences between the processes outside the similarities of the table.

Selection for mutual benefit was rare (cats being the prime example); the selection for credulity seems general. The credulity allele — for better or worse — facilitated belief in authoritative dicta without requiring supporting evidence. Waiting for mutation to produce a favorable allele is a much longer process than the 35 generations for selection of existing alleles. Species such as zebras and Scottish wild cats, lacking the necessary alleles, have never been domesticated. The implication is that we killed off the trusting among them, permanently destroying their ‘trusting’ allele.

A principal difference between the processes is the directionality of the result. Domestication results in animals regarding us as parents — patently in the case of household pets, rather dimly for farm animals°. The best example I can offer is a feral lamb I ran down in a side canyon of Utah‘s Green River on a kayak/Zodiac/inner-tube trip. My raftmates had intended to eat him. When I set him down after the terror of the chase, the first thing he did was butt my leg in the hope that this would produce milk. Still, this befuddled gesture won the hearts of the grad students and saved his life, for they thereafter saw him as cuddly rather than edible. Evidently there is bidirectionality involved here.
Domestication was almost certainly an inadvertent result of the maternal instinct, beginning with a foolish soft-hearted woman rescuing the offspring of the tribe's dinner. Religion, at its best, leads to a recognition of the brotherhood of man under the fatherhood of god (or clumsier gender-free circumlocutions). There is further divergence: People are seldom willing to die for their domestic animals, but, oddly, are able to identify so deeply with their stories that they are sometimes willing to die for them. Like altruism — in which (contrary to naïve theory) one sacrifices resources for the benefit of another's genes — this challenge to our current understanding of evolution and selection makes the function of religion a matter for research.

Incomprehensibly, people are also willing to kill for their stories. This makes religion, like murder, a matter for civil investigation and regulation. Most murders are crimes of passion or greed, driven by irrational but well understood motives with a solid theoretical foundation. Murder on behalf of a story is irrational to the point of insanity.

Religion was ‘for’ helping us live together. This raises 2 questions:
• By the middle of the 13th century, Thomas Aquinas had seen that as society and the problems of living together changed, religion must welcome and adapt to new discoveries. Why is this so difficult for us today?
• Killing people does not ‘help us live together’. Consequently, any story that advocates killing cannot be a part of religion. Why is this not obvious?
