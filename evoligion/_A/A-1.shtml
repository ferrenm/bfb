THE GOAL

▽   ▽

Life is a learning experience, both for the gods, and for people made in their image. The gods establish the laws that will govern their universe, set the initial conditions°, Despite the ~2e19 (short for ~2·1019, or approximately 20 billion billion) terrestrial-type planets in the visible universe, a computer simulation incorporating the laws of physics, the evolution of the universe, and data on exoplanets from the Kepler Space Telescope, produced only a single planet with the right size, gravity, temperature, composition, cosmic-ray-, solar- and particle-irradiation, rotation rate, and age. There are many suitable planets, but their mean age is only 1.7 Gyr, too young for intelligent life. (This result is for the visible universe, for we see younger epochs the farther away we look because of the finite speed of light. (This is not the coeval universe of the Big Bang, which we cannot see, and in which intelligent life should be common even if short lived.)

Still, this is not encouraging. We used the easily accessible fossil carbon to build the modern world. When it collapses — as have all its predecessors — we are unlikely to be able to repeat this process.

If there were intelligent life on Earth, this would inspire us to reconsidered our priorities.

THE FIRST 14 ÆONS

⩔  

Once upon a time, there were 3 student goddesses, and a canid. They did the best they could. Over the course of nearly 14 billion years, their universe developed stars, metals, life, and apes that used tools and fire. Nothing much happened for the next 160,000 years. Then one day in the Middle Paleolithic, Peira found a receptive tribal shaman and whispered in his ear. What she suggested worked for about 5 millennia. Then Eirene saw an opportunity to work with the tribal great-great-grandmother, and within 2500 years we had made the Watershed Leap into the Upper Paleolithic. All then went well for 100 centuries, until some of the physical laws combined to return the world to a half myriennium of cold that drove us into isolated refugia. Each group assumed Eirene's panacea was for itself alone.

After the cold departed, and groups began to meet once more, we were back to the behavior of the Middle Paleolithic. The Leap gave us tools — and weapons, and when the cold turned to drought, we used them on eachother.

Emphante thought it might help if she sent Coyotl — Trickster and Shapeshifter though he was — to explain the role of the gods, and although they tried numerous approaches, it was always a bit like talking to puppies. Not all of the message is understood. What came through, too often, was dysfunctional distraction and abiding confusion, leading to the 5000-year great recurring muddle that we call history. It was only when Emphante and Peira learned to work together that we began to see our way out of this. We found that not everything we can imagine is true and that there are usually ways of determining which is which. What we should do with that information is use it to help the students reach their goal. What we will do with it remains to be seen.

©?

  ▽

Every time I have written to a publisher for permission to use copyrighted material, one of 3 things has happened. Either I receive no answer, or I am run around Robin Hood's Barn by publishers who sold the rights on, in an apparently endless chain, or I am presented with premature questions like how many copies, in what binding, for what audience, at what price.

As an academic, I have always felt that classroom use of such material came under Fair Use, and scholars always seem happy to have their work so used. The Digital Millennium Copyright Act is terra incognita to me. Nobody will make money from this work-in-progress, and if I have overstepped I apologize. Leave a note on the blog, and we can negotiate.

As Tom Lehrer famously put it (and assuming that one is allowed to quote him!):

Let no one else's work evade your eyes!
Plagiarize! Plagiarize! Plagiarize!
But please, always to call it ‘research’.

With respect to copyrighted material like McLagen's wonderful ‘Drenched Cat’, I believe that at least as long as my material is non-commercial and for comment only, display of such images falls under fair use, for (as Wikipedia Commons puts it), it does not significantly impede the right of the copyright holder to sell the copyrighted material, it is low quality, is not being used to generate profit in this context, and presents ideas that cannot otherwise be exhibited. (In any case, sporadic attempts to contact McLagen over a 10-year period — including the tourist shop in Amsterdam where I first saw the Cat, the publisher of the post card, McLagen's newspaper in South Africa, etc. — have all failed.)

Suggestion: Those to whom copyright is important might create a searchable on-line database/tutorial with examples of what is and is not fair use, addresses of copyright holders, and sample request letters.

Editorial Quirks

It will undoubtedly offend purists that when British, US, and, say, norsk usages disagree, I often pick the the simplest, or most phonetic, version — single quotes and ‘burocrat’ being obvious examples. I also prefer eachother for the reciprocal pronoun, by analogy with another, and numerals instead of written-out names for small numbers (except at the beginning of sentences) — the latter as part of familiarizing the innumerate with the idea of number. ROAD SIGNS

  ▲

Donald Knuth, programmer extraordinaire, promulgated the use of marginal ‘road signs’ — cortical emoticons — to warn readers of what was coming and to help relocate particular material. The set we use is:

  • Take-home: Remember this, and act on it.
  • New idea, or at least one that I can't blame on anybody else.
  • Needed research: Why hasn't this topic been studied and publicized? There are PhD theses lurking here.
  • Arithmetic or jargon: Skip and ignore if you don't find this useful.
  • Soap box: Rant, but in a good cause.
  • Theater: Plausible, highly probable fiction here; enjoy.
  • Scholarly material. Possibly boring, but I have done my homework.
  • Enlightenment: essential ideas from 1750 or earlier (the icon is Thomas Jefferson).
  • Bell-the-cat: Good idea with a snowball's chance in hell of implementation.
  • Stop this! Write Congress, join quangos, do whatever it takes, but get this stopped!

Book 1: Its Preludes

and incidentally, the start of a 24-minute preview. Follow the right-pointing finger below.

Chapter 0, the Preface, introduces the things we need to get started. These include ideas (theories of religion and a strange definition thereof, a timeline, synthetic kinship, pothodoxy, metagods, some odd characters (Coyotl, Eirene, Gaia), and a pair of instincts.

Chapter 1, The Brain, observes that to the extent that religious behavior affects the world, the human brain is the instrument responsible for the effects, so we review what little we know about the brain. It has not always worked as it works today, and its ‘operating system’ (OS) has changed as we have changed. Anthropologists and one eccentric psychologist suggest that it worked rather differently in the days when we invented gods and religions. Then (when we needed them) the gods were unavoidable. There was no question about their reality and utility, nor need for arguments for their existence. When we no longer needed them, they left us, replaced by endless debates, the uncertainty of incomplete knowledge, and the counterfactual contortions of faith.

Chapter 2, Bicamerality, introduces Julian Jaynes's hypothesis of the bicameral mind, an earlier developmental organization of the brain, an OS that explains hypnosis, schizophrenia, ghosts, belief in an afterlife, angels, gods, and a great deal of ethology. The chapter cannot replace reading Jaynes's book, but it should provide sufficient background for our needs. The disappearance of the gods was simply a step in the evolution of the brain, as it develops different operating systems in the process of learning to use itself.

Those who receive revelations are convinced that they come from outside the human brain. Considering the fervor with which we act on revealed doctrine, the question of its source would seem to be the single most important question in human history. Chapter 3, Revelation examines a 28-year experiment at Princeton not concerned with revelation, but with a more accessible aspect of supernaturalism, concluding that ESP/Psi is both insignificant (<0.03% effect), and unreliable (the direction of the effect is sometimes opposite to intent). Evidence from evolutionary biology, communication theory, the Rosicrucians, and the Mormons suggests that revelation has a prosaic and understandable source inside the brain. Has any revelation included facts not previously known to the prophet?

First, there is language, then there are stories, and Chapter 4, Stories, concentrates on these, and what they did for us. Living together in larger groups than the extended family was once impossible, and is still not easy: witness intolerance, terrorism, torture, crime, corruption, warfare, deliberate gross inequality, and governments that believe they must lie to the governed. Clearly, we need something to help us live together. The earliest tools that filled this need were racism and religion — which is why they are still with us.

The objective of Religion 1 / Book A is to rethink some axioms. Since everyone brings his own idiosyncratic axioms to a discussion of religion, it is pointless to exemplify ‘some’. Instead, these are the working hypotheses that replace the axioms:

  • The purpose of religion is not salvation.
  • The developing brain writes its own operating system.
  • Meditation may not take us where we always thought it did.
  • Revelation is psychology, misunderstood as theology.
  • Religion is best understood as a variety of domestication.
