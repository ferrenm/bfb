Book 1: Its Preludes

THE COMMITTEE OF TWO

 ⩔   ▽  

[T]he gods were … a mere side effect of language evolution and the most remarkable feature of the evolution of life since the development of Homo sapiens himself. — Princeton psychologist Julian Jaynes.

The epigraph is possibly the most disruptive sentence ever written. As philosophical insights go, it encapsulates ‘the single most comprehensive, wide-ranging, imaginative[, and explanatory] hypothesis available’. Bob Meyers (2006) ‘Julian Jaynes: Gods and voices’ Numenware 2006-01-15. It gives fair warning of heavy going and strange ideas. Jaynes's ‘bicameral hypothesis’ — which he himself called ‘preposterous’ {84} — appeared to psychologists like Athena stepping full grown from Zeus's skull. They were unprepared for this irruption and did not take kindly to it. Most preferred to ignore it, then and now. Most people, not wanting to believe it, will find ways to reject it: As the (recommended) Wikipedia article says, ‘The hypothesis is generally not considered of practical importance by mainstream psychologists’.

The hypothesis was elaborated in the most important book of the 20th century, Julian Jaynes's The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind. A title like that will scare off many readers, and what he had to say was so difficult to swallow that the consensus among those who most needed to understand his insights was, ‘If we ignore him sufficiently, maybe he will go away’.

An analysis 30 years later ‘adresses two main critical issues: the neurological basis of the bicameral model, and the philological accuracy of Jaynes's arguments’. Brutally compressed, these arguments come down to this:

  1. ‘[F]rom an evolutionary perspective, it seems very unlikely that … a dramatic remodelling of extensive neural networks could have come about in the space of three millennia or so’.
  2. ‘[C]onsidered as a whole, the narration of the Iliad is not always consistent with [Jaynes's] thesis.’

To these charges I would respond:

  1. The change was not evolutionary but developmental; thus societal rather than molecular. A change in childhood environment is sufficient to initiate the transition. There are still many people who are partially bicameral.
  2. A sentence away from this complaint one finds: ‘The Iliad is a collection of oral poetry composed by a millenary succession of “aoidoi” or bards from different traditions, and then assembled around 700 B.C.’ — suggesting that one should not anticipate consistency from a collection assembled over the period of the ‘breakdown’.

Furthermore, we now know that there were intimations of a need for a bicameral explanation, never fully articulated, at the turn of the last century. If we start with these, they may make bicameral theory more accessible. After all, what Jaynes proposed was a Committee of Two, an Executive and a Worker, corresponding, perhaps, to the 2 hemispheres of the brain. Freud had founded a whole profession on a Committee of Three: Superego, Ego, and Id (a repackaging of Plato’s Spiritual, Rational, and Appetitive, aspects of the soul) which might in some way correspond to the 3 layers of the brain. In later chapters we will suggest that bicamerality was an early operating system of the wetware, so that a short analog of Jaynes's hypothesis is:

Bronze-age bicamerality : Axial-age introspection :: 1978-Apple DOS : 2018-MacOS Mojave.

At this point it is useful to remember that mind-sets die hard. Psychiatry had enthusiastically trotted down Freud's garden path without noticing that Freud admitted that his ideas depended upon the validity of Lamarckian inheritance (and were thereby falsified). Forty years after Bell & Weinberg, (1978) Homosexualities: A Study of Diversity Among Men and Women (Solomon & Schuster, NYC). the profession still allows its members to charge for doomed attempts to change people's sexual preference: this is malpractice by any standard. So too with the mind set of religion: contradictory dicta can't all be right, but the usual way we address differences is to talk past eachother, or throw rocks. Jaynes offers a civilized compromise, in which each religion can claim some measure of validity. One is entitled to feel that conscious, rational, beings would sieze such an opportunity to move forward.

The gods that Jaynes deals with are revelatory gods who instruct individuals. He is silent about other sorts, such as deist gods who set the universe in motion and do not thereafter tinker with it. We in the US are fortunate in having based our national theology Eugene Rostow, the dean of Yale Law School introduced the term ‘ceremonial deism’ in 1963 to refer to practices that are ‘so conventional and uncontroversial as to be constitutional’. on the deism of the Enlightenment. To the extent that this remains true, Jaynes's disruptive hypothesis should leave us unperturbed.

Consciousness

⩔  

While our concern is the nature of the gods, rather than the nature of consciousness, the topics are so interwoven that we need to clarify what we mean by ‘consciousness’. Even though it is an evolutionarily recent addition to the human experience, it is so interesting and elusive that it has been analyzed from many angles, acquiring thereby a confusing overlay of properties and consequent descriptions. The upshot, I suggest, is that the ancient problem of consciousness has been polymuddled:

  1. by a multiplicity of definitions, and
  2. by biting off more than it needs to chew.
⩓  

1) The Multiplicity

 ⩔  

Fools rush in where angels fear to tread. — ‘Charge!’ cried Becula.

What is ‘consciousness’? We possess it, rocks do not, and we debate the dividing line. Some will claim that rocks have the rudiments; others, that the unexamined survival-oriented life of most humans is unworthy of the term. There is a host of words that get confounded with consciousness, so for the sake of clarity, I suggest the sequence below, which constrains ‘consciousness’ to a specific process, perhaps still mysterious, but precisely defined. This is important, because once we blur the definition —

… once we acknowledge that consciousness comes in very many forms, we have to allow a role for the generation of these various types of consciousness by [the aggregation of] items that lack the types [of consciousness] in question. — Frank Jackson. (2006) ‘Galen Strawson on panpsychism’ in G Strawson et al, edited by A Freeman Consciousness and its place in nature (Imprint Academic, Exeter UK): 62–64.

and this can only compound the confusion. Nine items in this list are specifically distinct from consciousness, and the fewer of them we talk about at once, the clearer our discussion becomes. Note as you read that the italicized components seem essential to their definitions.

  1. Reactivity: Even rocks react to their environment, by metamorphism, erosion, dissolution, etc.
  2. Awareness: Perception of external events through specialized sensors. (Plants have sensors aware of gravity, sunlight, temperature, humidity, CO2, trace nutrients, insect attack, signal chemicals, and sometimes touch.)
  3. Attention: The focussing of sense perception; this is an automatic response to threats to survival. Root-connected trees spread an alarm after insect attack on one.
  4. Intentionality: Will, desire; the ability to have thoughts about something — even at the level of DNA. Ivy ‘wants’ to become arborescent by climbing trees and smothering them.
  5. Intelligence: Independent of consciousness, but requiring a modifiable structure to retain learning. (DNA does this too slowly to qualify except in an evolutionary sense.)
  6. Subjectivity: Sufficient individuality to be influenced by personal feelings, tastes, or opinions. Some cats like peanut butter, others corn-on-the-cob. Some cats swim in the ocean.
  7. Sentience: Subjective perceptual experience; response to qualia. (Purring cat). Purring is expensive. A cat who purrs for no reason is announcing ‘Free meal here!’ to any passing predator. Purring is a social signal, largely to kittens and humans: ‘I judge that we are safe here. Curl up and nap.’
  8. Self-awareness: Passing the mirror test, which normally divides into 4 levels:
    • social responses (aggressive display)
    • physical inspection (looking behind the mirror)
    • repetitive mirror-testing behavior
    • realization of seeing themselves
    Tests with animals are mixed. Some African Grey parrots, great apes, ants (!) and cetaceans apparently pass; cats, dogs, jackdaws and most primates seem to fail.
  9. Consciousness: The ability to introspect, retrospect, and imagine.
  10. Sapience: Wisdom, a rare and valued quality in which information is tempered with insight.

At this point, it may seem that we have defined away most of the mental properties that have been associated with consciousness. Therefore I let Jaynes, speaking to an audience of professional psychologist, describe what remains (a decade after his book):

Subjective conscious mind is an analog of what we call the real world. It is built up with a vocabulary or lexical field whose terms are all metaphors or analogs of behavior in the physical world. Its reality is of the same order as mathematics. It allows us to short-cut behavioral processes and arrive at more adequate decisions. Like mathematics, it is an operator rather than a thing or a repository. And it is intimately bound with volition and decision. — Jaynes (1986) {146}

Before we continue, let us pause for a moment beside a window in a zoo in Holland. On the other side of the glass is a warm and humid bit of the midlevel of a South American cloud forest: trees, vines, sloths, marmosets, algae. The algae recognizes tree bark and sloth fur as suitable moist and stable habitat. The sloths and marmosets are not aware of eachother. The marmosets' internal clocks run 300 times faster than the sloths'. Marmosets need to eat more or less constantly; sloths, once a month. Marmosets run along sloths the way they run along branches. The sloths, moving at breakneck speeds compared to the branches, don't notice.

The worlds that sloths and marmosets are aware of — their internal representations of the environment — are very different, and neither of them resembles our version. Yet none of these is incorrect: each abstracts the important bits and presents a working analog model of what is essential to survival. A critical difference between human and other animals lies in the form of internal representation. Before we had language, with its infinite flexibility, we constructed our internal analog of the environment out of photo albums and scent arrays and audio recordings. Our deepest thinkers (e.g., Roger Penrose) appear to construct their internal analog worlds in terms of equations, at a level of abstraction which is hard for mere mortals to follow.

To summarize, when the term is applied to humans:

Consciousness designates a 3-component internalized analog of the real world, consisting of:

  1. A mind-space, wherein
  2. An analog ‘I’
  3. Narratizes (manipulates linguistic analogs Jaynes offers an idiosyncratic description involving metaphiers, metaphrands, paraphrands, and paraphiers which after n readings and 40 years remain too fuzzy and unfamiliar for me to explain briefly. of reality) to:
    • introspect; contemplate, rank, and select alternatives; analyze options; evaluate choices; anticipate reactions; and make decisions. The mind-space is also decorated (and here I extend Jaynes's image) with the pictures, scents, sounds and slogans of the earlier approach, for the attention of the linguistically challenged who don’t notice the bookcases, such as cats and hoi polloi.
    Jaynes mentions in passing some additional properties of consciousness, to replace the earlier list of things-consciousness-is-not :
    • Concentration: the ‘inner’ analog of external perceptual attention;
    • Suppression: by which we stop being conscious of annoying thoughts, the analog of turning away from annoyances in the physical world;
    • Excerption: the analog of how we sense only one aspect of a thing at a time;
    • Consilience: the analog of perceptual assimilation.

  • Consciousness-as-introspection is entirely dependent upon the prior possession of language. I find it difficult to imagine what goes on in the minds of those who don’t notice the bookcases.

    • While we are defining things, note that among the various analogs is another set, not mentioned by Jaynes, which philosophers have found very puzzling. These are

  • Qualia: (Singular: qua-le). Mental representations of phenomena detectable by instruments, acknowledged by our cortex as convenient and our emotions as suasive, but having no external reality other than as naming conventions for sections of various neurally detectable continua. Among them: the color of Capri's Blue There are an infinite number of spectra which are interpreted by the brain as the same shade of, say, blue. Without qualia, we would experience the world in monochrome, and life without nuance — but they too are distinct from consciousness, and need not puzzle us further.

2) The Biting Off

 ⩔  

The hard problem of consciousness is the problem of explaining how and why we have qualia or phenomenal experiences — how sensations acquire characteristics, such as colors and tastes (Wikipedia).

[T]he defining features of mental states and events, features like their intentionality, their subjectivity and their conscious experiential quality, seem not to be comprehensible simply in terms of the physical operation of the organism. This is not just because we have not yet accumulated enough empirical information: the problem is theoretical. We cannot at present imagine an explanation of colour perception, for example, which would do for that phenomenon what chemistry has done for combustion — an explanation which would tell us in physical terms, and without residue, what the experience of colour perception is. — T. Nagel (1993) ‘What is the mind-body problem?’ Ciba Found Symp. 174: 1–7.

There are several aspects to the hard problem, and (for me) the problem goes away if these are considered in the correct order, which is:

  • Why do qualia exist? The more realistic In a qualitative sense: Honeysuckle (harmless, tasty) should smell pleasant; hydrogen sulfide (deadlier than cyanide) repulsive. our interior map of the world, the better our chance of surviving to reproduce.
  • How are they created? Understanding how the brain processes nerve impulses from sensors into qualia may be a long-term project for neurologists, but it is ‘only’ signal processing.
  • Why are the internal signals interpreted as they are? (Why is an optical wavelength of 475 nm seen as blue?) There is no answer except ‘because it works’: our mapping of color, taste, and smell is arbitrary and idiosyncratic. We can never know how others perceive qualia.

In other words, I see the ‘hard problem’ as hard only if it assumes that your blue is my blue because we agree on the name. This is assumming too much. The problem faced by the first predators was finding prey (after the first blind cropping of the primordial grass). They required information about the environment, the invention of sensors, and ways of using the signals from those sensors. If you say ‘signal processing’ to a communication engineer, he responds ‘transforms’, KG Beauchamp (1987) Transforms for Engineers: A Guide to Signal Processing (Oxford) which are ways of moving between the dimensional domains of space/time and frequency.

Example: An incoming audible signal is an additive superposition of frequencies which can be represented by a single wiggly line of sound pressure vs time. What we need for survival, instead of that wiggly line — if we hope to find breakfast and avoid becoming lunch — is a spatial map of particular frequency patterns characteristic of prey and predators. Enter the Fourier transform, which separates sound into its component frequencies — a function performed mechanically by the coiled cochlea of the inner ear. Add binaural hearing, microsecond differentiation of phase between the ears (plus odd reflecting contours in them), and we can differentiate between a chicken off to the right (lying loudly about where she just laid an egg), and our clumsy fellow hunter crackling twigs behind us.

While ears and cochlea resolve the aural signal into frequency and direction, the complex visual signal requires more processing. Figure 2.0 shows the mathematical/algorithmic/physical structure of a digital (discrete) Fourier transform. We need not understand its details, only its simple physical regularity. We run raw data through similar analog networks and recombine the results into maps of the world around us. The information thus gained more than compensates for the time spent The first indication of variation in the speed of mental processing came from observations of the noon transit of the sun. in such processing.

After much such processing and reconstruction, we have remarkably good internal maps of the environment. The brain is loathe to discard hard-won information. The visual FFT data was needed to establish boundaries and edges, but what else is it good for? If evolution has left any trace of its experiments with this question, no one has sorted them out — but what it did was turn them into qualia: arbitrary sensations mapping continuous spectra into recognizably different signals. We assume (but can never know) that qualia are common to all of us. I know of only 2 fictional mentions of novel colors: Dr Huer's ‘infra-yellow’ ray, which solidified air for Buck Rogers, and Sir Terry Pratchett's ‘octarine’, the color of magic on the Discworld. In neither case was any attempt made to describe the color. (The assumption is simply wrong for, e.g., deuteranopes, who lack green-sensitive retinal cones.)

The hard problem may arise from this expectation that qualia are identical for all observers. This would indeed be difficult to explain. But all that evolution requires is that an environmental continuum be mappable into distinguishable qualia for an individual. It is useful that 475-nm blue and 555-nm green ‘look different’; it is neither useful nor necessary that my 475-nm quale be identical to your 475-nm quale — which would require either internal absolute reference points or telepathy for calibration, neither of which we know how to implement! We may assume that everyone sees the same colors, but there is no possible way to test this assumption.

Perhaps the key to recognizing the arbitrary nature of qualia is the phenomenon of synesthesia, or cross-talk between sensory qualia. Some individuals experience Middle C as blue, showing just how fortuitous these mappings are (and how remarkable the working set is!). There are 60 or more known types of synesthesia EM Hubbard, VS Ramachandran (2005). ‘Neurocognitive mechanisms of synesthesia’ Neuron 48(#3): –520; doi:10.1016/j.neuron.2005-10-012. and their interaction is even less understood than the qualia themselves. In the prehistoric world, such sensory confusion was likely to have been life-shortening; today it is touted on the web as a desirable and learnable experience.

Anthropology + Psychology → Theories of Religion

 ⩔  

There is a strong parallel between the work of Andrew Lang and that of Julian Jaynes, both of whom integrated the research of anthropologists and psychologists into useful theories of religion.

Lang opens The Making of Religion with a paragraph describing what appears to be the current standard anthropological explanation of the origin of religion. A Lang (1900) The Making of Religion, 2nd ed. (Longmans, Gtreeen, London): 1. Then he suggests that this approach is incomplete and that ‘the savage theory of the soul’ was based on ‘experiences which cannot be made to fit into any purely materialistic system of the universe’ {2}. He was thinking of ‘clairvoyance, thought-transference, and telepathy’ {158}, which in his day were still respectable areas of research. The hypotheses of E.B. Tylor, T.H. Huxley, A.W. Howitt, Herbert Spencer, William James, and others now forgotten, were bubbling away in journals and conferences. Among this ferment is a quote from Max Dessoir:

This ‘fully conscious life of the spirit’, in which we moderns now live, ‘seems to rest upon a substratum of reflex action of a hallucinatory type’ — to which Lang adds: ‘In that case … their psychical experiences would be such as we cannot estimate, yet cannot leave, as a possibility influencing religion, out of our calculations [emphasis added]. — Lang, Making of Religion: 57.

One may wonder what ‘reflex action of a hallucinatory type’ might be. Guy Swanson, summarizing Durkheim, E Durkheim (1912) The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life, A Study in Religious Sociology Trans. JW Swain (George Allen & Unwin, no date). described the substratum of hallucination more exactly 60 years after Lang recognized it, in a passage closely anticipating Jaynes:

[People] develop a concept of personified supernatural beings directly from the model which their society provides. Unaware, as they generally are, of the extent to which overt behavior and inner impulse are formed by relations with other people, men find themselves in the hands of mysterious forces. Unlike other forces, these social customs seem to speak to individuals, chiding them for misbehavior, directing them to choose some goals rather than others, and rewarding their conformity. The thoroughly socialized individual has so acquired these social standards that they are effective in directing his conduct even when he is quite alone. — Swanson, 1960. GE Swanson (1960) The Birth of the Gods: The Origin of Primitive Beliefs (University of Michigan Press, Ann Arbor MI): ; Emphasis added.

Swanson concluded that these vocal ‘spirits are associated with the presence of organized decision-making [groups or] purposes’ {179}.

What Lang and Swanson described was a system in which the unconscious part of the brain successfully directed a person's behavior into socially decreed patterns via hallucinated instructions — a preposterous system indeed, which could not be made to fit the modern mind because in its experience, hallucinations led quickly to incarceration for antisocial behavior. This outré insight by anthropologists fell unregarded from professional view.

Rudyard Kipling also offered a classic example of bicamerality Kipling titled his poem ‘In the Neolithic Age’, but this is 5 or 10 thousand years too late for the Solutréan rival, and (I claim) 23,000 years too late for the lecture by the totem. Granted, ‘Neolithic’ scans a bit better. in 1892:

In the Pale'lithic Age, savage warfare did I wage,
For food and fame and furry horse's pelt.
I was singer to my clan, in the dim red dawn of man,
And I sang of all we feared and fought and felt.

Yea, I sang as now I sing, when the Prehistoric spring
Made the piled Biscayan icepack split and shove,
And troll and gnome and dwerg, and the gods of cliff and berg,
Were about me and beneath me and above.

Then a rival from Solutré told the tribe my style was outré;
Neath a tomahawk, of diorite, he fell.
And I left my views on art, barbed and tanged beneath the heart,
Of a mammothistic etcher in Grenelle.

Then I split them, scalp from skull, and my hunting dogs fed full,
And their teeth I threaded neatly on a thong.
And I wiped my mouth and said, it is well that they are dead,
For I knew my work was right and theirs was wrong.

But my totem saw the shame, from his ridgepole shrine he came,
And he told me in a vision of the night:
‘There are nine and sixty ways of constructing tribal lays —
And every single one of them is right.’

The ‘ridgepole’ is perhaps an intrusion from Margaret Mead's Melanesia, but the dream is exactly the bicameral, vicarial type described by Jaynes. The behavior of the singer is straight Middle Paleolithic Instinct-1. The attitude of the totem is not yet conscience, it is the social conditioning of the tribe that kept the peace in the Upper Paleolithic.

It was left to Jaynes to rediscover and elucidate the process, this time by analysis of schizophrenic patients, and texts and artwork from the dawn of writing, whose dominant theme was ‘my god has abandoned me’. Viewed in the light of ethnology, his work is not the wild and unfounded science fiction seen by his perplexed peers, but the next step in the gradual demystification of a complex and forgotten process. In essence, all that Jaynes did was remove the ‘seem to’ (underlined above) which Swanson had had to place before ‘speak to individuals’.

An Analog 'I’ Narratizing In Mind-Space

⩔  

[M]ost of [people's] everyday life is determined not by their conscious intentions and deliberate choices but by mental processes that are put into motion by features of the environment and that operate outside of conscious awareness and guidance. — Bargh and Chartrand (1999). JA Bargh, TL Chartrand (1999) ‘ The Unbearable Automaticity of Being’ American Psychologist 54(#7) 462–479. With 160 references, the authors do not cite Jaynes.

‘Few problems have endured longer or traversed a more perplexing history than the problem of consciousness and its place in nature.’ So Jaynes introduced a 145-page discussion of the meaning of ‘consciousness’. He begins by showing that consciousness — the process and content of introspection — is not any of the things we o ?

[C]onsciousness is not all mentality, not necessary for sensation or perception, … it is not a copy of experience, nor necessary for learning, nor even necessary for thinking and reasoning, and has only an arbitrary and functional location. As a prelude to what I am to say later, I wish you to consider that there could have been at one time human beings who did most of the things we do — speak, understand, perceive, solve problems — but who were without consciousness. — Jaynes ‘Consciousness and the Voices of the Mind’ Canadian Psychology 27 (#2)
[E]verybody from Darwin on, including myself in earlier years, was wrong in trying to trace out the origin of consciousness biologically or neurophysiologically. [… W]e have to look at human history after language has evolved and ask when in history did an analog ‘I’ narratizing in a mind-space begin. — Jaynes (1986). J Jaynes (1986) ‘Consciousness and the Voices of the Mind’ Canadian Psychology 27(#2) –148.

The ‘analog I narratizing in a mind space’ describes what we do when we introspect. The lack of evolutionary predecessors means that consciousness is an emergent property, unpredictable in advance, and resulting from complexity, including language.

Jaynes's discussion of ‘what consciousness is not’ is prolog to 315 pages in which he holds that we did not attain consciousness until the dawn of history, roughly 1200 BCE. ‘It is as if all life evolved to a certain point, and then in ourselves turned a right angle and simply exploded in another direction.’ Jaynes denoted the predecessor of consciousness ‘bicamerality’, describing a ‘2-room’ brain. The rooms were perhaps the paired temporal lobes of the brain, for as we saw in Chapter 1, the brain does not waste space by duplicating functions. The rooms were occupied by an agent on one side (the left, with whom we identify), and a decision-making executive on the other — neither of whom could think about his mental processes. One-way communication, from executive to agent, was verbal (aural hallucination), perhaps transmitted over the anterior commissure.

This is the enormous step that Jaynes's peers were reluctant to follow. To pursue evidence for a major change in the behavior of the brain (of the sort anticipated by Hegel), on the basis of an idiosyncratic analysis of a few almost prehistoric artefacts and texts, seemed an unlikely way to advance one's career. As a result, Jaynes's work remains under-appreciated. Still, all that he said was that some of the things that distinguish us from the other great apes happened so recently that we can study them with the tools of art history and psychology. If one reduces the bicameral conjecture to its component hypotheses one gets research projects that granting agencies can understand:

  1. Language-based consciousness,
  2. Right-temporal-lobe specialization,
  3. The bicameral mind,
  4. The second-millennium BCE development of consciousness, and
  5. The hemispheric neurological model,

and finds growing support for each component. M Kuijsten (2006) ‘Consciousness, Hallucination, and the Bicameral Mind’, in M Kuijsten (ed.) Reflections on the Dawn of Consciousness (Julian Jaynes Soc., Henderson NV): –120. Evidently, when presented with a novel holistic result, specialists like to see it rediscovered piecemeal before accepting it.

What it is Like to be Non-conscious

⩔  

What is different about the daily life of an unconscious person? Oliver Sacks describes Joseph, who was thought to be retarded until he was 11, when someone realized that he was merely deaf. A year of learning sign language gave him rudimentary communication — but with odd results, for Joseph lacked the ability to introspect, to create an analog ‘I’ with the ability to explore a rich mind-space filled with memories coded in language, in which non-immediate options could be explored:

Joseph … had no problem with perceptual categorization or generalization, but he could not … hold abstract ideas in mind, reflect, play, plan. He seemed completely literal — unable to juggle images or hypotheses or possibilities, unable to enter an imaginative or figurative realm. … He seemed like an animal, or an infant, to be stuck in the present, to be confined to literal and immediate perception. — Sacks (1989). O Sacks (1989) Seeing Voices (HarperPerennial, NY, ): 40, 45.

It may be that Joseph's condition is not so much pathological, as one end of a continuum. I suggest this because the immediate visual image brought to mind by this passage is Grant Wood's American Gothic, which simultaneously depict M Fineman (2005) ‘The Most Famous Farm Couple in the World’ Slate, 2005-06-08. both ‘pinched, grim-faced, puritanical Bible-thumpers’ and the ‘steadfast American pioneer spirit’. Like H.L. Mencken, I see religious fundamentalism here.

Joseph's plight raises the issue of the relation of language to mental development. What is the role of vocabulary? How does the ‘imaginative and figurative realm’ of adults differ as a function of their early vocabularies? Are the ‘See Spot run’ books and the many schools that do not expect 4th-graders to spell ‘cat’ and ‘dog’ actually creating literal-minded fundamentalists, unable to contemplate hypotheses?

What Can Be Done Without Consciousness

 ⩔  

All past and present claims to the contrary, science cannot yet resolve the question of animal consciousness. For over a century, scientists believed that evolutionary theory required at least some traces of consciousness in animals. Empirical studies (e.g., on insight, language acquisition, mirror self-recognition, and deception) lent further support to this viewpoint. In retrospect, however, neither theory nor experiments proved decisive. — M Nissani (2004).‘Theory of Mind and Insight in Chimpanzees, Elephants, and Other Animals’ Ch 7 (pp. –261) of Comparative Vertebrate Cognition: Are Primates Superior to Non-Primates? LJ Rogers, G Kaplan (eds.) (Kluwer Academic/Plenum Publishers, NYC). (and this is discussing a less inclusive definition of consciousness than we and Jaynes are using).

The vernacular use of ‘conscious’ as identical to ‘awake’ is too imprecise to be useful. Jaynes noted that one can be alert and reactive to the environment in the way that animals are, J Sleutels (2006) ‘Greek zombies’ in M Kuijsten (ed.) Reflections on the Dawn of Consciousness: Julian Jaynes's Bicameral Mind Theory Revisited (Julian Jaynes Society, Henderson NV). while still lacking introspection. Since readers of this book will normally be conscious (in Jaynes‘s sense of being able to introspect) most of the time, it may be useful to describe more familiar examples of unconscious functioning.

On long night hikes it is useful to sleep while walking. At such times, I retain enough awareness to navigate slightly concave mountain trails. I awaken when the feet no longer detect the trail, maybe a mile from my last location. One needs well designed and well maintained trails for this to work. California parks qualified; the mud and roots of New England, the rocky vida of Norway, the hummocky, pot-holed bogs of Ireland, the gullies of the Pyrenees, the forests of Madagascar, and the trackless bush of Australia, do not! Veteran infantrymen sleep while marching in formation. Perhaps sensors in the feet control leg muscles through a spinal ganglion, but however this is managed, it makes travel as effortless and expeditious as wearing 7-league boots.

Knitters tell me that one can read and knit at the same time. I have startled people by conversing while my fingers empty a typing buffer (which seems to hold at least a sentence), in a process I wasn't aware of.

Segovia seemed to fall asleep on stage, while his fingers continued to perform magic on the guitar. Concert pianists concentrate when they learn a piece, but conscious attention to fingering would demolish a performance. A frog/quarterback integrates a suite of differential equations to hit his fly/receiver. Animals live without consciousness, and it is not necessary for the most demanding occupations: A successful diagnostician may not know how he arrives at a medical diagnosis. THE BICAMERAL BRAIN

⩔   ▽  

The end comes when we no longer talk with ourselves. It is the end of genuine thinking and the beginning of the final loneliness. — Edward Gibbon.

The discussion above may be unfamiliar and bizarre — but is it any less bizarre than traditional ideas? It opens the way to a simple, natural explanation of the concept ‘god’ that is comprehensive, free of overbelief, and ultimately testable. Jaynes's argument J Jaynes (1976) The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind. (Houghton Mifflin, Boston, ): 202. for the existence of the gods goes:

The gods were in no sense ‘figments of the imagination’ of anyone. They were man's volition. They occupied his nervous system, probably his right hemisphere, and from stores of admonitory and preceptive experience, transmuted this experience into articulated speech which then ‘told’ the man what to do.

The support for Jaynes's ‘committee of 2’ is as good as that for Freud's ‘committee of 3’, and the approaches are not mutually exclusive. Freud's division, following Plato's tripartite soul — appetitive, rational, and spiritual — parallels the layering of the brain. Bicamerality parallels the division of the brain into hemispheres. The hemispheres are complex cognitive systems that process information independently and in parallel, E Zaidel (1985) ‘Academic implications of dual-brain theory’ in The Dual Brain (The Guilford Press, NYC). as in dual-core computer chips. Brain-imaging shows right temporal lobe involvement in auditory hallucinations. MS Buchsbaum &12a (1083) ‘Cerebral glucography with positron tomography. Invisible Playmates
⩔  

[T]he inability to play, pretend, and imagine is actually a sign of trouble for children. — PL Harris (2000) The work of the imagination (Blackwell, Oxford).

Two-thirds of our children have invisible playmates, JL Singer, DC Singer (1984) Television, imagination and aggression (Erlbaum, Hillsdale NJ). who generally wander off by age 9. The playmate need not be another child, or even human. Christopher Robin's friend Winnie-the-Pooh is the classical prototype, while Calvin (child) and Hobbes (philosophical bipedal tiger) in Fig. 2.1 are the best known US pair, from Bill Watterson's comic strip. Hobbes camouflages himself as a stuffed toy in the presence of adults.

Children with invisible friends develop a ‘theory of mind’ earlier than others, and develop faster, JB Wigger (2011) ‘Learning from Children and Their Invisible Friends’ J Childhood and Religion 2(#3) 34 pp. both psychologically and linguistically. In rare cases today the playmate does not disappear, but matures along with the child as a constant advisor.

A hallucinated dialog is more efficient than an internal debate. Instead of asking oneself, ‘What will Mom say if I ask her thus-and-so?’, it is easier to divide the brain and ask a practiced Internal Mom, ‘Mom, may I do thus-and-so?’, and Internal Mom answers. Neural plasticity means that the brains of children in different environments develop differently. In a bicameral world invisible playmates became civic advisors. In our world, ‘Internal Mom’ becomes our conscience. Same function, different neurological details (synapses). Those with active consciences may find them annoying, but seldom need to account for them. Bicamerality is a different form of conscience, and needs no other explanation.

Evidently The Origin of Consciousness in the Breakdown of the Bicameral Mind presents more of a challenge to the psychological establishment than I realized.

Ghosts
⩔  

[T]he real problem is to account for the evolution out of ghosts of the eternal, creative, moral god. — Andrew Lang (1900) The Making of Religion, nd ed. (Longmans, Green, London): Ch. 9.

Ghosts are so omnipresent in small-scale societies that ethnologists treat them as an explanation, not as something to be explained. (Ferguson RB Ferguson (Ed.) (1984) Warfare, Culture and Environment (Academic Press, NYC). is a rare exception.) According to Jaynes, a ghost is a persistent bicameral representation of a dead person. Talion Law at the retributive level of ‘an eye for an eye’. requires that the murder, or death in battle, of a relative be avenged. Until the responsible person accomplishes this,

the old women will taunt him; if he were unmarried, no girl would speak to him; if he had wives, they would leave him; his mother would cry and lament that she had given birth to so degenerate a son, his father would treat him with contempt, and he would be a mark of public scorn. — EB Tylor (1881).Anthropology: An Introduction to the Study of Man and Civilization (Macmillan, NYC): 415.

To this, Davie MR Davie (1929) The Evolution of War: A Study of its Role in Early Societies (Yale Univ. Press, New Haven CT). adds

If the survivors fail in their duty, the enraged ghost will haunt them all their lives. Ghost-fear, transcending all other forms of fear, assures the fulfilment of this obligation. Blood revenge, therefore, is fundamentally a form of propitiating the ghost or soul of the departed.

Bicameral ghosts cannot be evaded, they speak with absolute authority, and they will continue to haunt their relatives until the blood debt is repaid. Hamlet, stripped of sub-plots, is the prototypical Western representative of the story: Hamlet's uncle kills Hamlet's father, who then appears to Hamlet as a ghost and demands vengeance. In the manner of Greek tragedies, all of the principals die in the last act — but Hamlet does succeed in avenging his father. And yet even while Hamlet is experiencing classical bicameral hallucinations, he is also the first introspective dramatic hero. As for Socrates 2 millennia earlier, his brain finds uses for both operational modes.

Yet not all ghosts are malevolent. The Pawnees of Nebraska were bicameral into the 1600s, and ‘auditory hallucinations, A Lang (1900) The Making of Religion, 2nd ed. (Longmans, Green, London): Ch 14. as of a voice giving good advice in time of peril, [were] regarded as the speech of ghosts’. Herbert Spencer (1897) Principles of Sociology, 3 vols. (Appleton, NYC): I, 307. mentions a culture that Jaynes missed, in which ghosts are loving and helpful:

[A]mong the Mandans, who place the skulls of their dead in a circle, each wife knows the skull of her former husband or child. There is scarcely an hour in a pleasant day that more or less of these women may be seen sitting or lying by the skull of their child or husband — talking to it in the most pleasant and endearing language … and seemingly getting an answer back.

Jaynes would remove ‘seemingly’ from this description.

Pneuma
⩔  

Before Enlightenment scientists sorted out the circulation of the blood and the chemistry of oxidation, there was understandable confusion about the function of breathing. This confusion is epitomized by the Greek word pneuma, which originally meant ‘blast, wind’. Later it was softened to ‘air, breeze’, then with metaphorical qualifiers, ‘air imagined as filling the veins’, and ‘breath of life’. In bicameral days, deceased relatives commonly spoke to their surviving family — but the shades of animals apparently did not. The speaking animals of folklore indicate that they were at least heard by anyone able to interpret the nonverbal communication of living animals. Those to whom departed animals never said a word naturally assumed that people possessed something unique, unknown to animals. The egocentricity of the religious personality, demanding that there be some exalted difference between us and them, seized upon this as the essential difference. Respiration — which had been the practical distinction between an unconscious body and a dead one in the Iliad — was transmogrified by mystified poets into a vehicle for the bicameral voice of the departed. By the time of the New Testament, pneuma was used with the connotation we today attach to ‘soul’. As Tylor EB Tylor (1871) Primitive Culture, 2 vols, 4th ed. (John Murray, London, 1903): I, 432. shows, this interpretation was common throughout the primitive world. The spirit of man departs with the last breath: taken at face value, this indicates that it enters with the first one — a detail of possible interest in the abortion debate.

Indeed, this understanding J Vinycomb (1906) Fictitious & Symbolic Creatures in Art (Chapman and Hall, London): 53. is reinforced by the Christian symbology of the winged lion of St Mark: ‘The lion was accepted in early times as a symbol of the resurrection because the young lion was believed always to be born dead, but was awakened to vitality by the breath … of its sire.’

Logically, this usage removes the essential characteristic of humanity from the fetus. It is potentially human until it takes its first breath. For 200,000 years, the interval between delivery and first inhalation gave midwives an opportunity for a final error check before accepting a new candidate for tribal membership. Obviously deformed neonates never took their first breath: Better a wasted pregnancy than a wasted life. Mercantilism and its demand for an ever increasing market led us into the trap of regarding the fetus as a potential customer, to be preserved at whatever cost — to the mother.

Socrates
⩔  

We cannot help viewing Socrates as the turning-point, the vortex of world history. — Nietzsche (1872) The Birth of Tragedy 11tr. F Golffing (Doubleday Anchor, NYC, 1974): 94.

Nietzsche seems to have understood that Socrates stood on the cusp of the bicameral/introspective transition, for he wrote:

In this totally anomalous character, instinctive wisdom reveals itself only in order to stand up now and then against conscious knowledge as a hindrance. Whereas in all productive men instinct is the truly creative and affirming power, and consciousness acts as a critical and cautioning reaction, in Socrates instinct becomes the critic, consciousness becomes the creator — truly a monstrosity per defectum [from some defect]! — F Nietzsche (1872) The Birth of Tragedy §13.

One can question Nietzsche's identification of instinct as the source of creativity, because instinct, strictly speaking, is retrospective, conservative, and the epitome of what kept our ancestors alive. Nietzsche seems to use it here to mean what he would later call the unconscious, of which instinct is but a part. He also misconstrued the nature of Socrates's daemon, for it was not instinct but the accumulated wisdom of the bicameral mind.

Despite his confusions, Nietzsche captured the essence of Socrates, and what it was that made him the Classical ‘vortex’. Or Western vortex. In a wider view, Isaiah, Zoroasters, Gautama, and Confucius were the vortices of their cultures. I will maintain below that the Axial Age (of which Socrates was a primary manifestation) was the the first impressive result of the process that Jaynes called ‘the origin of consciousness in the breakdown of the bicameral mind’.

Socrates heard a ‘warning voice’ in childhood (ek paidos arxamenon, Apology 31d), and as an adult his ‘spiritual sign visits [him] now and then’ (Euthyphro 3b); this might seem an opaque reference, but it is clarified later, for in Phaedrus 242bc we find that he never had to think about ethics. Whenever the possibility of unethical action arose, his personal daemon intervened and told him not to do it. Aristotle interpreted that statement figuratively, but I think Socrates meant exactly what he said: He did not ‘hear’ the still small voice of conscience, he heard an internal Attic Greek voice saying ‘STOP!’. This is an idiosyncratic interpretation, for other members of the Julian Jaynes Society side with Aristotle. On the other hand, the ‘Socratic method’ of asking leading questions until the answerer argues himself into a truth can also be interpreted as questions by a sharp, conscious, analytical mind that was also vestigially bicameral. Realizing his own difference from others, Socrates may have been genuinely interested in how other people worked their way to ethical judgements. I think Jaynes and Socrates would have had a great deal to talk about!

Bicamerality persisted for half a millennium afer Socrates, as we know from the Syrian Mara Bar Serapion. Writing to his son from prison some time between 73 and 256 CE, and urging him to follow wise men of the past (mentioning Socrates, Pythagoras, and Jesus), he observed, ‘A Letter of Mara, Son of Serapion’ (transl. BP Pratten) in Ante-Nicene Fathers Vol. VIII, Memoirs of Edessa And Other Ancient Syriac Documents: 735. (Note that ‘lived on in’ is apparently a translator's explanatory insertion, as it does not appear in Pratten's version, but is imported from Habermas, G (1996) The Historical Jesus (College Press Publishing Company, Joplin MO).) ‘But Socrates did not die for good; he lived on in the statue of Hera.’

Socrates saw that ‘the unexamined life is not worth living’. He still heard a bicameral voice, yet he could introspect and examine his life, and it seems that he noticed that many did not, and that this led to a wasted life. Many still do not, so conscious introspection is not automatic after bicamerality is lost. Apollo's ‘Know thyself’ was the most important advice that could be offered at the dawn of consciousness. Or later.

Socrates's daemon is for me a convincing verification of Jaynes's bicameral conjecture, but he was not alone. Isaiah heard the voice of YHVH. The hymns of the orally transmitted Vedas are the special sort of knowledge (vid) that is heard (sruti), and they differ from the Gathas of Iran only in dialect. This suggests that Zoroaster was bicameral: certainly he knew the concept of ‘messenger of god’. Confucius — otherwise a ‘benevolent agnostic’ knew of statues which spoke HA Giles (1905) Religions of Ancient China (Norwood Editions, Norwood PA, 1977); Ch 2. in times of stress, and perhaps was remembering a bicameral age when he said of spirits, ‘We listen for them but do not hear them; yet they enter into all things, and there is nothing without them’. Nothing I have read suggests that Gautama was bicameral.

THE DECLINE OF BICAMERALITY

 ⩔   ▽  

When am I going to see gods face-to-face again? {Jaynes's translation of Psalm 42:2}. (‘Misreading!’, cry his critics. ‘You're tampering with Scripture!’)

Mobility and diversity make a morality based on shared valuation of traditions and institutions quite difficult. (Whose traditions? Which institutions?). — J Haidt, J Graham (2007) ‘When morality opposes justice: Conservatives have moral intuitions that liberals may not recognize’ Social Justice Research, 20(#1): –116; doi 10.1007/s11211-007-0034-z .

How did consciousness replace bicamerality? This question is as important as the idea of bicamerality itself, because it represents a dramatic change in mental behavior which no one before Jaynes had seen in the historical record. Nor is there any obvious reason for such a change to occur.

Perhaps part of the problem is that defining consciousness as ‘that which is introspectable’ seems too broad, and I would replace it with ‘that which is introspected’. Possession of an ability does not guarantee its use, and those who fail to examine their own motivations no more understand themselves than do sheep. Only those things which we mull over are truly our own. This definition also emphasizes consciousness as a graded property which cannot be acquired suddenly; it is, in short, learned behavior, and learning takes time, both personal and historical.

Consider the unrelated psychological behavior of denial. We are all still prone to this protective device, despite the problem that follow in its wake. An anguished primal scream ‘No-o-o!’ at the loss of a loved one may mean either ‘This isn't happening!’, or ‘I didn't want that to happen!’. The first is denial, and if persisted in, problematic. The second is realistic grief, and curative. To the uninvolved onlooker, the internal significance is undetectable, and prior to Freud there was no way to analyze the difference. Sophocles was aware of denial's consequences, but he did not baptise it with a common noun to make it public property. So too with bicamerality. When observers do not understand it, they do not see it from outside, and its effects get lumped into the ‘simplicity’ we attribute to small-scale societies. No anthropological research prior to Jaynes's 1976 book was aware that it should investigate bicamerality.

The system does not adapt to changing circumstances, and is disappearing upon contact with Europeans. None of the above reasons for the loss of bicamerality seems applicable to New Guinea. Some feel that fragility alone is sufficient to end bicamerality when there is an alternative social model. In New Guinea as in Meso-America, Westerners did not suspect the existence of bicamerality, and did not report it. No one asked the right questions; lacking a common language, the symptoms of bicamerality disappear into the mists of misunderstanding. When a bicameral culture is suddenly exposed to fully conscious people the voices are nonplussed because the only advice they have to offer is suddenly irrelevant tradition.

Africa?
⩔  

Jaynes did not mention sub-Saharan Africa in connection with bicamerality. The African world was rife with animist spirits, mostly inimical, and benevolent spirits of any sort are rare. ‘The Hausa's whole existence is occupied in combating these evil influences’, according to AJN Tremearne. (1914) The Ban of the Bori (Heath, Cranton, & Ouseley, London): 20. An early and not particularly helpful form of bicamerality appears to have come out of Africa with us, related to the ka and double in the next section, an ambiguous companion sometimes referred to as a qarina {138}.

Arabia
⩔  

At this point, Arabia seems to be something of an anomaly. We know that Arabians still heard voices in the late first milliennium CE, centuries after they had gone quiet in nearby Mesopotamia. Ibn Al-Kalbi H Ibn Al-Kalbi (c 800) The Book of Idols Being a Translation from the Arabic of the Kitab Al-Asnam tr. NA Faris (Princeton, NJ, 1952): 16. However, personal voices in Arabia seem to have been destructive, and certainly were not known for providing the socially useful direction attributed to bicameral voices. Perhaps this was a product of the Reichian armoring that accompanies desertification; perhaps the mores and social rules of tribal nomads lay closer to the wishes of the id.

There are also aspects of Islamic culture that do not object to constant supervision by a nanny:

  • Islam tries (in Shari'a) to provide rules for every aspect of behavior, so that one knows at all times what Allah wants him to do. This feels like an attempt to provide the certainty of bicameral days after the voices go silent.
  • Islamic schooling seems designed to retain bicameral control of the faithful. At the same time, it seems to have gotten itself a reputation for being evil, but given the enthusiasm with which Muslims have historically killed other Muslims, these are not discordant results. ⩓  
Exotic Bicamerality
⩔  

Several other contemporary phenomena are best explained by the bicameral conjecture. Spiritualist mediums who hear voices and truly believe that they ‘contact the spirit world’ are obvious candidates, but 150 years of increasingly critical study (in the sense of clever design, double-blinded experiments in which neither parsicpant for experimenter knows the expeted result, and statistical analysis), have suggested no promising avenues of research. We look at less emotionally loaded examples.

⩓  
Hypnotism
⩔  

A Dublin psychiatrist who did stage shows for fun (and advertizing) explained that the difference between entertainment and healing lay in the suggestions offered by the hypnotist. Calling for volunteers, he sent half back to their seats, assessing susceptibility at a glance. The remainder were putty in his hands. When he told them they itched, they scratched. Some were so tormented that they started after the troubling spots by disrobing on stage. He told them to freeze; they stood immobile in awkward poses until he gave them new instructions. He told them they were happy; they laughed. He told them they were sad; they cried.

Next he gave them a short rest, in which they could do anything they wished, and then asked them about their activities. One young chap was with his girl and indignant at the interruption, so the hypnotist left him to get on with it. Another, more imaginative, was ‘skiing the prominences of the sun’, and it was the most thrilling experience of his life.

The finale was a posthypnotic command. As the audience filed out, each of his subjects would spot a leprechaun, pursue him, capture him, and take him home. And so they did. In a most undignified performance, well-dressed middle-aged ladies leaped off the stage, clambered over seats, and pushed people aside as they pursued elusive little men whom only they could see. One fellow held an empty armful as though he were restraining a thrashing 4-year-old, asking me, ‘Isn't he cute?’. Cab drivers watched bemused as customers stuffed uncooperative leprechauns into their vehicles. One driver, inured to the effects of Guinness, locked his front door, insisting, ‘Both of ye, get in the back.’ The amount of brain processing needed to produce these leprechauns comes perilously close to the strong hallucinations — ‘3D, … resist[ing] with a sense of solidity, &c’ — that Daniel Dennet (1991) Consciousness Explained (Penguin, Hammondsworth, 1993): 7. feels must be fictional — including those described by Carlos Castañeda in Don Juan. For the record, I too felt strongly that Castañeda was not a trustworthy guide, so I stopped reading him after his second book. Others, less sceptical, insist that he becomes more reliable in later volumes. I seldom disagree with Dennett, but these post-hynotic subjects were physically struggling with their hallucinations on the streets of Dublin.

Edoardo Casiglio (2008) ‘Hypnosis in the Theory of the Bicameral Mind’ The Jaynesian, Sping 2008: –14. of the University of Padua, explains why hypnosis is so easily induced:

Consciousness … is also accompanied by epiphenomena that are not always welcome, such as the awareness of the imminence of death, the passing of time, guilt, regret: in short, the loss of Eden. This loss is so recent … that it takes very little to ‘forget’ the real state of the emancipated humans and go back temporarily to the latent bicameral condition.

In bicameral days, the instructions of the internal adviser were as compelling as those of the today's hypnotist. Because ‘such individuals had no mind-space in which to question or rebel, such voices had to be obeyed’, says Jaynes. As long as the ‘voices’ — the gods of society — agreed on behavior, society worked as smoothly as an ant hill, with people uncomplainingly building ziggurats as instructed by their gods. The pyramids did not require coercion.

⩓  
Schizophrenia
⩔  

Schizophrenia means ‘split mind’, and its voices arise when the right hemisphere acts as the speech center for the unconscious, or for the alternate personality in the right temporal lobe. The caliber of the communication depends upon the quality of the unconscious. Since only disturbed patients get to the clinic, selection bias is at work, so that the clinical assumption is that schizophrenia is always dysfunctional. Factor analysis AC Lahti &5a (2006) ‘Correlations Between rCBF and Symptoms in Two Independent Cohorts of Drug-Free Patients with Schizophrenia’ Neuropsychopharmacology 31: –230. then shows 3 independent components: negative (poverty of speech, affect, and movement), distortion (delusions and hallucinations), and disorganization (thought disorder and inappropriate affect).

In any case, schizophrenia is not always a malady, but a graded property of normal brains D Smith (2007) Muses, Madmen, and Prophets: Rethinking the History, Science, and Meaning of Auditory Hallucination (Penguin, Harmondsworth). — mysterious, perhaps, but normally so. Only when the instructions are socially disruptive are those who obey them noticed — and generally incarcerated in mental hospitals as a result. Yet not all internal voices are disruptive, and internet searches turn up a wide range of ‘spirit guides’ indistinguishable from schizophrenic voices.

⩓  
Oracular Statues
⩔  

Sometimes ‘the voices’ have clear external locations. A classical example is the speaking statue. In Linear B (the earliest readable Greek) the ruler is a wanax. His chief steward is a basileus, or ‘king’. Evidence from other cultures suggests that the wanax was not a person, but a statue who instructed the king. ‘Statue who’ because it was perceived as, and gave orders in the manner of, a god. If one hears an ‘external’ voice, and is troubled because there is no one to produce it, the voice issues instructions to make a statue for it.

The bicameral mind may hear more than one voice. The Internal Playmate becomes a personal daemon with advice on personal behavior. The rules of your trade are heard as its tutelary deity. Laws are heard in the voice of the king, and outlive the king himself: The dead king becomes a living god. His voice is still heard, so his body is preserved, with a historical progression in which body parts of the king are steadily decreased: first he is mummified, later his skull is built into a statue, finally he is replaced by a statue. Late statues equipped with speaking tubes were not deliberate fraud, but a desperate attempt to continue hearing traditional advice as the voices went silent.

Bicameral cultures tell us that people really die long after their apparent death. For the lifetimes of their grandchildren, their hallucinated voices may still be heard. It follows that during this period, the ‘dead’ need all the paraphernalia of grave goods.

The Bible describes YHVH speaking from a fire {Deut 4:12}, so none of this should seem strange to Jews or Christians. On his way to punish the Israelites for their sins, ‘The king of Babylon, he consulted with images’ {Ezekiel 21:21}.

Hammurabi heard the voice of Shamash, god of justice, and what he heard was good enough to influence our own law code. His letters to viceroys (all in his own handwriting) began with an instruction to the letter, ‘Say unto So-and-so …’, as though the letter were not read but heard. In Catholic girls’ schools, statues of the Virgin still verbally soothe adolescent turmoil. The figures on Achilles's shield conversed like living mortals {Iliad 18.539}. The speech of herms, postlike statues with a human head and priapic genitals, is well attested in Greek literature. One of Guthrie's S Guthrie (1993) Faces in the Clouds (Oxford University Press, NYC): Fig 5-29. examples of anthropomorphic religious objects is an illustration of a 4th-century stele whose caption reads ‘Greeks thought steles could think and even speak’. That's a very patronizing attitude: the steles did speak to bicameral Greeks.

⩓  
Guardian Angels, Invisible Gurus
⩔  

Individuals experiencing auditory verbal hallucinations will fail to recognize that their ‘voices’ embody their own internally generated thoughts and will often seek alternative explanations for their genesis. — JR Atkinson(2006) ‘The Perceptual Characteristics of Voice-Hallucinations in Deaf People: Insights into the Nature of Subvocal Thought and Sensory Feedback Loops’ Schizophrenia Bulletin 32(#4): 701–708; 2006 doi:10.1093/schbul/sbj063.

For many people, ‘voices’ are a useful resource. Among my acquaintances are college-educated professionals who assure me that they have ‘guardian angels’ or ‘invisible gurus’ who offer consistently good advice. They seldom talk about them, because people look at them oddly when they do.

From examples offered, a sceptic notes that the information spans a range of genres. At one end, anthropologists would class it with descriptions of the shamans’ spirit world. It slips imperceptibly into behavior modification with philosophic generalities and advice on diet, social relations, purchases, and health care. The advice is valid — but characteristically something which the person might have noticed subconsciously. Night-driving fatigue induces the guardian angel to suggest, ‘Turn around. There's a motel on the left a couple of miles back.’ The suggestion is never, ‘There's a motel a half-mile ahead’.

While 40-odd medical conditions JR Brasic (1998) ‘Hallucinations’ Perceptual and Motor Skills 86: 851–877. induce hallucinatory experiences, studies consistently find hallucinations reported by 5–25% AY Tien (1991) ‘Distribution of hallucinations in the population’ Social Psychiatry and Psychiatric Epidemiology 26: 287–92. of normal populations. Twice as many H Sidgewick (1894) ‘Report on the census of hallucinations’ Proc Soc Psychical Res 34: –394. people have visual hallucinations as auditory. While there is an obvious need for more research on this question, any brain that can dream can hallucinate, so that hallucination is more a matter of a sticky switch than anything supernatural or alarming. Twice, a dream image persisted through the hypnopompic state until I was fully awake. Once it occupied the center of the visual field as though sitting between me and the foot of the bed. I tried to preserve it for study, succeeding for not more than 5 seconds, before it winked out and the foot of the bed continued across the space the image had occupied. Brief as it was, it gives a clear indication that the dream mechanism — which can call on memory, the subconscious, the audio-vocal system, and the sensory-motor system — projects its images along the same path used by the retina, and in the same manner. I have not found a better description of this process in the literature. The second time, I watched a water strider (Gerrid) take off and fly — something I have never seen in real life. It flew slowly, and I woke up to observe better (an attempt that clearly implies a lack of coordination between cortex and limbos during sleep!). It kept flying as the background slowly dissolved from small stream to bedclothes, and finally faded out. We don't know how either dreams or hallucinations occur, but the mechanism seems to be the same.

In a recent book (whose cover shows a mountaineer backpacking in a blizzard), John Geiger (2009) The Third Man Factor: Surviving the Impossible (Cannongate, Edinburgh): 15. relates

a common experience that happens to people who confront life at its extremes, and strange as it may sound, given the cruel hardships that they endure to reach that place, it is something wonderful. […T]hey report that at a critical point they were joined by an additional, unexplained friend who lent them power to overcome the most dire circumstances.

The impression of companionship is so convincing that one mountaineer reported breaking a Kendall Mint Cake in half to share with his friend, before discovering that he was alone. For a familiar prime-time version, recall ‘Lewis’, Lex Luthor's hallucinated, level-headed, companion on an otherwise deserted Caribbean island {Smallville, Season 3, Episode 1, }, whom Lex ultimately tried to kill. Considering the daily hardships of Paleolithic life, any mechanism that provided helpful guidance would soon find its way into our genes, to become a normal part of human experience.

Unfortunately, such guidance is not always helpful — and we don't know why. Unfortunately, the paper is behind a paywall (it's a medical journal), and the abstract does not say what fraction of the episodes included helpful advice. At least one of the co-authors was a mountaineer who had hallucinated deceptive mountain guides when he was in trouble on Dhaulagiri (Nepal, 8167 m): ‘Jump here. you’ll be out of danger in no time’, offering a 1000-foot precipice. Cautious, he tried a short jump, which hurt sufficiently to dissuade him from the long one.

[T]he mountain guides … talked kindly to him and offered more advice. They urged him to jump down the east face [a sheer 6500-meters]. ‘In few seconds you will be on a flat, safe place 2,000 meters lower. This will solve all your problems.’

Cautious, Tomazin tried a 6-foot jump: the resulting pain disuaded him from following the guides' advice, and he survived. One wonders how many climbers whose bodies have never been found followed such guidance.

The Hearing Voices Movement, MAJ Romme, ADMAC Escher (1989) ‘Hearing Voices’ Schizophrenia Bulletin 15(#2): 209–216. which aims to change the public perception of those who hear voices, began life under this name in England in 1988, following earlier work by Marius Romme and Sandra Escher in the Netherlands. It is an organization for voice hearers and others interested in this phenomenon whose attitude is that auditory hallucination is not a pathological phenomenon in need of eradication but a meaningful, useful experience. Many of its members started hearing voices after a traumatic stress, just as in bicameral days. Oddly, the movement mentions Jaynes's work only in passing, as one ‘frame of reference’ among many (‘psychodynamic, mystical, parapsychological, and medical’) adopted by a particular hallucinator.

⩓  
Current Echoes
⩔  

Hypnotism and schizophrenia may be remnants of the non-conscious bicameral state, but they are transient or pathological, and oracular statues are rare nowadays. However, persistent forms of bicamerality are still with us, and either harmless or downright useful.

⩓  
Dreams
⩔  

Today our dreams are vicarial (the analog ‘I’ is doing something besides sleeping) and translocative (the action is somewhere other than where one is sleeping). In bicameral days this was apparently not the case. The dreams in the Iliad and Genesis are of a god standing by the bed issuing instructions. Dreams recorded in cuneiform tablets go back to 2500 BCE and are all of this type. Jaynes J Jaynes (1986) ‘Consciousness And The Voices Of The Mind’ Canadian Psychology 27(#2) –148. regarded this

historical change in the nature of dreams, as one of the great confirmations of the strong form of the central hypothesis of the origin of consciousness in the breakdown of the bicameral mind.

If I sleep long enough to finish all of the brain's ‘chron’ jobs (the computer maintenance program run by Unix systems every morning at 03:00), somebody lets the brain-kittens loose, and I wake myself laughing at the dreams they turn out. What a dull life bicamerality must have imposed!

Incidentally, if you have conversations in your dreams, your brain is obviously capable of generating internal speech without conscious help. The difference from bicameral advice is twofold:

  • Dreams, these days, do not habitually offer good advice and often seem totally random.
  • Speech generation by the right hemisphere is normally shut down except during sleep.

A Guardian columnist C Brooks (2006) ‘Get on the right track’. Guardian Weekly 2006-09-15 p16. writes that non-English-speaking cab drivers who do not know London expect fares to type their destination into the GPS, after which it tells the driver when to turn. Said he,

This exactly describes our earlier operating system. Even the expletive ‘by God’ is spot on. This is the way the gods ran our lives — and simplicity was the reason. Thinking is hard work: why do your own if it has already been done for you? Possessing a complex organ does not mean that we know how to use it, so perhaps bicamerality was a needed half-way stage between the animal unconscious and what we have today. A darker example of bicameral invocation is the neonazi movement. Faith Replaces Bicamerality
⩔  

Faith includes a sacrificium intellectus [sacrifice of the intellect] (provided that there is an intellect to sacrifice). — Jung

As bicamerality died, people ceased to experience gods, leaving the emptiness of Fig. 2.2. This is analogous to the disappearance of an established fishery, with 2 possible responses. Philosophers should rejoice at the loss of bicamerality, as it enables us to move forward into a conscious exploration of our universe. This has not been the usual response. More typical is an ersatz bicamerality:

When people cease to experience God, they are forced to believe in him, implied Tillich — and belief is a commodity subject to loss. The inner sense of God is a quality of the deeper psyche [i.e, limbos] — and not of reason. When the West was lost to spiritual interiority, all that remained was belief, or the religious euphemism for it, faith. — SA Hoelle, (1989) Jung and the Lost Gospels (Quest Publishing, Wheaton IL). emphasis added.

The assumption always seems to be that the ‘experience of god’ or ‘spiritual interiority’, or the bicameral voice that made our difficult decisions for us, was valuable and something we want to return to. A less nostalgic view might see the bicameral voices as a stopgap measure that saw us through a difficult transition until we could stand on our own feet.

Jung, like all who seek nirvana, Bowing to colloquial usage. I write ‘nirvana’ where, strictly speaking, one should perhaps use ‘samadhi’. This led him to feel that the subjective nature of nirvana did not disqualify it as ‘real’. This is a valid hypothesis which we might formalize as ‘Metaphysical archetypes are encoded in DNA’, since there is no other biological way to transmit information between generations. ‘Archetype’ is no more unlikely a concept than ‘instinct’, and perhaps no harder to encode, so as DNA reading progresses, such questions will become answerable. Or not, if nirvana is not a spiritual experience. What if it is, as suggested above, merely a transient return to the infant's state of absolute egoism? What becomes of ‘reality’, ‘value’, ‘spiritual maturity’, and ‘archetypal truth’ in that case? What if the West was never ‘lost’, but merely enabled to move on?

Different Culture, Different Brain
⩔  

… the tremendous rôle played, in an individual's life, by the social environment in which each is born and reared. One by one, aspects of behaviour which we had been accustomed to consider invariable complements of our humanity were found to be merely a result of civilisation, present in the inhabitants of one country, absent, in another country, and this without a change of race. — Margaret Mead, Coming of Age in Samoa

The functioning of a cybernetic system depends both on the hardware and the operating system. Silicon OSes are regularly upgraded to take advantage of new hardware features, but this does not happen automatically with brains. Even though their brains are probably equipped for introspection, fundamentalists give the impressions of trying to continue with the bicameral OS. Taking advantage of the Enlightenment requires serious introspection — and this is hard work. Any potential ‘clash of civilizations’ is fomented by cultures with the bicameral outlook, incompatible with those influenced by the Enlightenment.

Unfazed by the most complicated structure in the known universe, many people respond to the idea of bicamerality by demanding a mechanistic explanation. This will be forthcoming soon after we can explain how a finger moves when we want it to. This ‘mind-body’ problem has excersized philosophers throughout history: Fig. 2.3 offers 2 relevant suggestions:

  • Perhaps bicamerality did not so much vanish, as go underground, losing its voice to urges and impulses from our unconscious and intuition — no less mysterious. Many plants have juvenile leaves that serve young plants well, and adult leaves that work better for adult plants; we might think of Jaynesian bicamerality as our juvenile OS, and Freudian consciousness as the adult OS.
  • It is easy to talk of the bicameral–conscious change as ‘rewiring’, but the brain already has shorter interlobe wires C Cherniak &3a (2002) ‘Global Optimization of Cerebral Cortex Layout’, Proc Roy Soc Lond Biol Sci B252: 13–18. than a billion other spatial arrangements of the same components, which is better than we do with computer chips. This makes literal rewiring of interlobe communications unlikely. The suggested difference between ‘Freudian’ and ‘Fundamentalist’ OSes is the focus of improvements, with more abundant synaptical interconnections surviving infant culling in the cortex leading to the Freudian OS, and stronger synapses and a more influential limbic system leading to the Fundamentalist OS. These will not appear in the fossil record, but may continue down the columns.

Recent research shows that the brain is more flexible than we have realized, raising the question of whether an OS change might be intentionally mediated. Even though the frontal lobe is the likely home of the OS, and we know little about how to influence it, suppose that we had a source of bicameral subjects: might we discover brain exercizes that could update them? Could we try this on Fundamentalist volunteers?

A small example S Yamaguchi &6a (2003) ‘Synchronization of Cellular Clocks in the Suprachiasmatic Nucleus’ Science 302: 1408–1412. of the blue-to-yellow transition might be the ability to access more directly the clock cells of the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) that manage our circadian rhythm and let us estimate the passage of time. Add a few cells to the 20,000 in the SCN and we might be able to say, ‘It's 18:15’ with an error of 5 minutes.

Although ‘fundamentalist’ is something of a pejorative these days, the utility of the OS of this name depends entirely upon the belief that drives the system. A brain driven by end-times eschatology or jihad is not helpful; the same brain driven by an Eirenic and Gaian belief would be distinctly useful. Research on how to encourage such a switch could be helpful.

Brain Plasticity
⩔  

[T]he brain changes its very structure with each different activity it performs, perfecting its circuits so it is better suited to the task in hand …. [This is] surely one of most extraordinary discoveries of the 20th century. — Norman Doidge [tense modified].

To those familiar with the view of the brain as a collection of specialized, hard-wired processors located in specific areas (the accepted view since the Enlightenment) the idea of changing its operating system will seem wild speculation and unjustifiable science-fiction. On the other hand, a lot of little-known work has been done in recent decades.

Beginning with Paul Bach-y-Rita in the 1960s, there has been a gradual recognition that while the identified local processors are among the most efficient regions of the brain, perhaps 80% of their functionality can be carried out by the learned rewiring of other areas, sometimes neighboring, sometimes improbably distant and known for efficiency in their own specialties. The Qarin
⩔  

Perhaps the most controversial aspect of Fig. .3 is the label ‘Qarin’ for the OS contemporaneous with the Jaynesian. The name comes from the invisible companion described as a ‘double’ by Breasted and Budge above, and called qarina in Hausa and qarin in Arabic (well known to Mohammed, as will appear below). For now, it provides a name for an OS that Jaynes acknowledged only parenthetically — and which, I suggest, formed the other end of the bicameral continuum.

Militca Žurkovič (who does not mention Jaynes) offers a psychological approach to the Doppelganger of literature which encompasses both bicamerality and the qarin: ‘the double shows in graphic forms the tension between laws of human society and the resistance of the unconscious mind to these laws’, noting that

dialogues between the self and the double are increasingly acknowledged as being colloquies within the self: the double has become an aspect of the personal and interpersonal life, a manifestation of unconscious desire. — M Žurkovič (2000). ‘The Double as The "Unseen" of Culture: Toward a Definition of Doppelganger’ Facta Universitatis Series: Linguistics and Literature 2(#7): 121–128.

The major differences implied by the paired OSes are:

  • The Qarin's right temporal lobe (?) does not develop a strong sense of personality.
  • The mode of influence is not necessarily vocal for the Qarin, and is not specified.
  • The intent of its guidance is not social or beneficial, but the satisfaction of primal desires, as Freud said of the id.

Think of the Qarin as the parent of today’s Fundamentalist OS: unreflective, very sure of itself, conservative, and more open to the aspirations of the darker aspects of human nature. It may be that empathy, compassion, altruism, and socialization depend upon the ability of the ‘other’ hemisphere to differentiate and distance itself from, and moderate, the fiercely self-centered Me. Note too that it is just these Axial/Enlightenment qualities that are deprecated by Libertarians: R Iyer &4a (2012) ‘ Understanding Libertarian Morality: The Psychological Dispositions of Self-Identified Libertarians’ PLoS ONE 7(#8): e42366. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0042366

If any civilization is to survive, it is the morality of altruism that men have to reject. — Ayn Rand.

Bicameral instructions are audible, apparently arising from the right-hemisphere. Our principal speech centers are Broca's (frontal lobe) and Wernicke's (temporal) areas in the left hemisphere: Jaynes suggested that their counterparts on the right — with unknown functions today — were used by the bicameral executive, to formulate instructions heard as peremptory command hallucinations: A Beck-Sander, M Birchwood, P Chadwick (1997) ‘Acting on Command Hallucinations: A Cognitive Approach’ Brit J Clin Psychol 36(#1): 139–148. ‘Do thus-and-so’. Needed Research
   ▲

The ‘single most comprehensive, wide-ranging, imaginative hypothesis available’ has attracted surprisingly little attention considering its importance. If Jaynes is right — and all the evidence suggests that he is — the bicameral hypothesis lays bare the arbitrary nature of revealed religion and explains why prophets were so sure that they were transmitting the word of god. It reveals the true nature of gods as autonomous mental agents of the evolving operating system. Think of them as alternate personalities, inflexibly laying down the rules. Our earliest religious texts are not derived from supernatural authority, but belong wholly to unconscious sociology.

We can dismiss charges of manipulation and charlatanry against the early prophets, because bicamerality seems to have suppressed the incipient political chicanery of chimpanzees in favor of smoothly working communities. The revival of deception must wait for a conscious mind. The rules of the prophets belong to evolutionary psychology and the ethology of the unconscious, chosen by the blind forces of natural selection. The ‘singer to his clan, in the dim red dawn of man’ unconsciously shaped the sagas that shaped his tribe, so that groups and stories co-evolved, and the ones that worked best became the foundational epics of early city states. It is unfortunate that the milieu was Late Neolithic, when population density crowded out foragers and warfare became a vital component of survival, for this left our early religions tainted by the cult of the warrior.

Jaynes suspected that language had not evolved sufficiently to support a bicameral voice until 10 ka, and I am pushing this moment back another 25,000 years. There is little evidence for this — but note that the Antikythera mechanism pushed our development of analog computers back a totally unexpected 2100 years with a single lucky find. The apparently unchanging nature of relict tribes from the 60-ka African exodus, and their ability to cross 90 km of water, lend some support to the idea of effective verbal communication by 50 ka.

We cannot assign a single date for the birth of consciousness, but psychohistorians might be able to draw temporal maps of the decline of bicamerality because its passing produces deep changes in cultural behavior. Any new way of looking at history will produce new insights, and thinking in terms of the dawning of consciousness being a local affair might explain many anomalies.

We also need research into the ‘operating systems’ of our prophets, because not all of them were bicameral. Table 2.5 is a preliminary stab at sorting them, far from definitive because the question — which is one for experts — has hardly been raised, let alone studied.
